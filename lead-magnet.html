Step 2: Think It Through Together

Here's the mistake most people make: they tell AI "make me a thing" and hope for the best.

Don't do that. You're not placing an order—you're thinking out loud with a partner who can help you figure out what you actually want.

This is where the real magic happens. AI will ask you questions. But more importantly, its questions will spark questions you didn't know you had. You'll discover things about your own project that weren't clear until you started talking through it.

Start with something like this:

Opening prompt
"I want to build [describe your project in a sentence or two].
Here's what I know so far:
Why I want this: [what problem does it solve? why now?]
Who it's for: [just you? your team? someone else?]
What done looks like: [how will you know it's working?]
Key features: [what does it need to do?]
Constraints: [time, tools, keep it simple, etc.]
I'm still figuring some of this out. Can you ask me questions to help clarify what I'm actually trying to build?"

Then: have a real conversation

When AI asks you something, don't just answer—notice what comes up for you. If its question makes you realize you're not sure about something, say that. If it sparks a new idea, explore it. Ask AI questions back.

This back-and-forth is how you go from "vague idea" to "clear spec." You're essentially building a brief together—one that captures what you actually want, not just what you thought you wanted when you started.

Here's What That Looks Like

Let's say you picked the weekly review tool. Here's how a real conversation might unfold:

Example conversation in Claude
You
I want to build a weekly review tool. I keep saying I'll do reviews but never know what questions to ask. Can you help me figure out what this should look like?
C
I'd love to help. A few questions: Do you want this to be quick (5-10 min) or thorough (20-30 min)? And are you more interested in reflecting on what happened, or planning what's next?
You
Quick is better—if it takes too long I won't do it. And honestly maybe both? I want to capture what I learned but also feel clear on what matters next week.
C
That tension is useful! What if we did a quick "look back" (3-4 questions) and a "look ahead" (2-3 questions)? One more thing: do you want to write answers, or just think through prompts mentally?
You
Oh, I hadn't thought about that. Writing would help me process it. But short answers, not essays.
(conversation continues...)

Notice what's happening: the questions aren't just gathering requirements—they're helping you discover what you actually want. Keep going until you're clear on:

  • What problem this solves (and why it matters to you)
  • Who it's for
  • What "done" looks like
  • Key features it needs to have
  • Any constraints (time, complexity, tools)

This is the real skill: using AI as a thinking partner, not just a task executor.

Step 3: Build It

Once you've had the conversation and you're both clear on what you're making, it's time to make it real. Say something like:

When you're ready "Okay, I think we're aligned. Let's build this."

Claude will create an "artifact"—a working version of your project that appears in a panel on the right side of the screen. You can interact with it right there. Click around. Try it out.

Something that didn't exist 20 minutes ago is now real. You made that.

Step 4: Make It Better

Your first version probably isn't perfect. That's fine—this is where the magic happens. Try it out. Notice what works and what doesn't. Then tell AI what to change:

  • "Can you make the questions shorter?"
  • "The tone feels too formal—can you make it more casual?"
  • "This part is confusing—can we simplify it?"
  • "What if it gave me a summary at the end?"

You're not starting over. You're iterating. Do 2-3 rounds of this. Watch how fast it improves.
