Most people treat AI like a search engine. You type something in, you hope something useful comes back, and when it doesn't, you assume you did it wrong. That mental model makes AI frustrating fast.

There's a more useful frame. One that changes how you prompt, how you read responses, and how much you actually get done.

Using AI is a managerial relationship. You manage the inputs, you set the goals, and you evaluate the outputs. Claude handles the execution. That shift in how you think about the relationship changes everything about how you work with it.

## What managing AI actually looks like

Think about how a good manager works with a capable employee. They don't just bark a task and disappear. They explain the context, give the employee enough background to do the job well, check in on what comes back, and give specific direction when something needs to change. They stay in the loop without doing the work themselves.

That's exactly the dynamic with Claude or ChatGPT.

When you open a chat and type "summarize this document," you're not giving Claude enough to work with. You're leaving the "brief" mostly blank. A good manager would tell their employee why the summary matters, who it's for, what length makes sense, and what to emphasize. That same briefing makes AI output dramatically better.

The comparison to management is a practical operating model, not a loose analogy. The same habits that make someone effective at directing people make them effective at directing AI.

## Three responsibilities in the managerial relationship

When you take on the role of AI manager, three things become your job: setting the goal, providing the context, and evaluating the output. Each one matters. Skipping any of them is where most people lose time.

### Setting the goal

Vague goals produce vague outputs. "Help me with this email" and "Write a two-paragraph email to my VP asking for additional budget on our Q2 project, with a conciliatory tone because the original budget discussion was tense" are very different briefs. One gives Claude a category. The other gives it a job.

The goal-setting step is where most people spend the least time, and it's where they'd get the most return. Before you write your first message, spend thirty seconds asking: what does success actually look like here? What does the output need to do? Then write that down and include it.

A clear brief means fewer rounds of correction. A vague brief means you'll eventually get there, but after more back-and-forth than necessary.

### Providing context

Context is everything AI doesn't know but needs to know. Your role, your audience, your constraints, the background that makes this task specific to your situation. Claude doesn't know your company, your manager's communication style, or why this particular deliverable matters. That gap is your entry point. Filling it in is what separates useful output from generic output.

The professionals who get the most out of AI are the ones who've gotten into the habit of briefing it the way they'd brief a new team member. What does this person need to know to do this job well on their first try? Give Claude that.

In MIT Sloan research on [AI and expertise in the workplace](https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/whats-your-edge-rethinking-expertise-in-the-age-of-ai/), one of the consistent findings is that AI can synthesize vast information, but it cannot bear the weight of consequences or apply contextual judgment the way humans can. That's the whole point: your context and judgment are the inputs. Claude handles the rest.

### Evaluating the output

This is the step that most people skip or rush. Claude produces something, it looks "fine," and it goes out the door. But a manager doesn't rubber-stamp everything their employee submits. They read it, notice what works and what doesn't, and give specific feedback.

Evaluating AI output is a skill. You're reading like an editor, not a requester. You're asking: does this accomplish the goal? Is the tone right? Is anything missing? What's close but not quite there? That evaluation is what drives the next round of direction, which gets you to something you can actually use.

A Harvard Business Review article on [developing judgment in the AI era](https://hbr.org/2026/02/how-do-workers-develop-good-judgment-in-the-ai-era) makes the case that evaluation is now a critical professional skill in its own right. As AI handles more of the doing, the ability to distinguish good from good-enough becomes the differentiator. That starts with reading output carefully.

> "Human judgment on output quality is the critical layer; AI speeds up the workflow but humans still evaluate, refine, edit, and decide what to keep." — Insight from MVP Club's Getting Started series

## The shift from individual contributor to manager

Before AI, most professional work was individual contribution. You did the task. You drafted the email, you built the spreadsheet, you wrote the report. Your value was in the doing.

With AI in the picture, the doing gets faster. Claude can draft the email, build the spreadsheet, write the report. Where your value concentrates is in the direction: what to do, for whom, to what standard. You move from individual contributor to manager, even if nobody gave you that title.

For most professionals, this is actually an upgrade in leverage. Managers typically operate at higher leverage than individual contributors. They shape outcomes rather than executing individual tasks. The professional who understands how to direct AI well is operating at that level even when they're a team of one.

Matt's signature phrase captures it: "Using AI puts people in a managerial relationship rather than an individual contributor relationship." That's a complete rethinking of where your energy goes at work, not just a rebranding of what you already do.

## How iteration works in practice

The managerial relationship runs on a loop, not a single exchange.

You set the goal, provide context, and give Claude a task. Claude produces something. You evaluate it. You give specific direction based on what you find. Claude revises. You evaluate again. You're not waiting for perfection from the first response. You're expecting a draft that you can steer toward something finished.

Most professionals who haven't internalized this stop after round one. The output is "not quite right," they assume the tool failed, and they either give up or start the conversation over. Starting over throws away all the context Claude has built up. Iterating from what's there is almost always faster.

The key is being specific with your feedback. "Make it better" gives Claude nothing to work with. "The second paragraph is too long, cut it by half and move the main point to the front" is something Claude can act on directly.

> "Anything you do more than once, write down how to do it, and then tell Claude, hey, here's how I want you to do it. Just go." — Matt Hastings, in an MVP Club session on Claude Skills and automation

That principle applies to the full iteration loop. When you find a type of task where the briefing-and-evaluation cycle works well, document it. Write down the context you always provide, the format you always want, the things that always go wrong in round one. That becomes a starting point you can drop in at the beginning of any similar conversation, and it compresses rounds of iteration from five to two.

## What makes a good manager of AI

Some people pick this up quickly. Others get frustrated and stuck. The difference usually comes down to a few habits.

**Willingness to be specific.** Vague inputs produce vague outputs. The professionals who get consistently good results are the ones who've learned to be precise before they hit send, not after. This takes a little extra time upfront and saves a lot of time on the back end.

**Comfort with evaluation.** Not everyone finds it natural to read their own output critically. But the evaluation step is where you earn the leverage. If you're not willing to read what comes back carefully and name specifically what's wrong, the iteration loop doesn't close.

**Patience with the loop.** The professionals who get frustrated quickly are usually expecting a vending machine: put the request in, get the perfect output out. When round three produces something worth sending, that's not a failure. That's the process working exactly as it should.

**Using your domain expertise.** This is where the managerial relationship pays off most clearly. Claude doesn't know your field the way you do. It doesn't know your stakeholders, your organization's culture, your professional judgment about what's realistic. When you bring that expertise to the evaluation and direction steps, you produce output that AI couldn't generate on its own. The combination is what makes the collaboration valuable.

## A practical starting point for your next task

Here's a simple structure to try the next time you work with Claude or ChatGPT on something that matters:

**Before you start:** Write down the goal in one sentence. Then list the context someone would need to do this job well. Paste both into your first message before anything else.

**When you get output:** Read it before you use it. Ask yourself: does this accomplish what I described? What's off? Be specific about what needs to change, rather than general.

**In your follow-up message:** Quote the problem, explain why it doesn't work, and give direction. "The tone is too formal for a Slack message to my direct report" is better than "make it more casual."

**Keep going:** Two or three rounds of iteration is normal. You're not doing it wrong. You're managing toward a result.
