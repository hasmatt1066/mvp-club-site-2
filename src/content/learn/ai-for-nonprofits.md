Your grant deadline is Friday. You have a program report due Monday. And your executive director just forwarded a major donor request asking for a personalized update by end of week. You have one full-time staff person and two part-timers.

This is the daily reality for most nonprofit professionals. More communication, more documentation, more reporting, less time. And unlike corporate roles, there's usually no budget for consultants, no marketing department to hand things off to, and no one whose job it is to write for you.

That's exactly where tools like Claude and ChatGPT can make a real difference, not by replacing the expertise and mission-knowledge only you have, but by eliminating the blank-page problem on tasks you'd do anyway.

## Where AI actually fits in nonprofit work

Before diving into specific workflows, it helps to get clear on what AI is genuinely useful for in this context versus where your human judgment is irreplaceable.

AI does well with: first drafts, synthesizing information into readable formats, adapting the same message for multiple audiences, pulling structure from your notes, and handling repetitive writing tasks. These are exactly the tasks that eat nonprofit staff time without requiring the deep expertise you've spent years building.

AI does not do well with: knowing your specific community, understanding what your funders actually care about, or replacing the relationship-building that is the core of nonprofit work. A grant written entirely by AI and unreviewed by staff will almost certainly miss the nuance that wins funding.

The useful mental model here is the same one that applies across every professional role: [using AI is a managerial relationship](/learn/how-to-use-ai-at-work). You direct the work, give it context, evaluate the output, and push back when it misses the mark. You bring the mission expertise. Claude or ChatGPT brings the drafting capacity.

## The three highest-impact use cases for nonprofit professionals

After working with professionals across roles and sectors, these three areas consistently produce the most time savings for people in nonprofit and community-focused work.

### 1. Grant writing (the biggest time sink, and the best fit for AI)

Grant writing is a perfect AI use case because it is largely a drafting problem. You know your program. You know your outcomes. You know your community. The hard part is translating that knowledge into the specific format a funder wants, often under deadline pressure and for multiple funders with different requirements.

AI can dramatically accelerate that translation work.

### 2. Donor outreach and stewardship communications

Writing personalized donor communications at scale is something most small nonprofits simply don't have time for. A major donor portfolio of 50 people should theoretically get individualized touchpoints throughout the year. In practice, most organizations send one or two generic newsletter blasts and hope that counts as stewardship.

AI changes that calculation.

### 3. Impact reporting and program documentation

Board reports, annual reports, grant progress reports: these are necessary, important, and completely demoralizing to write when you're staring at a year's worth of program notes, spreadsheets, and anecdotes. AI can synthesize that raw material into readable narrative quickly.

## Detailed walkthrough: Grant writing with Claude

This is where most nonprofit staff get the most immediate value, so let's go through a full workflow.

**What you need before you open Claude:**
- The grant guidelines and application questions (paste the actual text, not a summary of it)
- Your program description (even rough notes are fine)
- Any data you have on outcomes, participants, or impact
- Any previous successful grants for this program (if you have them)

**Step 1: Feed Claude the context**

Start a new Claude conversation and give it everything it needs to understand your organization and program. Something like:

> "I'm the program director at [Organization Name], a nonprofit that [brief description: who you serve, what you do, your geography]. I'm writing a grant application for [funder name]. Here are the grant guidelines: [paste full guidelines]. Here's background on our program: [paste your program description or notes]. Here's our outcome data: [paste whatever you have]. Please read all of this before we start, and let me know what you've understood about our work."

That last sentence matters. Asking Claude to confirm its understanding before drafting helps you catch any misreads before you're deep into a draft.

**Step 2: Answer application questions one at a time**

Resist the urge to ask Claude to "write the entire application." Instead, take each question individually. Ask Claude to draft a response, then read it critically: does it sound like your organization? Does it address what the funder is actually asking? Is the emphasis right?

The first draft is almost never what you'd submit. That's fine. Your job at this stage is to react, correct, and direct. "This paragraph oversells the outcomes data we have. Scale it back and be more specific about what we've actually measured." That conversation is where the value is.

**Step 3: Adapt, don't start over**

Once you have a strong grant response to one funder, you have a foundation. The next funder application is not a blank page. It's an editing project. Ask Claude: "Here's the grant response I wrote for Funder A. Now I need to adapt this for Funder B, whose guidelines are attached. Their focus is on [X] instead of [Y]. Help me revise."

This is where the time savings compound. Your second grant application might take 40% of the time your first one took. Your third, less still.

## Donor outreach: Making stewardship feel personal at scale

Personalized donor communication is the gold standard of nonprofit fundraising. It is also the thing most understaffed development teams sacrifice first when time is short.

Here's a workflow that makes individualized outreach realistic without adding hours to your week.

**The "donor context card" approach**

For each donor you want to steward personally, keep a simple note in a document or spreadsheet: their giving history (years of support, approximate level), any personal connection to your work (why they give, family history with the issue, past conversations they've mentioned), and any recent news about them you'd want to acknowledge (a promotion, a family milestone, anything you'd note if you were keeping up with a friend).

This doesn't have to be elaborate. Even two or three sentences per donor gives Claude enough to work with.

When it's time to write a touchpoint, open Claude and say: "I need to write a brief, personal email to a donor named [Name]. Here's what I know about them: [paste your context card]. The purpose of this email is [thank them for a recent gift / update them on a program they care about / invite them to an event]. I want this to sound warm and genuine, not like a mail merge. Can you draft something I can edit?"

Read what comes back. Edit it until it actually sounds like you. The goal is not to send AI-written email unreviewed. The goal is to spend 10 minutes on a personal outreach instead of 45, and to send more of them.

**Adapting one message for multiple audiences**

Say you have a program update you want to share: a cohort just graduated, or you hit a milestone, or you have a powerful participant story (with permission to share). You need to communicate this to major donors, your email list, and your board. Three different audiences, three different tones, three different levels of detail.

Ask Claude to draft all three versions from a single brief. "I have a program update I need to share with three different audiences. Here's the core story: [paste]. Can you draft: (1) a 2-paragraph personal email to a major donor who has funded this program, (2) a newsletter paragraph for our general email list, (3) a brief board update for our quarterly board report?"

One piece of information. Three outputs. That's where the time savings are real.

## Impact reporting: Turning raw data into readable narrative

The board report you've been putting off probably exists somewhere as a pile of: program notes, attendance spreadsheets, a few compelling participant stories, some outcome numbers that feel hard to contextualize, and a general sense of "we did a lot this year."

Claude is genuinely good at helping you shape that raw material into coherent narrative.

**Paste in the raw material, not a summary**

The key mistake people make when using AI for reports is summarizing first, then asking for help. That loses information. Instead, paste your actual notes, your actual data, your actual stories (de-identified if needed for privacy). Let Claude work with the real material.

"Here are my program notes from this quarter: [paste]. Here's our attendance data: [paste]. Here's a participant story I'm allowed to share: [paste]. I need to write a 2-page program update for our board. It should cover: what we did, who we served, what we're seeing in terms of outcomes, and one story that illustrates the impact. Please draft this in a clear, professional tone."

You'll still need to edit. Claude doesn't know your board, doesn't know what the ED has already told them, and doesn't know which metrics they care most about. But getting from raw notes to a readable draft in 20 minutes instead of two hours is a real change.

**Grant progress reports**

These are the least beloved documents in the nonprofit world: required, formulaic, and usually due when you're already slammed. Most funders have a specific format. Feed Claude the format, feed it your program data, and ask for a first draft. Then review it carefully: funders notice if the report doesn't match what you proposed, and they notice if the outcomes you're claiming don't match what your contract said you'd deliver.

Claude can draft. You verify and vouch.

## Tool recommendations

For most of these workflows, **Claude** (claude.ai) is the better choice. Claude handles long documents well, which matters when you're pasting in grant guidelines, program notes, and outcome data all at once. It also tends to write in a more measured, professional tone by default, which suits grant writing and formal donor communications.

**ChatGPT** is a solid alternative and either works for the core workflows above. The paid tier ($20/month for Claude Pro or ChatGPT Plus) gives you significantly longer conversations and better reasoning, which matters when you're working with large amounts of program context. If you're doing this work regularly, the paid tier is worth it.

One important note: before pasting any sensitive information into an AI tool, check your organization's data policies. Most nonprofits do not have formal AI policies yet, but you should avoid pasting personally identifiable information about participants, donor financial details, or confidential grant communications. De-identify examples when you can, and treat these tools the way you'd treat any cloud document: useful and generally fine, but not a place for truly sensitive data.

## Your first week: a five-day practice plan

You don't need a major project to start. Here's a low-stakes entry point for your first week.

**Monday:** Find one piece of writing you've been putting off. A grant question you're dreading, a donor email you've been meaning to send, a program update nobody's asked for yet but you know you'll need. Open Claude. Give it context. Ask for a draft. Read it and revise.

**Tuesday:** Take that revised draft and improve the prompt you used. What would you tell Claude differently now that you've seen what it does with basic context? Try again with a better setup.

**Wednesday:** Adapt the same content for a different audience. If Monday was a grant narrative, Wednesday is the donor newsletter paragraph from the same material. Same program story, different format and tone.

**Thursday:** Try a reporting task. Take your messiest pile of program notes and ask Claude to help you organize them into a readable summary. You're not trying to produce a finished document. You're practicing giving AI raw material and seeing what it does with it.

**Friday:** Reflect for ten minutes. Where did Claude actually save you time? Where did it produce something that needed heavy editing? Where did it miss the mark entirely? This reflection is part of the practice.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is it okay for grant applications to be AI-assisted?**

Most funders don't have explicit policies on this yet, though that's changing. The more important question is whether the application accurately represents your organization and program. AI-assisted writing that is carefully reviewed, fact-checked, and edited by the person who actually runs the program is no different in substance from hiring a grant writer or working with a consultant. The expertise, the outcomes, and the accountability are still yours. Unreviewed AI output submitted without checking for accuracy is a different matter. Review what you submit.

**What if I give Claude our program information and it ends up training on it?**

Anthropic's standard terms for Claude Pro do not use your conversations to train models by default. You can verify Anthropic's current privacy policy at anthropic.com. If your organization has strict data policies, ask your leadership or legal counsel before using any cloud-based tool with organizational information.

**How do I get my team to try this?**

Start with yourself and a real task, not a demo or an experiment. When you show a colleague a grant draft that took you 45 minutes instead of half a day, that's more persuasive than any workshop. Offer to sit with one person and work through a specific document together. That's how this tends to spread.

**What if the AI output doesn't sound like us?**

It won't at first. That's expected, and it's not a failure. Your job is to edit it until it does sound like you. Over time, you'll get better at giving Claude context about your organization's voice, your funder relationships, and your community, and the drafts will need less editing. The practice builds on itself.
