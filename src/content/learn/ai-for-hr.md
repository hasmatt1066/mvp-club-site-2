An HR director in one of our community sessions pulled up Claude on her laptop and, in about four minutes, drafted a job description that would have taken her 90 minutes to write from scratch. She wasn't using a template. She was having a conversation: here's the role, here's the team, here's what good looks like, what questions should I be asking candidates?

That moment is available to you right now. And it's not about replacing your judgment. It's about giving your judgment better material to work with.

This guide covers the three areas where AI for HR professionals delivers the fastest results: recruiting, onboarding, and policy development. For each one, you'll get a concrete workflow and the specific prompts that actually work.

## Why HR Work Is Particularly Well-Suited for AI

HR involves a lot of writing. Job descriptions, offer letters, onboarding documents, policy drafts, performance review templates, communication plans. Most of these follow patterns you've run through dozens of times. You know what good looks like. You can evaluate output quickly.

That's exactly the profile of work where Claude and ChatGPT save the most time. You're not outsourcing the hard thinking. You're offloading the blank-page problem so you can spend your time refining, personalizing, and applying your expertise where it actually matters.

The other factor: HR work requires a lot of context-switching. You're rarely deep in one project for hours at a time. AI gives you a way to move faster across that constant switching without losing quality.

## Three High-Impact Use Cases for AI in HR

Here's where to start, ordered by speed of payoff.

**Recruiting:** Job descriptions, screening questions, interview guides, candidate outreach
**Onboarding:** Welcome packets, role-specific orientation materials, first-30-60-90-day plans
**Policy development:** First drafts, gap analysis, plain-language summaries of complex policies

Start with recruiting. It gives you results you can see in the same sitting.

## Use Case 1: Recruiting (Your Fastest Win)

### Writing Job Descriptions in Minutes, Not Hours

Most job descriptions follow the same arc: role summary, key responsibilities, what you're looking for, why someone should want to work here. You've written this structure many times. AI can draft the skeleton in minutes; your job is to make it real.

Here's the prompt pattern that works:

> "I'm writing a job description for a [job title] on our [team name] team. This person will [primary responsibility]. They'll work closely with [stakeholders]. Our company [brief company description]. What I care most about in this role is [top 2-3 must-haves]. Write a draft job description that leads with what makes this role interesting, not just what the person needs to do. Keep it under 400 words."

The key moves here: give it the team context, name what you actually care about, and ask it to lead with what makes the role compelling. Generic job descriptions read like they were written by committee. Specific ones attract better candidates.

After you get the draft, your editing session with Claude or ChatGPT is where the value compounds. Tell it what's off: "The tone is too corporate. We're a fast-moving team. Make it sound like we're looking for a partner, not an employee." Or: "The requirements list is too long. Cut anything that's a nice-to-have." Each round gets faster as the tool learns what you're going for.

### Building Interview Guides

Once you have a job description, your next move is to ask Claude to generate interview questions from it.

> "Based on this job description [paste it], write 8 interview questions that would help me evaluate whether someone can actually do this work, not just talk about it. For each question, include what a strong answer would include and what a weak answer would look like."

This is one of the more immediately useful things AI can do for recruiting. Structured interview guides with evaluation criteria reduce bias and make your hiring decisions more defensible. They also help managers who are less experienced with interviews. And they're time-consuming to write from scratch every time.

### Candidate Outreach That Doesn't Sound Like a Template

Outreach messages for passive candidates are notoriously hard to write. Too generic and they get ignored. Too specific and they sound like you've been stalking someone's LinkedIn profile.

Try this:

> "Write a short outreach message (under 100 words) for a [role] candidate. She [brief description of relevant background]. We're reaching out because [specific reason]. The message should feel personal and specific to her background, not like a mass send. Don't open with 'I hope this message finds you well.' Don't mention 'exciting opportunity.'"

The negative constraints matter. Telling Claude what not to include often produces more useful output than describing what to include.

Onboarding is one of those areas where HR teams know the materials could be better and rarely have time to fix them. AI changes that math.

Claude drafted a welcome letter, a role summary, a first-week schedule, and a quick-reference guide. She edited and personalized each piece. The whole process took about three hours instead of three days.

### The Prompt Pattern for Onboarding Documents

The key to getting useful onboarding materials from Claude or ChatGPT is specificity about the audience. "Write an onboarding guide" produces generic output. "Write a 30-day guide for a new mid-level HR business partner joining a 300-person company in the middle of a system migration" produces something much more useful.

Try this approach:

> "Write a first-week schedule and overview document for a new [job title] joining our team. Context: [2-3 sentences about your organization]. This person is coming from [background]. In their first week, they need to [key goals]. Format as a day-by-day guide with the 'why' behind each item, not just a task list."

Then ask follow-up questions:

> "What questions does a new employee in this role typically have that aren't addressed in a standard onboarding document? Write a FAQ section that anticipates those questions."

That FAQ section alone often becomes one of the most-used documents in the onboarding package.

### 30-60-90 Day Plans

These take a long time to write and are often either too vague to be useful or so detailed they become irrelevant two weeks in.

> "Write a 30-60-90 day plan for a new [role]. Day 1-30 should focus on learning and relationship-building. Day 31-60 should be about early contribution. Day 61-90 should be about independent ownership of [key responsibilities]. Each phase should have 3-5 concrete goals, not vague outcomes like 'understand the business.'"

The editing process is where you make it real. Take what Claude gives you and replace the generic goals with the specific outcomes that matter to your team. Ten minutes of editing makes the difference between a document that looks good and one that actually guides behavior.

## Use Case 3: Policy Development Without the Two-Month Slog

Writing new policies or updating old ones is one of the most time-consuming things HR does. It involves drafting, legal review, stakeholder input, plain-language revision, and distribution. AI can significantly compress the first and third steps.

### First Drafts

Before AI, the first draft of a policy usually took days: research comparable policies, identify the key components, write something, send it to legal, get it back red-lined, revise. Claude can generate that first draft in minutes.

> "Draft an AI use policy for a company of about [size] employees in [industry]. The policy should cover: what tools employees are allowed to use, what data they should never input into AI systems, what review is required before sharing AI-generated work externally, and what the consequences are for misuse. Write in plain English. Avoid legal jargon. This is the starting point for review, not the final document."

The output gives you something concrete to edit and send to legal, rather than starting from scratch. Your legal team's time is better spent reviewing and refining than writing from zero.

### Gap Analysis

If you already have policies, Claude can help you identify what's missing.

> "Here are our current HR policies on [topic]. What important scenarios are these policies not addressing? What common questions would employees have that aren't answered here? What have other organizations included in similar policies that we should consider?"

This is using Claude as a thinking partner, not a document generator. It's one of the more valuable ways AI helps with policy work.

### Plain-Language Summaries

The policy goes through legal. It comes back in legalese. Nobody reads it.

> "Here is our [policy name]. Write a plain-language summary that employees will actually read and understand. Use short sentences. Use examples where helpful. The goal is comprehension, not legal precision. Keep it under 300 words."

This has become standard practice for HR teams that work with Claude regularly. The full policy lives in the policy library. The plain-language version goes in the employee handbook and onboarding documents.

## Tool Recommendations

For HR work, Claude is particularly strong at longer, more structured documents: policy drafts, onboarding guides, interview frameworks. The context window is larger, so you can paste more material into a single conversation.

ChatGPT is comparable for shorter, more transactional tasks like outreach messages or screening questions.

Start with whichever one you have access to. The learning is in the doing, not in finding the right tool first.

One practical note: never paste personally identifiable employee information into AI tools. When you need AI's help with something that involves specific people, describe the situation in general terms. "I'm working with an employee who has been late to meetings consistently" rather than naming the person or including HR file details.

## Your First Week: A 5-Day Practice Plan for AI in HR

The fastest way to get comfortable is to bring real work to Claude or ChatGPT every day for a week.

**Monday:** Take a job description you've written before and ask Claude to rewrite it from scratch using the same role details. Compare the two. What did it miss? What did it catch that you hadn't emphasized?

**Tuesday:** Build an interview guide for a role you're currently hiring for. Start with the job description, ask Claude for 8-10 questions with evaluation criteria, then edit it down to the ones you'd actually use.

**Wednesday:** Pick a policy you think could be clearer. Ask Claude to write a plain-language summary, then share it with one colleague who isn't in HR and see if they understand it.

**Thursday:** Draft a first-30-day onboarding plan for a role you frequently hire. Focus on making the goals specific enough to be actionable.

**Friday:** Reflect on what worked and what felt awkward. The things that felt awkward are almost always cases where you gave the tool too little context. That's the pattern to watch for.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use AI for things like performance reviews or disciplinary documentation?**

Yes, but with care. Claude and ChatGPT can help you draft frameworks, templates, and plain-language summaries. They can help you think through what to include and what language to avoid. They should not be generating the actual performance assessments or discipline letters without significant human review. The judgment about a specific employee's performance belongs to the manager. AI can support the process, not replace it.

**What about bias in recruiting?**

AI can actually help here, not just hurt. Asking Claude to review a job description for language that might discourage qualified candidates is a useful step. Asking it to generate diverse interview questions helps. But AI can also reproduce bias if you give it biased inputs. Check your job descriptions for unnecessarily gendered language. Ask Claude: "Does this job description include any language that might discourage women or underrepresented candidates from applying?" It's a useful gut-check.

**What's the biggest mistake HR teams make when starting with AI?**

Giving it too little context and then concluding it "doesn't work." Every AI tool needs to know who you are, what the situation is, and what good output looks like. The more you tell it about your organization, your team, and your constraints, the more useful the output. Start with one specific task, give it full context, and build from there. That's covered in more depth in [how to use AI at work](/learn/how-to-use-ai-at-work).

**Do I need technical knowledge to use these tools?**

No. Claude and ChatGPT are conversational. You type your request in plain English. If you can describe your work to a smart colleague, you can get useful output from these tools. The skills that matter are the same ones you already use: clear communication, domain knowledge, and the judgment to evaluate what comes back.
