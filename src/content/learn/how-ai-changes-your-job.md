Most of the "AI will change your job" conversations I see are happening between two camps that aren't talking to you. One camp says AI is going to eliminate everything. The other says it's all hype and your job is fine. Neither of those is particularly useful when you're sitting in a meeting trying to figure out what this actually means for you, specifically, right now.

So let me give you a more useful frame.

## The Shift Is Already Happening, Whether You're Participating or Not

McKinsey's 2025 "Superagency in the Workplace" report found that employees are three times more likely than their leaders expect to already be using generative AI for at least 30% of their daily work. [NEEDS CITATION] That's not a projection. That's what's happening right now in your organization.

Some of your colleagues are using Claude or ChatGPT to draft emails, summarize documents, prep for meetings, and think through problems. They're not announcing it. They're just working faster. And while that gap stays invisible for a while, it eventually becomes visible in output, promotion conversations, and who gets asked to lead things.

The World Economic Forum's 2025 Future of Jobs Report is clear on one thing that often gets lost in the headlines: 170 million new roles are expected to be created alongside 92 million displaced ones. [NEEDS CITATION] The jobs that survive (and the new ones that get created) will look different because AI handles some of what used to be purely human work, freeing up capacity for what AI still can't do. The labor market is in transformation, not freefall.

What that means for your career depends entirely on how you position yourself in that shift.

## What's Actually Changing in Your Day-to-Day Work

Your judgment is still the center of the work. What's shifting is where in the process you apply it.

Think about the parts of your job that involve assembling information: writing up a meeting summary, drafting a first version of a proposal, researching a vendor, building a presentation from scratch, compiling a weekly status report. These are the kinds of tasks where AI has already shown it can produce a workable first pass in minutes rather than hours.

That doesn't mean you're done. It means you spend less time on production and more time on evaluation. Deciding whether the draft is right. Knowing what's missing. Applying your specific knowledge of the client, the team, the history, the nuance. That judgment is still yours. But you're applying it later in the process and more deliberately.

> "All these companies are hyper competing right now... they all want your attention and they want people hyping them up. But what do I want these tools to do for me?" (Matt Hastings, in a recent MVP Club Getting Started session)

That question (what do I actually want this to do for me?) is the one worth sitting with. The professionals who get the most out of AI are not the ones who read the most articles about it. They're the ones who started using it to solve a specific problem they actually have.

## The Managerial Relationship

One of the most useful frames for understanding how your job is changing comes from thinking about AI as a new kind of working relationship. You're not executing tasks manually anymore for the tasks AI can handle. You're managing: setting the goal, providing context, and evaluating the output.

That's a fundamentally different posture than typing something up yourself. And it turns out to be a skill in itself.

[NEEDS VERIFICATION: confirm community member] Carmella Thompson, a real estate agent in the MVP Club community, built a daily habit around this. Every morning she pastes that day's property data into Claude and gets a market report draft. It took her three months of occasional use before she committed to a repeatable workflow. Once she did, her prep time went from 45 minutes to under 10. She didn't automate her job. She changed where her time went.

The shift Carmella made is worth understanding. She's not a passive user who enters a prompt and accepts whatever comes back. She's the evaluator. She knows when the draft is right, when it's generic, when it misses something only she would catch. That human judgment layer is the job now.

## What's Not Changing

Your domain expertise is still your primary asset. Possibly more valuable than before.

AI generates confident-sounding output. It doesn't know your organization's political dynamics, your client's actual concerns, your team's history with a particular approach, or what "good" looks like in your specific context. You do. That knowledge is what turns a generic AI output into something that actually works.

The professionals I've coached who struggle with AI are often the ones who see it as a magic box that should produce finished work. The ones who thrive are the ones who see it more like a very fast, very diligent, sometimes wrong colleague who needs clear direction and careful review. Your expertise tells you when the output is trustworthy and when it needs to be pushed further.

## Three Things AI Is Changing Right Now, By Role

**The writing layer.** Regardless of your role, if you produce written communication, reports, or documents, AI is changing how the first draft gets made. The question is whether you're using that to your advantage or still producing everything from scratch.

**The research layer.** Gathering background information, competitive analysis, industry context, and precedent used to eat real time. Claude, ChatGPT, and Perplexity can compress that work substantially. What remains is your interpretation of what that information means for the specific decision you're making.

**The preparation layer.** Prepping for meetings, reviews, negotiations, or presentations involves assembling context. That's something AI does well. Which means the professionals who show up to those conversations more prepared, more quickly, are the ones who've figured out the assembly step.

None of these changes make your job easier in a relaxing sense. They change what hard means. The creative, relational, strategic work doesn't get delegated to AI. The assembly work does.

## How to Find Out What AI Means for Your Specific Role

The only real way to understand what this means for your job is to pick up the tool and use it on your actual work.

That sounds obvious. It's also the step most people skip. They read about AI, watch demos, feel informed, and go back to working the way they always have. Then six months later they're trying to catch up to colleagues who spent those six months building a daily habit.

The experiment doesn't have to be elaborate. Take one task you did last week that involved assembling information or drafting something. Do it again with Claude or ChatGPT. Compare the result. Notice what the AI got right, what it missed, and what you had to add. That comparison tells you more about what AI means for your job than any report or article.

## The Career Positioning Piece

AI capability is already showing up as a differentiator in performance reviews and promotion conversations, not in formal job descriptions (yet), but in who gets recognized for output quality, speed, and range.

The professionals who are getting noticed are the ones who can produce more, cover more ground, and bring more prepared thinking to conversations than they could have two years ago. Some of that is because they're working harder. Most of it is because they've integrated AI into how they work.

You don't need to be an AI expert. You need to be someone who has a real practice. The distinction matters. An expert knows the technology. A practitioner knows how to apply it to their actual work. The practitioner is what most organizations actually need right now and what most people in your immediate peer group are not yet.

> "There's something really interesting about telling a story that when somebody sees it, it's just compelling on its own. It's like, oh, I need this person. I don't know their title or what specifically they're going to do, but I need them to do this for me." (Matt Hastings, in an MVP Club Demo/Share session)

That's what building a real AI practice does. It changes the story you're able to tell, about your work and your range.
