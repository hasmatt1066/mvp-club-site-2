Most people either leave AI off their resume entirely or list "ChatGPT" under skills and hope for the best. Both approaches are leaving something on the table.

If you've been using AI tools regularly at work (even informally, even imperfectly), you have more to say than you think. The challenge isn't whether to include it. The challenge is figuring out how to describe it in a way that's honest, specific, and actually lands with the person reading your resume.

This isn't a guide to gaming the system. It's a guide to getting credit for the work you're already doing.

## Why "Familiar with AI Tools" Doesn't Cut It Anymore

The bar has moved. According to a 2026 [hiring insights report from ResumeGenius](https://resumegenius.com/blog/job-hunting/hiring-insights-report), more than 60% of hiring managers say they want to test, discuss, or see evidence of AI capabilities rather than take resume claims at face value. [NEEDS CITATION: verify this is independent research, not marketing] Simply listing vague AI claims doesn't tell a reader anything useful.

What hiring managers actually want to know: Can you apply AI to the work? Can you describe what you've done with it? Are you someone who uses AI as part of how you work, or someone who took a webinar about it once?

The good news is that if you've been using Claude, ChatGPT, or any other tool to draft documents, research, prep for meetings, or speed up your workflows, you already have specifics to work with.

## The Framework: What, How, and So What

Before you touch your resume, do this exercise. For each AI use in your work, fill in three things:

**What** tool did you use? (Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini, Perplexity)

**How** did you use it? (Drafting first passes on reports, generating meeting summaries, building outreach sequences, analyzing feedback data, creating first-draft training content)

**So what?** What was the outcome? (Shortened a process from three days to one, cut document review time in half, expanded output volume without adding headcount)

This framework keeps you grounded. You're not claiming to be an AI expert. You're describing what you actually did and what happened because of it. That combination (specific tool, specific task, specific outcome) is what reads as credible.

## Where to Put AI on Your Resume

### In Your Work Experience Bullets

This is the highest-value placement. Instead of a skills section checkbox, weave AI into the work itself. The goal is to show AI as part of your workflow, not as a credential you're collecting.

Compare these two:

**Before:** "Managed content production for the L&D team"

**After:** "Managed content production for the L&D team; used Claude to generate first-draft facilitator guides and scenario variations, reducing average content development time by 40%"

The second version doesn't overclaim. You're not saying you automated the job. You're saying you changed how you did the job, and you can say specifically how.

If you don't have a percentage or concrete metric, a qualitative description still works. "Piloted AI-assisted research workflows across a three-person team" is more specific than "familiar with AI."

### In a Skills Section

A skills section is a reasonable place to list the tools you use regularly, but do it with at least one qualifier. Instead of just listing "Claude, ChatGPT," try something like:

*AI Tools: Claude (daily use for writing, research, and document drafting), ChatGPT (prompt iteration and content ideation), Perplexity (research synthesis)*

This tells the reader more than a bare tool list. It shows you actually use these tools in context.

### In Your Summary or Profile Statement

If AI capability is genuinely central to how you work (and increasingly it is), it's fair to mention it briefly in your summary. Something like:

*"...brings a working knowledge of Claude and ChatGPT to [role function], integrating AI-assisted workflows into [type of work]."*

Keep it short. The summary should pull them to the experience section, not try to contain everything.

## What About LinkedIn?

LinkedIn rewards specificity in the same way resumes do, but you have more room to tell a story. A few places to focus:

**Your About section.** If you're actively integrating AI into your work, mention it in plain language. "I've spent the past year building AI into how I [core function], using Claude and ChatGPT to [specific application]." One or two sentences is enough. This gets picked up in recruiter searches and signals that you're current.

**The Skills section.** LinkedIn lets you add skills that connections can endorse. Adding specific tools (Claude, ChatGPT, generative AI) here puts you in relevant search results. It's low-effort and worth doing.

**Posts and shares.** This is where LinkedIn differentiates from a resume. If you share what you're actually building or figuring out with AI, even informally, you create evidence. A short post about a workflow you tried, a tool comparison, or a problem you solved with Claude's help is worth more than a skill endorsement from someone who's never seen you work.

One of our community members, T Gaines, built a GitHub portfolio in a two-hour session on Getting Unstuck Friday, after realizing that employers were asking to see portfolios, not just resumes. She described it this way: "They're asking for a portfolio, not just my repos. Here's what I'm sharing with people: a profile README, a portfolio repo, and then my resume links to it."

The portfolio is the evidence that the resume points to. That's the move.

## The Honest Part: What Not to Claim

There's a version of AI self-promotion that backfires. Here are the claims that tend to raise flags:

**Claiming expertise in things you haven't done.** "Expert in AI implementation" or "AI strategist" suggests you've led AI initiatives at organizational scale. If that's not accurate, a single pointed question in an interview will make it obvious. Stick to what you've actually done.

**Being vague to sound bigger.** "Used AI to improve productivity" tells a reader nothing. It sounds like AI-generated resume copy (which it might be). Specifics are always more credible than abstractions.

**Omitting the human judgment layer.** The most sophisticated thing you can say about AI work is that you know where it falls short. "I use Claude for first drafts and spend roughly the same amount of time editing as I used to spend writing" is a more compelling sentence than "AI reduces my writing time by 80%." Hiring managers who know anything about these tools will trust you more for the nuance.

## A Note on Non-Technical Backgrounds

If you came to AI without a technical background (and most mid-career professionals did), that's not a liability. It's actually a strength worth naming, because the majority of AI work in most organizations isn't engineering. It's judgment, communication, and workflow design.

Coming from a non-technical background actually helps you coach and work with others on AI adoption because you remember what the learning curve felt like. "I figured this out without a technical background and have since helped colleagues do the same" is a real thing to say on a resume or in an interview. It positions you as someone who can bridge.

## A Simple Starting Prompt

If you're staring at your resume and don't know where to start, try this in Claude or ChatGPT:

*"Here is my current resume. I've been using [tools] regularly in my work to [describe how]. Help me rewrite 3-4 bullet points in my most recent role to accurately reflect this AI integration. Don't overstate or invent anything. Work only with the tasks I describe. Keep the tone professional but specific."*

Then paste your resume and your actual AI use cases. What comes back will need editing, but it gives you something real to react to rather than a blank page.

> **Matt Hastings** on why the specifics matter: "There's something really interesting about telling a story that when somebody sees it, it's just compelling on its own. It's like, oh, I need this person. I don't know their title or what specifically they're going to do, but I need them to do this for me."

That's the goal. Not a skills section that checks a box. A picture of how you work.
