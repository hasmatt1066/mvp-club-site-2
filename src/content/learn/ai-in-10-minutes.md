You have 10 minutes, a vague sense that you should be doing something with AI, and no idea where to start. That is a completely normal place to be. Here is the shortest version of what actually matters.

This is not a full guide. You will find those elsewhere (including [a detailed day-by-day practice plan for your first week with AI](/learn/how-to-use-ai-at-work)). This is just the orientation: what AI actually does, what to ignore for now, and where to put your first 10 minutes.

## What AI actually is (the only explanation you need right now)

Claude and ChatGPT are text tools. You type something, they respond. What makes them different from search is that they can read, write, summarize, draft, think through, and respond in natural language, including your language, about your specific situation.

You don't need to know how they work. You need to know what to ask them to do.

Think of it like this: you have a new colleague who is extremely well-read, has no ego, never gets tired, and will attempt any task you give them on the first try. They need direction, though. They do not know your job, your boss, your company's tone, or what you are trying to accomplish. Your job is to give them that context. Their job is to do the work.

That mental model, of managing a capable but context-free collaborator, will serve you better than any prompt hack or tutorial you can find.

## What you can skip (for now)

There is a lot of AI noise. Here is what you do not need to worry about this week:

**Prompt engineering.** You do not need to learn any formal prompting frameworks. Write to Claude or ChatGPT the same way you would write to a smart colleague in a message. Be specific, give context, say what you want. That is it.

**Which model is best.** Claude and ChatGPT are both excellent for professional work. Pick one and start. You can compare them later. [MATT REVIEW: should we name Claude as the slight preference here based on coaching experience?]

**Automation and agents.** You will eventually get to AI workflows that run on their own or handle repetitive tasks end-to-end. Not this week. This week, you are just having conversations.

**The existential stuff.** Whether AI will change your career in five years is a real question worth thinking about, but it has no bearing on whether you should open Claude today.

## The only three things to try in your first 10 minutes

### 1. Paste something real into Claude

Open Claude (claude.ai) or ChatGPT (chatgpt.com). Do not try to think of something interesting to ask. Instead, paste in something from your actual work today. A meeting agenda. A document you need to read. An email you are not sure how to respond to.

Then ask a specific question about it: "What are the two or three things I need to act on here?" or "What is the main point of this?" or "Draft a response that agrees with the core ask but pushes back on the timeline."

The first time you paste real work context into one of these tools and get a useful response, something clicks. The tool becomes real instead of abstract. This is the step most people skip, because they try to come up with a test prompt instead of just using their actual work.

### 2. Ask it to write something you would normally write yourself

Pick one thing on your to-do list that involves writing: an email, a summary, a bullet-point update, a short document. Give Claude the context (who you are writing for, what the situation is, what you want to accomplish, what tone fits) and ask it to write a first draft.

Read the draft. It will probably not be exactly right. Edit it. The key insight is this: editing is much faster than writing from scratch. Most people find they can get to a finished draft in half the time by starting from something imperfect rather than starting from nothing.

> "Anything you do more than once, write down how to do it, and then tell Claude, hey, here's how I want you to do it. Just go." — Matt Hastings, from a recent MVP Club community session on building repeatable workflows with Claude

### 3. Ask it to help you think through something

This is the one that surprises people. AI tools are genuinely useful as thinking partners, not just drafting machines.

If you are stuck on a decision, a conversation you are dreading, or a project that is not moving, describe the situation to Claude and ask it to help you think through the angles you might be missing. Not "make the decision for me." More like: "Here is what I am weighing, here are my constraints, here is what I am worried about. What am I not seeing?"

You will not agree with everything it surfaces. That is fine. The value is in the friction, the moment where you read something and think "no, that is wrong, because..." because that is you clarifying your own thinking.

## What happens after 10 minutes

Those three exercises take about 10 minutes combined. Here is what you are likely to notice:

One of them worked better than the others for your specific situation. That is your starting point. The next day, do that one thing again. Build the habit before you expand the use cases.

The thing that will not happen: you will not feel ready. You will not feel like you have figured it out. The learning with these tools does not come from reading about them or watching videos. It comes from using them with your actual work every day, seeing what works, and getting a little better each time.

## What you can safely ignore for months

Just so you have permission: you do not need to understand large language models. You do not need to take a course. You do not need a system for organizing your prompts. You do not need to read every AI newsletter.

The gap between people who are getting real value from these tools and people who are not is almost never a knowledge gap. It is a practice gap. People who use Claude or ChatGPT with their real work every day get better fast. People who read about using it do not.

This is what we mean when we say [AI is a do-first, understand-later tool](/blog/what-to-actually-do-about-something-big-is-happening). The understanding arrives through doing, not through preparation.

## One last thing: the $20 question

The free versions of Claude and ChatGPT are good enough to start. If you find yourself using one of them daily and hitting limits, the paid tier ($20/month for Claude Pro or ChatGPT Plus) is meaningfully better: faster, higher quality responses, longer conversations, and access to the best available models.

It is worth it once you have established the habit. Do not spend the $20 before you have opened the tool at least five times. Earn it by building the habit first.

## Your actual next step

Open claude.ai right now. Paste in something from your work today. Ask one question about it.

That is the whole orientation. Most professionals who get good at this did not start with a course or a certification. They started with one real task, on one real day, and kept going from there. You can too.
