Most AI workshops fail before they start. Not because the facilitator doesn't know enough about AI, but because the design is wrong. A slide deck about what AI can do doesn't make anyone go back to their desk and try it. What changes behavior is doing something real, in a room with other people, and seeing that it actually works.

If you're tasked with running an AI workshop for your team (or you've decided to organize one yourself), this guide covers what actually works: the design choices that create momentum, the facilitation moves that lower the fear, and the follow-through that turns a good session into lasting capability.

## Start with demystification, not instruction

The instinct for most first-time AI facilitators is to teach. To explain how large language models work, walk through a dozen use cases, and leave people with "homework." Resist this entirely.

The first AI workshop is about one thing: making the tool feel less foreign. [NEEDS QUOTE: Matt on demystification — the core design challenge in every session he runs, something like "the goal is to demystify, hey, this is just a chat window"] When that clicks for someone (when they realize the thing they've been avoiding is actually just a text box), everything else becomes possible.

Aim for one moment per person where they use the tool themselves and it works. That's the whole objective. Everything else is secondary.

## Before you run the workshop

### Pick the right scope

A two-hour "AI overview for the whole company" is almost always the wrong format. Too many skill levels in the room. Too much ground to cover. Too little time to actually try anything.

Instead, aim for smaller, more focused sessions: a team of 8-15, a single use case they all share, and 60-90 minutes of hands-on work. You can run more workshops. You can't undo the damage of a session where half the room tuned out because the content was pitched wrong.

The sweet spot is a team that shares a real problem. HR teams running this with performance review season approaching, for example, or a marketing team prepping for a campaign sprint. The more specific the problem, the more engaged the room. Vague prompts get vague outputs; the same is true of workshops.

### Choose one tool and one task

Ask participants to install Claude on their laptops before the session (the free version works). Claude's conversational interface is the lowest-friction starting point for most non-technical professionals. It doesn't require setup, accounts, or technical context to start producing useful output.

Then pick one task the whole team does regularly. Meeting notes. First drafts of weekly updates. Summarizing a long document they've already read. The task should be something participants have actual material for, because the magic happens when they use their own real content, not a hypothetical example.

### Set the right room expectation

Send a one-paragraph message before the session. Something like: "This isn't a presentation. We're going to try something together and see what happens. Bring a work task you've been avoiding, or something you'd like to do faster. No preparation required."

That framing does two things. It removes the test anxiety that comes with "learning." And it signals that confusion and imperfection are expected. Both matter enormously for a room that includes skeptics.

## How to facilitate the session

### Open with honesty, not hype

Don't start by telling people AI is going to change everything. That's a conversation that puts people on the defensive immediately. Instead, start with: "I'm going to show you something I've found genuinely useful. I'd love for you to try it. Let's see what happens."

The energy you want in the room is curiosity, not pressure. Your job as the facilitator isn't to convince anyone of anything. It's to create enough safety for people to experiment.

Ask people to share their current experience level in the first five minutes. A quick round-the-room: "On a scale of 1 to 5, how much have you used AI tools for work?" This does two things. It levels the playing field (nobody feels alone in being a beginner). And it gives you a read on the room before you decide how much to explain versus how much to jump in.

### Run a "prompt along," not a demo

The most common facilitation mistake is showing the tool while participants watch. Watching Claude respond to someone else's prompt is about as engaging as watching someone else eat. What shifts the room is having everyone try the same thing at the same time, together.

A "prompt along" format works like this: put a starting prompt on screen and explain what you're trying to do, then give everyone 3-4 minutes to try it with their own content. Then ask 2-3 people to share what they got. The variation in outputs, across the same team, is usually the best moment of the session. People realize there's no single right answer, and they start to see what steering the tool looks like.

One thing to plan for: people who get stuck immediately. Have a simple fallback prompt ready. Something like: "Summarize this paragraph in three bullet points" with a sample paragraph pre-loaded. The goal is that nobody sits with a blank screen for more than 30 seconds. Momentum matters more than any individual output.

### Address the skeptic in the room directly

Every workshop has at least one. The person who is visibly unimpressed, or who asks pointed questions about accuracy or job displacement. Don't deflect these. Engage them.

The most effective response to skepticism is honest agreement. "You're right, it gets things wrong. Let me show you what that looks like." Then pull up a bad output, ask the room what's off about it, and walk through how you'd revise the prompt. That moment reframes the whole workshop: the workshop is about learning to steer AI, not blind trust.

Jill Ozovek's framework for meeting skeptics applies here directly: the issue is rarely the tool. It's that people derive real meaning from expertise they spent years building, and AI feels like it's questioning the value of all that. Acknowledge that directly, and the resistance often softens.

### End with a commitment, not homework

At the end of the session, don't hand out a reading list or a list of "things to try this week." Those get ignored. Instead, ask each person to name one task they're going to use Claude for before the next team meeting. Out loud, to the group. That social commitment is more powerful than any follow-up email.

Give them 5 minutes at the end to do it. Right there, before the session ends. Have them open Claude, start the task, and then close their laptop. The point is to make the first real try feel normal, not to finish the task.

## Common mistakes to avoid

**Covering too many tools.** Pick one. Claude, or ChatGPT, or whatever your company uses. The moment you introduce multiple interfaces is the moment you've made this about tool comparison instead of practice.

**Skipping the hands-on component.** [NEEDS VERIFIED STAT: percentage of employees who feel confused about how to use AI at work — remove or replace with verified source] Explanation rarely resolves confusion. Doing does.

**Running a one-and-done event.** A single workshop builds awareness. Capability comes from repetition. The most durable outcomes happen when the session is followed by regular, low-stakes practice: a 30-minute team check-in two weeks later, a Slack channel where people share what's working, a follow-up session for the people who got curious.

**Making it about you.** If you're the person who organized this, there's a temptation to demonstrate expertise. Resist it. The more you frame yourself as someone also figuring it out, the more permission others feel to be imperfect. "I tried this last week and got a mediocre first draft, and here's how I iterated" is always more useful than a polished demo.

## What to do after the workshop

The 72 hours after a workshop are where most of the follow-through happens. Or doesn't. Send a short message the next day. Not a summary of everything covered. Just a single prompt: "What's one thing you tried since yesterday?"

Two weeks out, schedule a 30-minute follow-up. Ask what's actually working. Share one new technique. Give people space to report that they haven't used it at all, without shame. The "I tried it and stopped" conversation is just as useful as the success story, because it reveals where the friction actually lives.

Over time, look for the two or three people who've gotten genuinely curious. Those are the people who can keep the momentum going without you needing to run every session yourself. Give them space and recognition, and they'll do most of the work.

## On being the person who runs this

[NEEDS REAL EXAMPLE: Matt personal anecdote about running a session that got messy — a moment where something didn't work as expected and how staying curious about it changed the room's energy]

There's a particular courage in volunteering to run an AI workshop when you're not an expert. You're going to get questions you can't answer. You're going to have outputs that don't work as expected. That's actually the most useful thing you can show.

When you say "I don't know, let me try this" and then iterate in front of the room, you model the exact skill the team needs to develop. The ability to stay curious when the output is wrong. To try again without embarrassment. To treat AI as a collaborator rather than an oracle.

That's what running this workshop is. A managerial relationship. You manage the inputs, the context, and the direction. Claude brings scale and speed. The combination is what makes it work.

The professionals building this muscle fastest aren't doing it alone. They're doing it in community, with peers who are in the same learning curve, practicing together and sharing what's working.
