Your job is already about managing complexity on behalf of someone else. You context-switch faster than anyone in the building, you draft communications that sound like another person, and you do research that makes your executive look prepared for rooms they barely have time to show up to. That's not a small thing. That's a set of skills that maps almost perfectly onto how AI tools work best.

Executive assistants who are picking up Claude and ChatGPT right now aren't replacing their judgment. They're giving themselves a capable collaborator for the parts of the job that eat time without requiring the relationship intelligence only they have.

Here's where the biggest gains are, and how to get started.

## The three areas where AI for executive assistants pays off fastest

Not every task is worth rerouting through an AI tool. But three areas of the EA role are particularly well-suited to working with Claude or ChatGPT: drafting communications, preparing for meetings and research, and handling the information synthesis that used to mean reading everything yourself.

### 1. Communications: drafting in someone else's voice

Writing emails, memos, and messages that sound like your executive, not you, is one of the hardest parts of the job. You've spent months learning how they phrase things, what they care about, what they don't say directly but expect people to understand. That institutional knowledge is yours. AI can't replace it. But it can take that knowledge and use it to produce faster first drafts.

The key is giving Claude or ChatGPT enough context upfront. Not "write an email declining this meeting" but something more like this:

> "Draft an email from Sarah (VP of Ops, direct but warm, hates long emails) to her direct report declining a project proposal. The proposal is well-researched but the timing doesn't work with Q3 priorities. Sarah wants to encourage the person, leave the door open for Q4, and not get into specifics about why. Keep it under 100 words."

That level of context takes 45 seconds to write and produces a first draft that needs minor adjustments rather than a full rewrite. If you do this once a day for a week, the time savings compound.

### 2. Meeting prep and research: brief faster, better

An hour of research used to mean reading five articles and hoping you caught the important parts. With Claude, you can paste in a document, a news article, a company earnings call transcript, or a collection of meeting notes and ask specific questions about it.

The move isn't "summarize this for me." The move is: "My executive is meeting with the CFO of [company] tomorrow to discuss a potential partnership. Read this and tell me the three things she should know before walking in, the one number she should have memorized, and the most likely question she'll be asked that she won't expect."

That specificity is what separates useful AI output from generic output. [What AI can and can't do](/learn/what-ai-can-and-cant-do) matters here: Claude is good at pulling out what you tell it to look for. It's not good at knowing what matters to your executive unless you tell it.

Matt Hastings, co-founder of MVP Club, described his own version of this in a recent community session: "My current workflow checks our meeting transcripts, goes and opens our transcript website, navigates to the transcripts from the past week, reads through them, and gives me a summary. The web page navigation, scheduled automation, task workflow automation is very useful for getting information that's otherwise in a different context. You just say, go find it."

That's the EA mindset applied to AI: you're not doing less work, you're redirecting your effort toward the parts that require your judgment.

### 3. Scheduling logistics: reducing the back-and-forth

Scheduling coordination isn't intellectually demanding, but it is cognitively taxing because of the constant context-switching it requires. AI tools can help here in two ways.

First, drafting scheduling emails. Most scheduling emails follow predictable patterns: proposing times, confirming details, sending reminders, following up on no-responses. Claude can draft these in seconds if you describe the situation. You edit for accuracy and send. The drafting is the part that eats attention.

Second, building reusable templates. If your executive has a recurring set of meeting types (board prep, 1:1s, client dinners, media calls), you can build a set of prompts in Claude that handle the standard scheduling communications for each. Set it up once. Reuse it every week.

Angela Peat, an MVP Club community member, put it well in a community session: "If you had transcripts of your meetings and they were a cadence meeting you had on the regular, you could have AI send everyone an email with the updates from the last meeting. Or some really nice ways of automating keeping people's context put together when people jump in and out." Jill Ozovek, MVP Club co-founder, added: "You could have like a recap with who said they were doing what, and schedule it so 48 hours before the next meeting, it resends a shorter recap with who said they'd do what. How many times have we all been to a meeting when someone's like, oh, I didn't get to do that."

## A detailed walkthrough: drafting communications in Claude

This is the quickest win for most EAs, so here's exactly how to do it.

**Step 1: Build a context block for your executive.**

Before your first session with Claude or ChatGPT, spend 15 minutes writing a brief description of your executive: their communication style, their typical concerns, the audiences they write to most often, phrases they use and phrases they'd never use, and the general tone of their external vs. internal communications.

Save this as a document. You'll paste it into the beginning of any new Claude chat where you're drafting on their behalf.

**Step 2: Give Claude the situation, not just the task.**

Instead of "write an email declining this meeting," give Claude the situation:

- Who is this person to your executive?
- What is the subtext of the situation?
- What outcome does your executive actually want from this email?
- What should the tone be?
- Are there things that should NOT be said?

The more situation, the better the draft.

**Step 3: Edit for voice, not content.**

The first Claude draft will probably get the content right and the voice partially right. Your job is to edit for the places where it doesn't sound like your executive. Make a note of the phrases you changed. Over time, you can add those corrections back into your context block so future drafts need less editing.

**Step 4: Build your prompt library.**

After a month of doing this, you'll have a set of situation types you handle regularly. Write a standardized prompt for each one. Keep them somewhere you can grab them quickly. This is the stage where the time savings become significant.

## Tool recommendations

For most EAs, **Claude** (claude.ai) is the better starting point for communication drafting because its outputs tend to read more naturally and it handles longer context well. The $20/month Claude Pro subscription is worth it if you're using it daily: faster responses, longer conversations, and access to the best available model.

**ChatGPT** (chat.openai.com) is a solid alternative and slightly more familiar to most people. For research synthesis, both tools perform well. Try both for a week with the same types of tasks and see which output requires less editing for your specific use cases.

Neither tool connects directly to your calendar or email unless you're using a tool like Microsoft Copilot (if your organization has it) or a third-party integration. For most EAs, the workflow is: complete a task or get information, open Claude, draft or synthesize, copy to your actual tool. It's a two-window workflow, not a one-click solution. That's worth knowing upfront.

The pattern Matt Hastings described at dinner with two senior colleagues captures it: one was a Chief of Staff "still very much at the beginning of her journey," and the other was a high-level ops person who was nearly keeping up with the engineers. EAs who develop fluency with AI tools have a natural pathway to expanding their scope: AI project coordination, internal tool adoption, workflow design. These are roles that didn't exist two years ago and that organizations are actively trying to fill with people who already understand how they work.

That's not pressure to become something you're not. It's context for why the time you put into learning these tools compounds in your favor. ## Your first week: a 5-day micro-practice schedule

You don't need to overhaul how you work. You need five small experiments.

**Monday:** Draft one email in Claude that you'd normally write yourself. Give it real context (situation, audience, tone, constraints). Compare the draft to what you would have written. Note what you changed.

**Tuesday:** Take meeting notes or a document you need to read and paste it into Claude. Ask three specific questions about it instead of reading the whole thing. See if you got what you needed in less time.

**Wednesday:** Write a 10-sentence description of your executive's communication style. Save it. Use it as the opening context block for a drafting session.

**Thursday:** Build one reusable prompt for a scheduling email type you send regularly. Test it. Refine it.

**Friday:** Reflect: which task felt most useful? Which felt awkward? That tells you where to focus next week.

The goal in week one isn't to be efficient. It's to find the one or two use cases where you naturally reach for Claude instead of having to remind yourself to try it. Those are the ones that will stick.

## Frequently asked questions

**Will my organization's IT policy allow me to use Claude or ChatGPT?**

Many organizations have guidelines about what information can be pasted into external AI tools. If you're unsure, check with IT before pasting anything confidential. A good rule: treat Claude like a public email. Don't paste internal financial data, personal information about employees, or anything marked confidential. For most communications drafting and research synthesis, this isn't a barrier.

**What if the draft doesn't sound like my executive?**

That's the editing step. The draft is a starting point, not a finished product. Over time, your context block gets better and your drafts need less editing. [The first week usually feels awkward for everyone](/learn/ai-beginner-mistakes), not just EAs. Stick with it.

**Do I need the paid version of Claude or ChatGPT?**

The free versions are a fine starting point. If you're using it daily and running into limits or slow responses, the $20/month paid tier is worth it. Think of it as the cost of a few hours of your time per month, which it will save you in the first week.

**Can Claude access my calendar or email directly?**

Not without additional integrations. The core workflow is copy, paste, draft, copy back. That sounds like friction, but most EAs find the drafting speed more than makes up for it.
