A marketing manager at a mid-size company recently described her week like this: she spent Monday writing a campaign brief, Tuesday drafting three versions of ad copy for a campaign that launched Thursday, Wednesday pulling analytics from four different platforms and trying to make sense of conflicting numbers, and Friday writing a recap deck. She did all of this in parallel with twelve Slack threads, two agency calls, and a budget review.

That week is not unusual. It is the job.

What's changing is that every single task she described is one where Claude or ChatGPT can meaningfully cut the time and friction. Not replace her judgment. Not automate her job. But act as a capable collaborator who does the drafting, the synthesizing, and the first pass at analysis so she can spend her time on the parts that actually require her.

This guide walks through the three highest-impact use cases for AI in marketing work, with specific workflows, real prompts, and an honest look at where the tools shine and where they still need a human in the loop.

## The three places AI makes the biggest difference for marketing teams

Marketing work has a particular characteristic that makes it a strong fit for AI: most of it involves generating, editing, and analyzing text. Campaigns start as briefs. Briefs become copy. Copy runs, and then you write a performance report. You pitch new ideas, respond to stakeholder feedback, and then do it all again next quarter.

AI tools like Claude and ChatGPT are built for exactly this kind of work. Where AI currently falls short is anywhere that requires deep institutional knowledge, relationship context, or creative instinct that comes from years of understanding what a specific audience actually responds to. You bring that. The AI handles the volume.

Here are the three use cases where AI for marketing managers delivers the most consistent return.

### 1. Campaign briefs and strategy documents

The brief is both the most important document in marketing and the one most likely to be written in a rush on a deadline. A weak brief produces weak creative. A strong brief creates alignment, saves revision cycles, and gives agencies and internal teams something they can actually act on.

Claude is particularly good at brief development because it can help you think through structure, surface questions you haven't answered yet, and generate a complete draft from a conversation. Most marketers find that talking through a campaign with Claude (describing the product, the goal, the audience, the channel, the timing) produces a usable brief draft in about 20 minutes. Without AI, the same document might take two hours of writing and internal alignment.

The prompt that works well here: "I'm developing a campaign brief for [product/service]. The goal is [specific outcome]. Primary audience is [description]. We're launching on [channels] in [timeframe]. Here's what we know about the audience already: [paste any research or personas]. Help me draft a complete brief including objectives, key messages, audience insights, creative direction, and success metrics."

What you get back will likely be too long and not specific enough to your brand. That's fine. You edit it down. The point is having something to react to instead of a blank page.

### 2. Ad copy and content at scale

This is where the time savings are most obvious. Writing three versions of a Facebook headline, five variations of a subject line, and two email body drafts used to be an afternoon of work. With Claude or ChatGPT, it's 15 minutes.

The key is giving the tool real context, not generic prompts. "Write me a Facebook ad" produces something generic. "Write five Facebook ad headlines for a project management tool aimed at operations managers at 200-person companies. The campaign goal is trial signups. The main pain point we're addressing is that their teams are using three different tools to track work and nothing syncs. Tone: direct, slightly skeptical of hype, not corporate." That produces something you can actually use as a starting point.

Two things to keep in mind about copy generation. First, the first draft will sound like a competent-but-generic marketing professional wrote it. That's intentional. You then edit it to sound like your brand. The editing phase is where your expertise matters. Second, ask for multiple variations with different tones and angles. Claude can produce "professional," "conversational," "punchy," and "storytelling" versions of the same ad in one response. Pick the direction you like and iterate from there.

For longer content like blog posts, landing pages, or email sequences, the workflow is slightly different. Use Claude to outline first, then draft section by section, then edit. Trying to generate a 1,000-word landing page in a single prompt usually produces something that lacks the specific angle and proof points your audience needs.

### 3. Analytics interpretation and reporting

This is the most underused AI use case in marketing, and also one of the most valuable.

Most marketing teams spend a lot of time pulling numbers and not enough time interpreting them. You export a CSV from Google Analytics, another from your email platform, another from your ad manager, and then spend an hour trying to figure out what the story actually is. Did the campaign work? Why did open rates drop? What should we do differently next time?

Claude can help you interpret data when you give it context. The workflow: copy your data into the conversation (or describe the key numbers), tell it what the campaign goal was, and ask it to help you identify the story and the next actions. You're not asking it to make decisions for you. You're asking it to help you see the patterns faster.

Here's a prompt structure that works well: "Here are the performance numbers from our Q1 email campaign: [paste numbers]. The campaign goal was [specific goal]. Our benchmark from last quarter was [comparison data]. Help me identify the three most important findings and suggest what we should test or change next quarter."

One thing to be clear about: Claude does not have access to your analytics platforms directly unless you've set up an integration. You're copying the data into the conversation. For most marketing reporting use cases, this is fine. For ongoing monitoring and alerts, you'll need platform-specific tools.

## A detailed walkthrough: writing a campaign brief from scratch

Because brief development is the highest-leverage use case and the one that creates the most downstream value, here's a step-by-step workflow you can follow this week.

**Step 1: Open Claude and start a conversation, not a prompt.**

Instead of asking for a brief immediately, describe the campaign like you would to a smart colleague who's new to the company. Tell Claude what you're selling, who you're selling it to, what the campaign is supposed to accomplish, and what you already know about the audience. This context-setting step takes 5 minutes and dramatically improves everything that comes after.

**Step 2: Ask it to identify gaps.**

Before generating a draft, ask: "Based on what I've described, what information am I missing that a strong campaign brief would typically include?" Claude will often surface things like: "You mentioned the target audience but haven't described their current alternatives" or "You've described the goal but haven't specified success metrics." These are exactly the questions a good creative director would ask. Answering them makes your brief better.

**Step 3: Generate the first draft.**

Ask for a complete brief in the format you use. If you have a template, paste it in. If you don't, Claude will generate a standard structure. Expect the first draft to be longer and more generic than you want.

**Step 4: Edit for specificity and brand voice.**

This is your job, not the AI's. Read through the draft and ask yourself: does this sound like us? Does it reflect what we actually know about this audience, not what a generic marketing playbook says about this audience type? Add the specifics only you know. Delete anything that could apply to any company.

**Step 5: Run it back through Claude for a final check.**

Ask: "Read this brief and tell me the three weakest sections. Where is the language too vague to guide creative work effectively?" This is where Claude is genuinely useful as a reviewer. It will often catch things like: "The 'key message' section says 'we make your work easier,' which is too vague to translate into creative direction. What specifically makes their work easier, and for which part of the day?"

The whole process takes 30-45 minutes instead of a half day. More importantly, the brief is usually better because the AI pushed back on vague language and helped you surface the things you hadn't fully thought through.

## Your first week: a 5-day starter plan for AI marketing managers

You don't need to rebuild your workflow to start getting value from AI. Here's a low-commitment entry point.

**Monday:** Write your next campaign brief or strategy document with Claude's help. Use the step-by-step workflow above. Time yourself. Compare to how long it usually takes.

**Tuesday:** Take a piece of copy you need to write this week (an email, an ad, a social post) and generate five variations using Claude. Include a context paragraph before asking for the copy. Edit the best version into something you'd actually send.

**Wednesday:** Find a recent performance report or analytics export. Paste the key numbers into Claude. Ask for three findings and suggested next steps. See if it surfaces anything you hadn't noticed or framed it in a way you'd use.

**Thursday:** Try Claude as a creative thinking partner on a campaign idea you're developing. Describe the concept and ask: "What's the weakest assumption in this campaign concept?" or "What angle haven't I considered for this audience?"

**Friday:** Spend 15 minutes reflecting. Which of the four tasks saved you meaningful time? Which felt clunky or produced output you couldn't use? Write down what you'd repeat next week and what you'd skip.

## Common mistakes marketing professionals make with AI

After seeing a lot of professionals go through their first weeks with these tools, a few patterns come up repeatedly.

**Prompting without context.** "Write me a campaign brief" produces a template. "Help me write a brief for a Q3 retention campaign targeting customers who haven't purchased in 90 days, with a $50 reactivation offer, aimed at 35-55-year-old female shoppers who originally bought from our wellness category" produces something you can work with.

**Expecting brand-perfect output on the first try.** Claude does not know your brand voice unless you teach it. Build a context paragraph that describes your tone, your audience, and what you want to avoid. Save it somewhere and paste it at the start of any new creative conversation.

**Using it for one task instead of a conversation.** The real value is the back-and-forth. Ask Claude to generate copy. Tell it what's wrong. Ask for revisions. Push back on the revision. The third or fourth version is usually where it gets interesting.

**Skipping the editing step.** AI-generated copy that goes out unedited almost always reads as AI-generated copy. Your job is not to prompt; your job is to edit. The tool speeds up the drafting. Your expertise makes it real.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I paste confidential campaign data into Claude?**

Check your company's AI policy first. Most companies have guidance on what data can be shared with external AI tools. For analytics interpretation, you can often share aggregated numbers rather than raw customer data. When in doubt, anonymize or talk to your IT or legal team.

**How do I make the copy sound less like AI wrote it?**

Give Claude more specific direction before it drafts. Tell it to avoid certain phrases, match a tone reference, or write like a specific type of person ("write this like a brand director who reads The Atlantic, not like a tech startup"). Then edit for your specific voice. The more specific the prompt, the less generic the output.

**Will using AI get me in trouble with my agency or creative team?**

It depends on your relationship and what you're using it for. Most agencies are already using AI internally. Using it to speed up brief writing, prepare more thorough creative direction, or generate options to react to is generally seen as making their work easier, not replacing them. Be transparent about your process.

**My company doesn't allow ChatGPT or Claude. What should I do?**

Many companies have approved Microsoft Copilot (built on GPT) or Google Gemini as enterprise options. The workflows described in this guide apply to any capable language model. If none are approved, this is worth raising with your IT or leadership team. The [how to pitch AI to your boss](/learn/explain-ai-to-your-team) guide covers how to make that case.
