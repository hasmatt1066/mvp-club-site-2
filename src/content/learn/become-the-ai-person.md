The role doesn't get posted on an internal job board. It goes to whoever showed up and started figuring it out.

Every organization has one right now (or is desperately looking for one). In most cases, no one planned it. You just started doing it, and now people ask you questions.

If you're reading this, you're probably already further along than you think.

## What "the AI person" actually means

It's worth separating this from some of the loaded language that floats around this topic. You're not trying to become an "AI thought leader." You're not angling for a Chief AI Officer title (at least not yet). The role I'm talking about is simpler and more valuable in the short term: you're the person on your team who has actually used these tools on real work, who others feel comfortable asking questions, and whose judgment people trust when AI comes up.

That's it. The bar is lower than most people assume. Most teams are still in the "everyone has an account, nobody has a habit" phase. Anyone who has moved into genuine daily use is already differentiated.

The career value here is real. When decisions get made about which AI tools the team adopts, which workflows get standardized, who gets invited into the pilot, or who helps onboard the rest of the team, the AI person is already in the room. That positioning compounds quickly.

## Why you don't need a technical background (and why non-technical is actually better)

I came to AI coaching from a career development background. No CS degree, no coding experience, no engineering team to lean on. And what I've found, after working with dozens of professionals on this transition, is that the non-technical background is often a feature, not a gap.

Here's why. The professionals who struggle to become the AI person on their team are usually the ones who get lost in tool comparisons, model specifications, and trying to understand the architecture behind what they're using. (You don't need to know how the engine works to be a good driver.) The people who break through are the ones who pick a real task, open Claude or ChatGPT, and start.

Non-technical professionals tend to be better at translating what AI can do into language their colleagues understand. They're better at making it feel accessible to the majority of the team, who are also non-technical. They're better at meeting skeptics where they're at, because they remember being in that spot.

The question isn't whether you're technical enough. The question is whether you're willing to practice publicly, which means being imperfect in front of other people, and that's a different kind of challenge entirely.

## The move that actually builds the reputation

The career positioning I've watched work, over and over, follows a simple pattern. It's not a strategy. It's a behavior.

You figure something out. You share it.

Not in a formal presentation. Not in a carefully crafted Slack post with a header and bullet points. Informally: "Hey, I've been using Claude for X and it's saved me about two hours a week. Want to see how it works?" That's it. The seeds of the reputation are planted in those small, visible moments of use.

The key word is visible. Silent AI use doesn't build a reputation. Using Claude to write better reports but never mentioning it, never showing the process, never sharing what you figured out, keeps you efficient but anonymous. The career advantage comes from the habit plus the willingness to show your work.

## The thing most career advice on this gets wrong

A lot of content about "becoming an AI leader" front-loads the strategy. Learn all the tools. Get certified. Build a portfolio. Then position yourself.

That sequence has the causality backwards.

The reputation doesn't come before the practice. It comes from the practice. You don't need to be ready before you're visible. You need to be visible while you're getting ready.

This is the contrarian comfort I offer everyone I coach through this: you don't have to have it figured out to be the AI person. You just have to be the one who is visibly figuring it out. That's more compelling than expertise, because your colleagues can imagine themselves in your shoes. They can't imagine themselves in the shoes of someone who seems to have already arrived.

"There's something really interesting about telling a story that when somebody sees it, it's just compelling on its own," Matt Hastings has said in our community sessions. "It's like, oh, I need this person. I don't know their title or what specifically they're going to do, but I need them to do this for me."

The same logic applies to your internal reputation. You're building a story about how you work, not a credential about what you know.

## Four things to do (in order)

### 1. Build one real workflow before you talk to anyone

This is the foundation. You need something real, something that saved you actual time on actual work, before you can credibly show anything to a colleague.

Pick one recurring task. Something you do at least weekly, that has a clear output, that you've done enough times that you'd recognize if the AI version was better or worse than your own. Weekly status reports. Inbox triage. First drafts of a recurring document. Meeting prep.

Open Claude.ai (Claude Pro at $20/month is worth it for professional work; the free version is fine to start). Give it real context: who you are, what this document is for, who will read it, what the constraints are. Evaluate what comes back. Iterate until it's genuinely useful, not just technically done.

Document what you built, even informally. A saved prompt, a note in your phone, a short paragraph. That document is the start of something you can share.

If you need a place to start, [How to Build AI into Your Daily Workflow](/learn/ai-daily-workflow) has a concrete 30-minute daily structure that works for building the habit consistently.

### 2. Share before you feel ready

Once you have one workflow that genuinely works, tell someone. One person. Informally.

The timing matters here. Most people wait until they feel like an expert before mentioning something. With AI, that moment doesn't come, because the tools are changing constantly and there's always more to learn. Waiting for expertise is how people stay invisible.

Share early, with caveats. "I've been experimenting with using Claude for my weekly reports. It's not perfect but it's cut my prep time in half. Want me to show you?" That framing is honest and inviting, not a performance.

The colleague's response will tell you a lot. Some people will be curious. Some will nod politely. The curious ones are your early adopters. Spend your energy there first.

### 3. Help one person get started

Once someone shows genuine interest, offer to sit with them for 15 minutes and show them how you use it. Not a class. Not a guide. A screen share.

This is where the reputation really takes root. You're not explaining what Claude can do in theory. You're showing them the specific thing you built, in the context of work they recognize, with the real output you actually got. That's more persuasive than anything you could say, and it positions you immediately as the person who knows how to do this.

The dynamic is simple: most people don't push through the initial friction of a new tool. When someone shows them the specific step that gets past the friction, they remember who showed them. That's you.

### 4. Make yourself consistently findable on the topic

This part of the positioning work is easy to skip. But it matters.

Inside your team or organization, find small ways to stay present on the topic. Forward an article about how AI is being used in your industry. Share a prompt that worked in a Slack channel. Bring up what you've been trying in a team meeting, briefly and without making it the main event. Answer someone's AI question even if they didn't direct it at you.

None of this is showing off. It's signal. You're letting your colleagues build an accurate mental model of you as someone who is actively working with these tools. When AI comes up later, in a real decision context, they'll think of you.

## What to do when someone challenges you

The first time you share something and a skeptical colleague says "I tried that and it gave me garbage output," you'll be tempted to backpedal.

Don't. Agree with them.

"Yeah, it gives garbage output when you ask it vague questions. What were you trying to do? Let me show you how I've gotten it to work for that kind of thing."

This is the most valuable move in the whole playbook, and it's something I've coached dozens of people through. Skeptics become allies faster when you meet their resistance than when you argue with it. You're not defending AI. You're sharing what you've learned about how to get good results. That's a different conversation entirely, and it's the one that positions you as someone worth listening to.

## The career angle: what this actually gets you

Let's be direct about why any of this matters for your career.

AI adoption decisions are being made at organizations right now. Which tools to buy. Which workflows to standardize. Which roles to redefine. Who to put in charge of onboarding the rest of the team. Who to include in a pilot.

The people who get included in those decisions are the people who are already doing the work. Not the people who are planning to get started when things settle down (they won't), or who are waiting until they feel prepared (they won't).

Being the AI person isn't about having a title. It's about being in the right conversations at the right time. That positioning is built before the conversations happen, through visible practice and willingness to share.

I've worked with professionals across industries who came to this with zero technical background, myself included, and what I can tell you is: the window for this particular kind of positioning is still open. Not forever. But right now, it's genuinely available to anyone who's willing to show up and do the work.
