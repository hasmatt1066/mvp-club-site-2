Every person I've coached who's waiting to feel "ready" before they really try AI is making the same mistake: they think understanding comes first. It doesn't. Understanding comes from doing. And the sooner you accept that, the faster everything changes.

"Do first, understand later" is the only approach that actually works with AI.

## Why preparation doesn't work here

Most of us are good at preparing. We read the manual, take the course, figure out how something works before we use it. That's a perfectly sensible approach for deterministic tools: software that does the same thing every time you press the same button.

AI is different. It's probabilistic. The output shifts based on what you give it, how you phrase things, what context you bring. You cannot prepare your way into understanding that. You have to feel it. You have to run it, get something unexpected, poke at it, redirect it, and run it again. That feedback loop is the learning.

I've done this with over 100 people. The ones who read the most articles before trying didn't do better than the ones who just opened Claude and started. In fact, they often did worse, because they'd built up a mental model of how AI should work that got in the way of discovering how it actually works for them.

## What "doing first" actually looks like

It doesn't mean jumping in blind or treating this like you'd rip off a bandage. It means picking something real and specific from your actual work, not a test scenario, and asking Claude or ChatGPT to help you with it.

Something you do regularly. Something with a clear output. Something where you'd know immediately if the result was good or bad.

Not your most complex project. Not your highest-stakes deliverable. A routine email. A meeting summary. A first pass at a proposal you've written a dozen times before.

You give Claude the task. You see what comes back. And then you notice something important: how long it would have taken you to produce that draft from scratch, versus how long it took you to edit what Claude gave you. That gap is the first thing you actually understand about AI. You can't get that from reading.

> "All these companies are hyper competing right now... they all want your attention and they want people hyping them up. But what do I want these tools to do for me?" — Matt Hastings, MVP Club Getting Started session

That's the right question to start with. Not "how does AI work?" but "what do I want AI to do for me?" The first question has a hundred-page answer. The second one you can answer in five minutes, by doing.

## The understanding that only comes from experience

Some things only become clear once you're using AI regularly:

**AI doesn't do the same thing twice.** The first time you realize this, it's disorienting. You asked Claude something yesterday and got a great answer. You ask nearly the same thing today and it goes sideways. You learn, quickly, that your job is to manage inputs and evaluate outputs, not to find a magic phrase and repeat it. That's the managerial relationship with AI, and you only understand it by experiencing the variability firsthand.

**Context matters more than the question.** People who read about prompting often focus on how to phrase the request. People who use AI every day focus on what they give it to work with. The phrasing matters less than the background, the goal, the constraints, the raw material. You learn this by asking the same question with and without context and seeing what happens. Books don't teach that. Your own conversations do.

**Iteration is the process, not the failure.** Almost everyone expects a correct answer on the first try. The first time Claude gives you something that's 70% right, you want to close the tab and try again from scratch. The second or third time, you learn to build on what's working. By the tenth time, you're not even thinking about it. You're just iterating. That shift in expectation comes from repetition, not from understanding it intellectually first.

Lesley Richardson, an MVP Club member who came into our Getting Started series planning to just observe, ended up building and deploying a working site in two sessions. Her reaction afterward: "I didn't think I'd get this far." She didn't think she'd get there because she hadn't imagined getting there was possible for her. Doing it proved it was.

## The fear that keeps people in preparation mode

The most common reason people stay in preparation mode is that they're afraid of doing it wrong. They don't want to look stupid. They don't want to waste time. They don't want to figure out, two weeks in, that they've been using AI incorrectly this whole time and have to start over.

I understand that fear. It's reasonable. And it's also the wrong model for how this works.

With AI, there's no "using it wrong" in the way there is with traditional software. You don't break it. You don't corrupt your files. The downside of a bad session with Claude is that you got an output you didn't like, which took maybe five minutes. The downside of waiting to feel ready is weeks of not using a tool that would have made your work better.

The messy sessions are actually useful. The time you asked Claude something and got back gibberish taught you something. The time you gave it too little context and got something generic taught you something. The time you iterated through six drafts before landing on something good taught you something. All of that becomes part of how you work. None of it came from reading.

## How fast it actually happens

This is the thing that surprises people: you don't need very many sessions before AI starts to click.

Not because the tool is easy. Because doing something, even imperfectly, compresses the learning in a way that nothing else does. People I coach who use Claude every day for two weeks come back with opinions. They know which tasks it handles well. They know where they still have to do the work themselves. They know what to give it upfront. They didn't build that knowledge from articles or walkthroughs. They built it from forty imperfect conversations.

The [AI learning path](/learn/ai-learning-path) breaks this into three phases, but the common thread through all three is the same: you practice, you learn, you practice more. The phases exist to give you a structure for that practice. The practice itself is the thing.

Compare that to someone who spent those two weeks reading about AI, bookmarking articles, following every new tool announcement. They know more about AI in the abstract. They know much less about what AI can do for them specifically.

## One thing to do today

Open Claude or ChatGPT. Pick one task from your actual work this week, something you need to do anyway. Give it real context: who you are, what the task is, who it's for, what would make it good. Then read what comes back.

Don't judge it yet. Just notice: how close is it? What did it get right? What's off? What would you tell it to fix?

Then tell it that. And see what happens.

That loop, right there, is the skill. You can't understand it until you've run it. And once you've run it a few times, you won't need anyone to explain why doing first works. You'll already know.
