Your variance report is due in two hours, your FP&A model needs three more scenarios, and someone just emailed asking for a board-ready summary of last quarter. Finance professionals don't have a bandwidth problem. They have a time problem. And AI is one of the few tools that actually addresses both.

Finance is one of the roles where AI for finance teams has the clearest, most immediate ROI. The work is structured, the outputs are predictable, and the gap between "raw data" and "decision-ready insight" is exactly where Claude and ChatGPT do their best work.

This guide covers the three highest-impact ways finance professionals are using AI right now, with specific walkthroughs, tool recommendations, and a first-week plan you can start tomorrow.

## Why Finance Is One of the Best Fits for AI

Most roles have a mix of structured and unstructured work. Finance skews heavily structured: data goes in, analysis comes out, the formats are known, the audiences are consistent, and the stakes are clear. That structure is exactly what makes AI unusually effective here.

You're not asking AI to invent something. You're asking it to move faster through work you already know how to do. That's the sweet spot.

The three use cases below are ranked by how quickly you'll see results, not by how impressive they sound.

## The Three Highest-Impact Use Cases

### Use Case 1: Drafting Financial Narratives and Reports

The numbers in your variance report are the easy part. Writing the narrative that explains what happened, why it matters, and what leadership should do about it, that's where most finance professionals spend way too much time.

Claude is particularly good at this task because it handles structured input well. You give it the data, the context, and the audience. It produces a clear first draft. You edit for accuracy and voice. Total time savings: often 40-60% on narrative-heavy deliverables.

### Use Case 2: Scenario Analysis and Forecast Sensitivity

Running multiple scenarios manually is tedious. Rebuilding a model three times for different assumptions, then writing up what each scenario implies, eats hours. AI can't run your Excel model, but it can help you think through scenarios, draft the narrative for each, and surface assumptions you might have missed.

The combination looks like this: you run the numbers in your model, paste the outputs into Claude with context about your business, and ask it to help you articulate what each scenario means for the business and what it implies for decisions. The thinking gets faster.

### Use Case 3: Synthesizing Data for Executive Communication

Getting from a 40-row data table to a three-sentence exec summary is a skill. AI accelerates that skill considerably. You paste in the data and ask Claude to pull out the three most decision-relevant insights for a specific audience (your CFO, your board, your operating committee). You review and edit. The synthesis that used to take 30 minutes takes 5.

## Detailed Walkthrough: Drafting Financial Narratives with Claude

This is the use case with the fastest payoff, so we'll walk through it step by step.

### Step 1: Set up the context prompt

Before you paste any data, tell Claude who you are and what you need. The more context you provide upfront, the better the first draft.

A good setup prompt looks like this:

> "I'm a finance manager at a mid-size SaaS company. I need to write the narrative section of our monthly business review for our CFO and VP of Finance. The audience is financially sophisticated but busy. They want to understand what happened, why, and what we're doing about it. I'll give you the key numbers now."

### Step 2: Paste the key data points

You don't need to paste your entire P&L. Give Claude the numbers that tell the story: actuals vs. budget vs. prior period for the metrics that matter. Add any context you know that the numbers don't show (a one-time event, a timing shift, a new contract that hit late).

> "Revenue: $4.2M actual vs. $4.5M budget (6% unfavorable). The miss was primarily in SMB segment, which came in at $1.1M vs. $1.4M budget. Enterprise was favorable at $3.1M vs. $3.1M budget. The SMB shortfall is partly a timing issue, three deals that were expected to close in Q1 have now been confirmed for Q2. Gross margin was 72% vs. 71% budget, favorable due to reduced hosting costs."

### Step 3: Ask for the narrative draft

> "Write the variance narrative for this monthly review. Two to three paragraphs. Start with the headline, then the driver of the miss, then the one favorable item, then the forward-looking note on the Q2 pipeline. Keep it at an executive summary level, direct and factual."

### Step 4: Edit for accuracy and voice

The draft Claude returns will be close. It won't sound exactly like you, and it won't have the institutional knowledge that you carry in your head. That's expected. Your job at this stage is to review for accuracy, add any nuance the AI couldn't know, and adjust the voice until it reads like something you'd actually send.

This editing step is not optional, and it's not a sign that AI "didn't work." Editing a draft is always faster than creating from scratch.

### Common mistakes in this workflow

**Giving too little context.** "Write me a variance report" gives Claude nothing to work with. It needs to know your audience, your format, your business context, and the key numbers. The more specific you are, the less editing you do.

**Accepting the first output without checking the numbers.** Claude doesn't have access to your data systems. If you paste numbers in, it works with those. If your numbers were wrong or incomplete, the narrative will reflect that. Always verify facts before sending anything.

**Using it only for the first draft.** The real leverage comes from the back-and-forth. After the first draft, ask Claude to make the tone more direct, to cut it by 30%, to add a stronger opening line, or to reframe the Q2 outlook more optimistically. The second and third drafts are often where it really earns its place.

## Tool Recommendations

For finance narrative and synthesis work, Claude (at the Pro tier, $20/month) is the stronger choice over ChatGPT. The reason is practical: Claude tends to handle longer documents and more complex prompts without losing track of the context. When you paste a full operating report and ask for targeted analysis, Claude maintains coherence across longer conversations better than ChatGPT in our experience.

For data analysis that involves actual computation, ChatGPT's Code Interpreter (part of ChatGPT Plus, also $20/month) is worth knowing about. You can upload a CSV and ask it to run calculations, generate charts, or flag anomalies. It's not a replacement for your FP&A model, but it's useful for quick exploratory analysis before you build anything formal.

The honest summary: for writing, synthesis, and narrative, use Claude. For exploring raw data files when you need actual math done, ChatGPT's data analysis tools are worth trying.

Neither tool should touch your production financial systems. Both are most valuable as a drafting and thinking layer on top of the work you're already doing.

## Your First Week: A 5-Day Micro-Practice Plan

You don't need to overhaul your workflow this week. You need five small experiments that build enough familiarity that the tool becomes natural.

**Day 1 (Monday): Narrative from scratch**
Find the last variance commentary you wrote. Open Claude. Paste in the key data points and ask it to write a version with a different tone, more direct, or aimed at a less financially sophisticated audience. Compare your version to what it produces. Notice what it does well and where it misses.

**Day 2 (Tuesday): Exec summary compression**
Take a report you received or wrote this month. Paste it into Claude. Ask: "Summarize this in three sentences for a CFO who has 60 seconds. What are the three most important points?" See how close it gets to what you'd say. Edit it until you'd actually send it.

**Day 3 (Wednesday): Scenario narrative**
Pick a forecast scenario you're already running. Don't paste your model, just the key outputs. Ask Claude to write the narrative implications: what this scenario means for the business, what it implies for decisions, and what risks it surfaces. Use it as a first draft for your next scenario analysis memo.

**Day 4 (Thursday): Draft an email you've been putting off**
There's always an email in finance that requires careful wording: a budget variance explanation to a frustrated department head, a response to a question from the CFO, a memo about a process change. Open Claude, give it context about the relationship and the situation, and ask for a draft. Edit it until it sounds like you.

**Day 5 (Friday): Reflect on what worked**
Open Claude or ChatGPT and tell it what you tried this week. What saved time? What didn't land? What would you want to try next week? This reflection step isn't busywork. It helps you identify the two or three use cases worth building into your regular workflow. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I paste actual financial data into Claude or ChatGPT?

Check your company's AI policy before pasting any data. Many organizations have guidelines about what can be shared with external AI tools. When in doubt, anonymize the data or use illustrative numbers that match the real structure without exposing actual figures. You can get substantial value from AI analysis using sanitized or representative data.

### Will AI make mistakes with my numbers?

Yes, sometimes. Claude and ChatGPT can misread, misinterpret, or hallucinate numbers if your input is ambiguous. The rule is: always verify any numerical claims in AI-generated output against your source data before using them. Treat AI output as a starting draft, not a final answer.

### Do I need to learn anything technical to use these tools?

No. Claude and ChatGPT are chat interfaces. If you can write an email, you can use these tools. The skill is in how you describe your task and give context, not in any technical setup. The [AI in 10 minutes guide](/learn/ai-in-10-minutes) covers everything you need to know to get started.

### What if my firm doesn't allow AI tools?

This is increasingly common, and the answer is: respect the policy and ask questions about what's coming. Many firms are actively building out AI policies and approved tooling. Staying informed about what's available and raising your hand as someone who wants to be involved in the rollout is the smart move. The professionals who understand the tools are the ones who get asked to help figure out the policy.
