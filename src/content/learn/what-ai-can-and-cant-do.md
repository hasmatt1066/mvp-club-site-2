One of the most common things I hear from professionals starting out with AI is some version of: "I tried it, it didn't work, and now I'm not sure what it's actually good for." That confusion is reasonable. The hype around these tools is enormous, and the gap between what AI companies promise and what most people actually experience in their first few uses is real.

So here's a clear-eyed look at what AI tools like Claude and ChatGPT can genuinely do for you right now, and where they'll let you down if you lean on them wrong.

## What AI is actually good at

### First drafts of almost anything

The blank page is where most professionals lose the most time. AI is exceptionally good at giving you something to react to. You describe what you need, give it some context about your audience and goal, and it produces a draft you can edit.

The draft won't be perfect. It probably won't sound like you. But editing a 400-word draft takes 15 minutes. Starting from zero takes 45. That difference adds up fast across a week of writing.

This applies to more than emails. Status reports, meeting summaries, project briefs, talking points for a difficult conversation, LinkedIn posts, proposal outlines. Anywhere you'd typically face a blank screen, AI shortens the runway.

### Reading and summarizing long documents

You have a 50-page report that's relevant to tomorrow's meeting. With Claude or ChatGPT, you can paste the whole thing in and ask specific questions: "What are the three biggest risks in this proposal?" or "Summarize the financial assumptions in section four."

This works better than a generic summary request because the tool is reading to answer your question, not just compressing the text. You can follow up. You can ask for clarification. You can tell it what you already know and ask what's new.

This alone, for professionals who deal with information overload, can save hours a week.

### Thinking out loud about a problem

This one surprises people. Claude and ChatGPT are useful as thinking partners when you're stuck. Not because they have the answer, but because describing a problem clearly enough for the tool to respond forces you to clarify your own thinking.

Try it with something you're actually wrestling with. A team conflict you're not sure how to address. A project that keeps stalling for reasons you can't name. A decision where you can see two reasonable paths and can't get past the uncertainty. Explain the situation in plain language and ask the tool to help you think through what you might be missing.

It'll surface angles you hadn't considered. Some of them won't apply. A few of them will. That's not magic, that's useful.

### Repeatable tasks with a clear pattern

If you do the same kind of work every week (weekly status updates, meeting recaps, reviewing a standard document type), AI can dramatically speed up the routine parts once you invest a little time building a good prompt.

A product manager in our community described this: she spent 20 minutes building a prompt template for her weekly status updates. Now the first draft takes her about 5 minutes instead of 45, and she spends the rest of the time editing rather than writing from scratch.

## Where AI falls short

This is the part that matters as much as the list above. Using AI well means knowing its real limits, not the theoretical ones.

### It makes things up, confidently

This is the most important thing to understand about these tools. AI models like Claude and ChatGPT do not have a "truth detector." They generate plausible-sounding text based on patterns. When they don't know something, they often produce something that sounds credible but is simply wrong.

This is called hallucination, and it happens most often with specific facts: names, dates, statistics, citations, product details, legal or medical specifics. If you ask Claude "what were Apple's Q3 revenues in 2024?" you might get a number. That number might be correct. It might also be confidently fabricated. You need to verify it.

This isn't a reason to distrust AI entirely. It's a reason to treat AI output the way you'd treat a smart colleague's off-the-cuff recollection: useful as a starting point, worth double-checking before you stake anything on it.

### It doesn't know your context

AI tools have no idea who you are, what your organization does, who your manager is, or what happened in last week's meeting. Every conversation starts from scratch. The output you get before you give it context will be generic. The output you get after giving it real context, your actual situation, your actual audience, your actual goal, will be substantially better.

This is why so many people try AI once, get a mediocre result, and conclude it doesn't work. They gave it a vague prompt with no context and compared the output to what they could produce themselves after years of institutional knowledge.

"All these companies want your attention," I said in one of our Getting Started sessions. "But what do I want these tools to do for me?" That reframe matters. Start with your real problem. Give the tool your real context. Then evaluate.

### It has no taste (for real)

Visual work is genuinely hard for AI right now. Claude can verify that a button exists on a webpage, but it can't tell you whether the design feels right. It doesn't have aesthetic judgment.

A member of our community built a product specifically because of this problem. He put it simply: "Claude and LLMs have no taste, no visual opinion to render." That's an honest summary of a real constraint. If you need visual judgment, design intuition, or aesthetic direction, AI tools are limited partners right now.

### It can't take real-world actions (mostly)

Standard AI chat tools (Claude.ai, ChatGPT) are conversation-only. They can't send emails, update your CRM, schedule meetings, or do anything in the systems you actually use for work unless you're using specific integrations or agentic tools that wire them into those systems.

This matters because people often imagine AI as a more capable version of what it is. It's a powerful thinking and drafting tool. Getting it to actually *do* things in your existing systems takes additional setup that's outside the scope of most basic use cases.

### It doesn't remember between conversations

Each conversation with Claude or ChatGPT is a fresh start. It doesn't remember what you discussed last Tuesday. If continuity matters, you need to bring context into every session yourself: a brief summary of where things stand, relevant background documents, key decisions already made.

This is less limiting than it sounds once you build a habit around it. A short "here's my context" paragraph at the start of each session takes 30 seconds and dramatically improves the quality of what comes back.

## The category that depends entirely on you

There's one area where AI capability and your own skill are deeply intertwined: iteration.

The first output from Claude or ChatGPT is almost never the final output. The people who get the most value from these tools are the ones who treat that first response as a starting point, push back on what doesn't work, add detail, redirect, and refine. [That iterative relationship is what we mean by a managerial approach to AI](/blog/probabilistic-vs-deterministic-ai): you're managing inputs, goals, and outputs.

"This is the really important step of vibe coding," I said in a recent session. "You gave it the requirements, it built it, and now you're managing the output through evaluation." That applies equally to writing, analysis, and any other AI-assisted work. The value is in the back-and-forth, not the first result.

People who accept the first output and decide AI "doesn't work" are skipping the most important part of the process.

## A few honest calibrations

**AI is faster for most drafting work, but not always faster for high-stakes work.** When you're writing something where the specific details and your specific voice really matter, editing AI output can take longer than writing from scratch. Know when to use it and when to just write.

**It gets better as you get better at directing it.** The tool's capabilities haven't changed; what's changed is your ability to give it useful context and clear goals. [That's a practice skill, not a technical one](/blog/ai-time-paradox), and it builds with use.

**Free and paid are genuinely different.** Claude Pro and ChatGPT Plus ($20/month each) offer faster responses, more capable models, and longer conversations. If you're doing serious daily work with these tools, the paid tier is worth it. If you're experimenting, free is fine to start.

**The gap between what AI can do and what you get depends on your prompts.** "Be very specific," is advice I give constantly in our sessions. The more precise your context, audience, and goal, the better the output. Vague asks produce vague answers.

---

## Where to go from here

The honest summary: AI is genuinely good at drafting, synthesizing, thinking things through, and handling repeatable work. It's unreliable for specific facts, unaware of your context by default, weak on visual judgment, and not connected to your work systems without additional setup.

If you want to build a practical sense of where AI fits into your actual work, [the best starting point is picking one real task and trying it this week](/learn/how-to-use-ai-at-work). Not a test. Something you actually have to do.

The professionals who figure out where AI works for them aren't reading more about it. They're using it on real problems with real stakes, making note of where it helps and where it doesn't, and building judgment over time.
