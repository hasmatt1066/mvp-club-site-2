You've been using Claude for a few weeks. You can see exactly how it would help your team. And yet every time you try to say something about it at work, you get polite nodding and then nothing happens.

That gap, between your own use and actual team adoption, is where most AI efforts die. Not because the tools don't work. Because nobody knows how to bridge it.

This is the roadmap. Not a corporate framework with seven layers of approval. A practical sequence based on what actually works when mid-career professionals lead this kind of change from inside an organization.

## Why most "AI adoption" goes nowhere

Before the roadmap, it helps to understand why so many organizations stall. [According to McKinsey's 2025 State of AI report](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai), only one-third of organizations are scaling AI across their enterprise, despite most having adopted it somewhere. The gap between "we use AI" and "AI is changing how we work" is wide and full of the same predictable traps.

The biggest one: organizations try to do too much at once. They announce a broad AI initiative, send everyone to a workshop, buy a tool, and then wait for adoption to happen. It doesn't. Tools don't get used consistently. Enthusiasm fades. The initiative gets shelved.

The second trap is the opposite: buying a tool and hoping individuals will figure it out on their own. Without a structured way to share what's working, individual wins stay individual. The person who discovered how to use Claude for contract review tells nobody, because there's no space to tell them. That knowledge just evaporates.

A recent HBR analysis found that AI adoption resistance is primarily a leadership problem, not a technology problem [NEEDS VERIFIED LINK]. The tools work. What doesn't work is how they're introduced, supported, and embedded into real work.

The roadmap below is built around avoiding both traps.

## Phase 1: Start with yourself (weeks 1-4)

The most credible AI leader at any organization is one who actually uses these tools every day. Everything else in this roadmap assumes that.

If you're not there yet, start with [the 30-minute daily habit](/learn/ai-daily-workflow) and commit to it for four weeks before you try to bring anyone else along. You can't teach what you haven't practiced.

The specific, grounded things you'll have to say about AI after a month of real use are categorically different from anything you can say after reading articles about it. When you tell a skeptical colleague "I've been using Claude every day for six weeks and here's what actually changed," that lands completely differently than "I think AI could be useful for our team." One is evidence. The other is a pitch.

What you're doing during this phase: finding two or three use cases that are genuinely useful for your actual work. Not hypothetical wins. Real time saved, real work made easier. That becomes your proof of concept.

## Phase 2: Find the early movers (weeks 4-8)

You don't need to convince your whole organization. You need to find three to five people who are already curious, then make it easy for them to try something.

Every organization has these people. They're the ones who asked about AI at the last team meeting. They're the ones who mentioned they tried ChatGPT but "didn't know what to do with it." They're not necessarily the most senior people on the team. Often they're not.

Your job in this phase is to stop treating this as a solo project and start treating it as a small community.

One approach that works: a standing thirty-minute weekly call where you and a few others show what you tried that week. No agenda. No pressure to have impressive results. Just sharing, including the things that didn't work. I've been running these kinds of sessions at MVP Club since the beginning, and the consistent observation is that the things that don't work teach people as much as the things that do.

> "All these companies are hyper competing right now... they all want your attention and they want people hyping them up. But what do I want these tools to do for me?" — Matt Hastings, in a Getting Started session

That recentering is what makes early mover groups work. The question isn't "what can AI do?" It's "what do I actually need this to do for me?"

## Phase 3: Run a small pilot with real stakes (weeks 6-12)

Skip the approval-seeking proof-of-concept. Go straight to a real workflow change with a small group, and see what actually happens when people use AI to do their jobs.

The best pilots are narrow. Pick one workflow. Pick one team. Set a clear timeframe, four to eight weeks. Measure something specific before and after: time spent, drafts required, errors caught, whatever makes sense for the work.

Some high-yield candidates for a first pilot:
- Meeting notes and action item capture (with Claude or a dedicated tool like Otter.ai)
- First-draft generation for regular recurring deliverables (status reports, client updates, RFPs)
- Research and summarization for any role that processes a lot of reading
- Performance review and feedback drafts for managers

Narrow beats broad every time. A pilot that involves twenty people using AI vaguely for "everything" produces no useful information. A pilot where four project managers use Claude to draft weekly status reports for six weeks produces very specific data: how long the draft takes, how much editing it needs, whether the quality improved, whether they kept doing it.

That data is what you take to leadership, not a general claim that "AI is useful."

## Phase 4: Make it easy for AI use to spread (ongoing)

This is where most AI leaders get stuck. The pilot worked. A few people are genuinely using AI and getting value from it. But it's not spreading. Why not?

Usually one of three things:

**No visibility into what's working.** People don't know what their colleagues are doing with AI. If there's no forum for sharing, wins stay private. The answer is a standing space, weekly or biweekly, where people show their work. It doesn't have to be formal. A recurring Slack thread works. A fifteen-minute segment of an existing team meeting works. The format matters less than the consistency.

**No permission to experiment.** In most organizations, people are implicitly (sometimes explicitly) told that AI use should look professional. That pressure kills experimentation. If someone tries using Claude for something and gets a mediocre output, and there's no culture of sharing failed experiments, they stop trying. Give people explicit permission to try things that don't work. That's where the real learning is.

**The skeptics are too loud.** Every team has at least one person who's skeptical about AI, often with legitimate reasons. Skeptics are not the enemy. They're useful. But they can shut down a fragile culture of experimentation if they're positioned as the voice of reason. The move is not to argue with them. It's to show, not tell, and to give them something low-stakes to try themselves. ## Phase 5: Translate what's working into language leadership understands

Most AI efforts stall here: the pilot worked, a few people love it, but leadership doesn't see it. The reason is almost always that nobody made the translation.

Individual wins are real. Saved an hour on a report. Wrote a better email. They matter. But they stay invisible to leadership unless someone connects them to what leadership tracks: capacity, output quality, risk, dollars.

Do that translation yourself, as specifically as possible. Not "AI helped with our proposals." Instead: "Before we used Claude for proposal first drafts, the average time from brief to draft was three days. Now it's four hours. In Q1 we responded to eight RFPs we would have had to decline due to bandwidth. We won three of them."

That's a business case. That's what gets AI embedded into how your organization works, not just tolerated as something a few people do on the side.

The organizations where AI actually takes hold tend to be the ones tracking specific outcomes from the start [NEEDS CITATION: McKinsey on outcome-based AI objectives]. The measurement piece isn't paperwork. It's how you prove the value is real and earn the space to keep going.

## The identity question underneath all of this

The hardest part isn't the tools. It's the identity question.

When you start talking to your team about AI, you're implicitly asking people to question something about how they work, maybe about how they see themselves professionally. The colleague who's been writing excellent client reports for twelve years might hear "let's use AI for reports" as "your reports aren't good enough." That's not what you mean. But it can be what they hear.

The antidote is leading with curiosity instead of efficiency. Not "AI will make our reports faster" but "I've been experimenting with using Claude on my own writing and I'm finding it does some interesting things. Want to try it on something together?" That's a completely different conversation. One feels like a judgment. The other feels like an invitation.

> "The goal is to demystify, right? Like, hey, this is just a chat window and it's just doing stuff on your computer." — Matt Hastings

That framing, "just a chat window," is a gift, not a simplification. It gives people permission to interact with AI like they'd interact with a capable colleague: expecting to direct it, push back on it, and evaluate its work, rather than treating it as a magic box that either works or doesn't.

That managerial framing is what turns occasional use into real fluency. People stop waiting for the perfect prompt and start having conversations. They stop being disappointed by imperfect outputs and start iterating. The skill compounds from there.

## Where to start

Pick one thing from Phase 1 and do it this week. Not Phase 2. Phase 1.

Use Claude for thirty minutes on something real: a report, a meeting summary, a proposal section. Not as a test of the tool. As a genuine attempt to get useful work done. Notice what worked, what didn't, and what you'd try differently.

That's it. That's the start.

A month from now you'll have real examples to share. Two months from now, you'll have early movers. Six months from now, you may have a business case. The roadmap isn't a sprint. It's a sequence of small bets, each one building on the last.

The professionals making it real at their organizations right now didn't start with a strategy deck. They started with a Tuesday afternoon and a task they'd been putting off. You can do the same thing this week.
