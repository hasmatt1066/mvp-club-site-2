Most AI governance conversations start in the wrong place. They start with risk, restriction, and a list of tools employees are not allowed to use. That's understandable. But the organizations getting the most out of AI adoption right now started somewhere different: they asked what their people needed to feel confident using these tools, and then built the minimal structure to make that possible.

AI governance isn't primarily a legal exercise. It's a clarity exercise. Your team is already using ChatGPT, Claude, and a half-dozen other tools whether you have a policy or not. [NEEDS VERIFIED STAT: organizations lacking formal AI governance policies] That gap isn't going to close by hoping people stop. It closes when you give them clear guidance on how to proceed.

This is a guide for the leader who isn't a technologist but is responsible for how AI gets used on their team.

## What AI Governance Actually Needs to Cover

Before writing a single policy, it helps to understand what you're actually governing. Most of what gets called "AI governance" at the team or department level comes down to four questions:

1. Which tools are approved for which types of work?
2. What data can and can't go into those tools?
3. Who reviews and owns the outputs?
4. Who decides when a use case is new enough to need a conversation?

That's it. Everything else (model selection, vendor contracts, enterprise licensing, compliance audits) is real but lives above the team level. If you're a VP, a director, or a department lead trying to get your team moving safely, these four questions are the right scope.

I keep coming back to this in coaching sessions: "All these companies are hyper competing right now. They all want your attention and they want people hyping them up. But what do I want these tools to do for me?" That's the right frame for governance, too. Not "what do the vendors promise" but "what does my team actually need to do the work safely and well?"

## The One Document You Need Before Anything Else

Call it an Acceptable Use Policy, a Team AI Charter, or whatever fits your organization's culture. The name doesn't matter. What matters is that it answers the four governance questions above in plain language that any team member can understand in five minutes.

[NEEDS REAL EXAMPLE: Matt's governance coaching experience — the moment a simple one-page charter changed how a team engaged with AI tools]

Here's what a simple version looks like. On approved tools: Claude (Pro or Team accounts), ChatGPT (Team or Enterprise), Microsoft Copilot if your organization has it. Personal free-tier accounts are fine for experimentation but not for work involving real project data.

On what stays out of AI tools: client names and contact information, employee compensation or HR records, unreleased financial information, anything under NDA, login credentials. When in doubt, anonymize before you paste. Replace "Acme Corp" with "Client A," swap real budget numbers for ballpark figures. You keep 90 percent of the usefulness and eliminate nearly all the risk.

On who owns the output: the person who used the tool owns the output. They're responsible for reviewing it, editing it, and standing behind it. AI produces drafts. Humans produce deliverables.

On flagging something new: if you're not sure whether a use case is covered, bring it to your manager before you start, not after. This isn't a gotcha process. It's a conversation.

That document, even if it's two pages, gives your team something to stand on. It replaces the anxiety of guessing with the clarity of knowing.

## The Data Question Is the Only Hard Part

Most AI governance for non-technical leaders comes down to one practical skill: knowing what data belongs in an AI tool and what doesn't. The rest is process.

The risk isn't that your team will use AI wrong. The risk is that someone will paste something into a personal-tier account that shouldn't be there, and that information will potentially be used to train the model. That's the actual exposure. Everything else is noise.

One member from our Getting Unstuck sessions described her company's situation clearly: "Legal won't approve that for us. There's a lot of things they're still holding off on." That friction is real. But it usually signals that legal and IT haven't had the conversation yet, not that the use case is actually risky. Your job as the non-technical leader is to bring those stakeholders the right question: not "can we use AI" but "here's the specific use case and the data involved. What do you need to see to feel comfortable?"

The framing shift matters. A blanket question gets a blanket cautious answer. A specific question about a specific workflow gets a specific useful answer.

## How to Choose What to Allow (and What to Wait On)

Not every use case needs to wait for enterprise licensing and legal review. Some things are safe to green-light now. Others need more runway. Here's a practical way to think about it.

Some things are safe to green-light now: using Claude or ChatGPT to draft internal communications, researching market context, improving writing clarity on documents the employee already authored, generating templates the team then customizes. Low stakes, no sensitive data, plenty of human review before anything ships.

Talk to IT or legal first for anything that involves client data (even anonymized), AI features embedded in tools your organization hasn't formally approved, output that goes directly to a client without human review, or use cases involving regulated data like HIPAA or FERPA.

Some things need more runway: fully automated workflows with no human review, AI tools that connect to internal systems, or anything that influences personnel decisions. That's not a permanent no. It's a "not yet."

The goal isn't to lock down the hard category forever. It's to move the first two forward fast while you build the trust and processes needed for the rest.

## Building a One-Page Team AI Log

As your team's AI use grows, you'll want one simple document that tracks what's in use and who owns it. Not a compliance database. A living one-page list.

For each AI use case or tool your team has approved, track four things: what it does (one sentence), who owns it (the person responsible for reviewing outputs), what data goes into it, and when you last reviewed it (a date, updated quarterly).

This document serves a practical purpose: it gives you something to show when leadership, legal, or IT asks "what exactly are you doing with AI?" It also helps you spot when a team member has started using something that isn't on the list, which opens a conversation rather than a confrontation.

The teams doing this well aren't running it as compliance theater. They update it when something changes, bring it to quarterly planning, and use it to identify where they want to expand.

## The Skeptics on Your Team

Any team of more than five people probably has at least one person who is genuinely skeptical of AI, not as a performance but as a considered position. That person has a job to do, and your governance structure should make space for them.

The right governance framework isn't one that mandates AI use. It's one that clears the path for people who want to use it and doesn't penalize people who are still figuring it out. "You don't have to have it all figured out," is the right framing. The policy creates an environment where trying is safe. Individual pace is still individual.

The professionals who find it hardest to get started are often the ones who are most skilled at their current work. They've built deep expertise over years, and AI tools can feel like they're questioning that expertise rather than extending it. ## What "Leading AI at Your Organization" Actually Means

Most governance conversations miss the most important part. The people who become the AI leaders at their organizations don't start by drafting perfect policies. They start by using the tools themselves.

I say this constantly in coaching sessions: the only way to understand what AI means for you and your career is to pick up the tool and use it every day. That's not just advice for the individual contributor. It applies directly to the leader who wants to govern AI well. You can't write useful policy for something you haven't used. You'll write restrictions that don't address the real risk and miss the protections that actually matter.

The most effective non-technical AI leaders in our community are the ones who spent a month using Claude and ChatGPT for their own work before they started thinking about what to put in a policy. They came to the governance conversation with specific use cases, a real sense of where the output quality was good and where it needed review, and the credibility of someone who had actually done the thing they were governing.

Start with your own work, not a committee. A thirty-day habit of trying something new each week will teach you more than any policy document you could draft from scratch.

## The Governance Document Your Team Needs (And When to Update It)

A few practical notes on keeping this alive after you write it.

Review it quarterly. The AI tool landscape moves fast enough that a policy written six months ago may reference tools or account tiers that no longer exist in the same form. Put a date on the document. Set a calendar reminder.

Update it when something surprising happens. If a team member uses AI in a way that wasn't covered and it went well, add it to the green-light list. If something went wrong (or almost went wrong), update the policy to reflect the lesson. Governance that learns from real use is worth ten times the governance written from first principles and never touched again.

Make it easy to find. The policy that lives in a SharePoint folder nobody knows about is not a policy. A two-page doc in the team Notion or Slack pinned resources is.

Don't mistake the document for the culture. The document is a starting point. The culture is what happens when a team member asks a question and gets a useful answer, or tries something new and gets credit for being curious. The leader sets that tone. No policy does it for you.
