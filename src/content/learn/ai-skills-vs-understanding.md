Job postings requiring AI fluency grew more than 70% year over year in 2026, according to LinkedIn's latest workforce data [NEEDS CITATION: verify exact figure and source]. That number gets shared a lot. What doesn't get shared is that most of those roles are not asking for AI engineers. They're asking for people who can use Claude to draft a proposal, catch when ChatGPT gets a fact wrong, and explain to their team why a particular output needs another pass before it goes to the client.

That's a different skill set than what "AI skills" sounds like in most conversations.

## The Two Things Employers Are Actually Measuring

Here's the split I see when I work with professionals navigating this moment. On one side: people who have accumulated AI tool experience. They've used ChatGPT, they know their way around a prompt, they can produce a first draft faster than they could six months ago. That's real. It counts.

On the other side: people who have developed AI judgment. They know when to trust the output and when to push back. They can break a complex task into steps a model can actually follow. They understand the difference between a prompt that gets a generic answer and one that gets a useful one. They catch the subtle hallucination buried in paragraph three before it reaches a client.

Employers want both, but they are shorter on the second. Anyone can tell a candidate "I've used ChatGPT." Fewer can demonstrate that they know how to work with AI on a problem that has real stakes, real constraints, and real consequences if it goes sideways.

## Why "AI Skills" Became the Wrong Frame

The term "AI skills" implies something learnable in a workshop, certifiable with a badge, demonstrable in a bulleted list. That framing was borrowed from how we talk about technical skills (Excel, Salesforce, Photoshop) and it does not quite fit what working with AI actually requires.

McKinsey's research on the future of work puts it plainly [NEEDS CITATION]: as AI absorbs routine tasks like drafting content, sifting information, and organizing data, what employers need from humans is judgment, critical thinking, and the ability to validate outputs. These are not new skills. They are the skills you have already been developing throughout your career, now needed in a new context.

This is actually good news. It means the people who feel behind because they haven't taken an AI course are not as behind as they think. What you know about your industry, your clients, your organization's priorities: all of that is what makes AI outputs useful or useless in your specific situation. A generalist AI tool produces generalist outputs. Your expertise is what turns those outputs into something decision-ready.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

The split between tool familiarity and genuine AI judgment shows up in a few specific ways.

**Prompt quality.** Two people can use the same tool for the same task and get wildly different results. The difference is rarely the tool. It's how clearly the person can articulate what they need, what constraints matter, and what "good" looks like for their specific situation. That clarity comes from domain knowledge plus practice, not from knowing a list of prompting techniques.

**Output evaluation.** AI tools produce confident-sounding text. The person who can recognize when that confidence is misplaced, when a fact needs checking, when an analysis is missing a key variable, that person is worth more to an employer than someone who accepts outputs at face value. This is judgment, not a skill you learn from a tutorial.

**Knowing what to delegate.** There's a real art to figuring out which parts of a task AI genuinely accelerates and which parts still require your full attention. Over-delegating leads to sloppy work. Under-delegating leaves productivity on the table. Finding that calibration takes experimentation.

## The Resume and LinkedIn Trap

Here's something I see often: professionals who are doing genuinely sophisticated AI work, but describing it in their professional profiles as if they're checking an "AI tools" box.

"Proficient in ChatGPT and Claude" reads like a line on a technical skills list. It says nothing about how you actually work with these tools or what you've produced with them.

What gets attention is specificity about outcomes. Not "used AI for content creation" but "reduced a weekly report from 4 hours to 45 minutes by building a Claude workflow that pulls from four source documents and outputs a formatted brief my manager uses directly." Not "familiar with AI tools" but "built a client onboarding process that uses Claude to draft customized intake summaries, reviewed and edited by me before client delivery."

The second versions say something about judgment. They show that a real human with expertise is in the loop, making decisions, and taking responsibility for the output. That's what employers want to see.

## What to Build vs. What to List

The professionals I coach who are getting the most traction aren't the ones who have the longest AI tool list on their resume. They're the ones who can point to something they made.

A workflow that saves their team three hours a week. A template that eliminated a recurring bottleneck. A process that used to require four rounds of revision and now requires two. These are the things that signal AI judgment to an interviewer or a client, because they demonstrate that you understood a real problem clearly enough to apply AI to it effectively.

This connects to something T Gaines discovered during one of our community sessions. She went from learning that portfolios even existed, to building a GitHub profile README, to troubleshooting the setup live with the community, all in about two hours. At the end of that session, she had something concrete to show. A page that demonstrated that she wasn't just talking about doing technical work. She was doing it.

That compression of timeline (weeks of career development work into an afternoon) is one of the genuinely interesting things AI makes possible right now. And it's more visible to employers than any tool list.

## The Three Questions to Ask Yourself

If you're trying to assess where you actually are on the skills versus understanding spectrum, three questions are more useful than any checklist.

First: when AI gives you a bad output, can you diagnose why? Not just "this is wrong" but what would need to change about the input, the context, or the framing to get a better result? If yes, you have judgment. If you mostly just keep regenerating and hope, that's a skill you can build. (Read more about this in [What to Do When AI Gives You Bad Output](/learn/when-ai-gives-bad-output).)

Second: can you explain your AI workflow to someone else? Not the mechanics of the tool, but the thinking behind it. Why you prompt it the way you do. What you trust it with and what you don't. What your editing process looks like and why. The ability to articulate this is a signal of understanding, and it's also exactly what hiring managers and clients want to hear.

Third: is your AI use improving your domain work, or replacing it? The professionals who stand out are the ones using AI to do better work in their field, not the ones who have mostly offloaded thinking to the tool. Your expertise plus AI is a durable career advantage. AI without your expertise is something any generalist could produce.

## How to Develop the Judgment Part

Practice is the only real answer here, and I know that's not what people want to hear. But there's no shortcut to the moment when you look at an AI output and just know it's wrong in a specific, articulable way. That knowing comes from repeated exposure, combined with enough domain expertise to notice the gaps.

What you can do to speed this up:

Use AI on real work, not practice problems. Toy prompts produce toy intuitions. The judgment develops fastest when there are real stakes.

Make a habit of editing AI output carefully, even when you're in a hurry. Especially when you're in a hurry. The editing process is where the judgment builds.

Talk through your AI workflow with someone else. Explaining what you're doing and why surfaces assumptions you didn't know you were making. This is part of why community matters, which I'll say more about in a moment.

Get feedback on your outputs. Not just "does this look good" but "does this actually solve the problem." The gap between AI-generated and genuinely useful is the space where your judgment lives.

## A Note on the People Who Feel Behind

One more thing, because I talk to a lot of professionals who are carrying the weight of feeling like they started too late.

Most people who feel behind are just a few months away from not feeling that way anymore. What changes is starting.

The people who look ahead of you in AI capability are not smarter or more gifted. They are mostly just a few months further into the messy, imperfect, sometimes frustrating work of using these tools on real problems every day. That's all. The gap is bridgeable, and it closes faster than you think once you're in it.

The employers and clients who matter are not looking for people who have mastered AI. They are looking for people who are genuinely capable, self-directed, and honest about what they know and don't know. That's always been true. AI just added a new dimension to demonstrate it.
