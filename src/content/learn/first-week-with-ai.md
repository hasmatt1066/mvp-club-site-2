The most useful thing I've seen in a hundred-plus coaching sessions is also the simplest: people who use AI every day get better at it. People who use it occasionally wonder why it never quite works for them.

That gap has nothing to do with technical ability. It's practice. And most first-week attempts fail not because AI is hard, but because there's no structure to follow. You open Claude or ChatGPT, stare at a blank chat window, and have no idea what to type.

This guide fixes that. Five days, one task per day, real work. By Friday, you'll have more clarity about where AI fits in your life than any tutorial could give you.

## What this week is actually about

This week, your goal is not to get amazing AI output. Your goal is to build a habit. Small, daily contact with a real task beats a two-hour AI binge once a week.

Think of it the way you'd think about learning any new skill: you wouldn't practice guitar for three hours on Sunday and expect to improve. You'd play for twenty minutes every day. AI works the same way. The understanding comes through doing, not preparation.

One more thing before we start: use the paid version of Claude or ChatGPT if you can. $20/month unlocks meaningfully better responses, longer conversations, and fewer frustrating limits. It's the best professional development money you'll spend this year. But if you're on the free tier, everything here still works. Just know the paid experience is different enough to matter.

## Day 1 (Monday): One real email

Find an email you need to write today. Not a test email. A real one, to a real person, about something that actually matters to you.

Open [Claude](https://claude.ai) or ChatGPT and tell it: who you're writing to, what the relationship is, what you want the email to accomplish, and the tone you're going for. Paste in any relevant context (a previous message thread, background on the project, whatever helps). Then ask it to draft the email.

Read what it gives you. It almost certainly won't sound exactly like you, and that's fine. The point of today isn't to send what the AI wrote. The point is to start with something rather than a blank screen. Edit the draft until it sounds like you, and notice how much faster that is than starting from nothing.

That editing process, where you push back on what the AI gave you and make it yours, is the core skill. You're not outsourcing your work. You're managing the inputs, goals, and outputs of a capable assistant who needs your direction.

**One thing to try:** Before you send the email, open a new Claude chat and paste your final version. Ask: "Is there anything in this email that could be misread or land badly?" That second pass often catches things you'd miss on your own.

## Day 2 (Tuesday): Prep for a meeting

Pick a meeting on your calendar this week where you could use a sharper perspective going in. It could be a team check-in with a tricky agenda, a client call you want to prepare for, or a conversation you've been half-avoiding.

Paste the meeting agenda, pre-read materials, or a brief description of the situation into Claude or ChatGPT. Then ask a specific question: "What are the three things most likely to come up in this meeting that I should be prepared for?" or "What's the strongest argument against my proposal?"

You're not outsourcing your thinking. You're giving yourself a head start. Spend five minutes reviewing what it gives you and sharpen your own talking points from there.

**The difference between useful and useless here:** Asking "summarize this agenda" gets you a summary. Asking "what should I be watching for in this conversation given that the VP has been skeptical of this project?" gets you something you can actually use. The more context you give, the better the output. That's not a quirk of AI. That's how it works.

## Day 3 (Wednesday): Tackle something that's been sitting on your to-do list

You know the task. The one that keeps getting pushed to tomorrow. The report nobody's asked for yet but you know you need to write. The proposal that needs to go out. The summary of a project that's wrapped up but never properly documented.

Today you're going to take one of those and get a working draft by end of day. Paste whatever context you have (notes, meeting transcripts, emails, bullet points) into Claude and ask it to turn the material into a first draft of whatever you need. Doesn't have to be perfect. It just needs to exist.

> "All these companies are hyper competing right now... they all want your attention and they want people hyping them up. But what do I want these tools to do for me?" — Matt Hastings (from an MVP Club Getting Started session)

That question is exactly the right frame for today. Don't start with "what can AI do?" Start with "what do I actually need done?" Then ask AI to help with that specific thing.

**What to expect:** The first draft will probably need real editing. That's normal. A draft that's 60% of the way there is still ahead of a blank page, and you'll get faster at prompting as you build a sense of what context Claude actually needs.

## Day 4 (Thursday): Think through something you're stuck on

This is the one that surprises people most. You don't need a task for this. You need a problem.

Pick something you've been going around in circles on. A decision you keep postponing. A project issue you can't quite diagnose. A conversation you're dreading because you can't figure out what you actually want to say.

Describe the situation to Claude in detail. Not "help me decide" but "here's what I'm weighing, here's what I'm worried about, here's what I've already tried, help me see what I might be missing." Treat it like thinking out loud with a colleague who has no political agenda and a lot of time for you.

Push back on what it says. Ask follow-up questions. Tell it where it's wrong. This is where using AI stops feeling like a search engine and starts feeling like a working relationship.

**One observation from coaching:** The professionals who get the most out of this kind of session aren't the ones who accept Claude's first answer. They're the ones who say "okay, but what if you're wrong about X?" That back-and-forth is where the real thinking happens.

## Day 5 (Friday): Reflect and decide what sticks

Don't skip this one. It's the one most people skip, and it's the one that determines whether next week looks different from this one.

Open a Claude or ChatGPT chat and describe your week. What did you try? What worked? What felt clunky or forced? What would you want to do differently?

Then ask it to help you write a short paragraph capturing what you want to keep doing next week. Not a big productivity system. Just: what one or two habits are actually worth continuing?

That reflection is what separates people who build an AI practice from people who dabble. The ones who stick with it aren't the most technical. They're the ones who found, in week one, at least one thing that made their work genuinely easier, and they kept doing that thing.

## The common mistake to avoid this week

You're going to be tempted, at some point, to read about AI instead of using it. A newsletter comes in with ten prompting tricks. A LinkedIn post promises to "transform how you work in five minutes." You'll click it, read it, and suddenly twenty minutes have passed and you haven't actually opened Claude.

As I covered in [The AI Time Paradox](/blog/ai-time-paradox), this is the trap: the same pressure making you want to learn AI is the same pressure that eats the time you'd spend actually using it. Reading about AI is easier than using it. It also teaches you almost nothing.

Close the tab. Open Claude. Do the task.

## What "getting good at this" actually looks like

After week one, you'll probably have two or three use cases that clicked and three or four that felt like more effort than they were worth. That's exactly right. The goal isn't to use AI for everything. The goal is to find the places where it genuinely helps you and build those into your regular workflow.

The shift that happens around weeks three and four is that you stop consciously deciding to use AI. You start reaching for Claude or ChatGPT the way you'd reach for a calculator or a search bar: not because you're "doing an AI thing," but because it's just faster. The tool becomes part of how you think.

That shift doesn't come from a workshop or a tutorial series. It comes from [doing the thing every day until it becomes familiar](/blog/what-to-actually-do-about-something-big-is-happening). This week is the beginning of that.

## A few practical notes

**Which tool to use:** Claude and ChatGPT are both good starting points. Claude tends to produce writing that sounds more natural and handles nuanced professional situations well. ChatGPT is widely used and has a larger ecosystem of integrations. Try both this week if you're curious. See which one's responses feel more useful for your kind of work.

**How much context to give:** More than you think. When Claude gives you something generic, that's almost always because you gave it generic input. Paste in the actual email thread. Describe the actual person you're dealing with. Tell it what you've already tried. The more real context you provide, the more real the output.

**When the output is bad:** That's information, not failure. Ask yourself: what context did I leave out? What did I tell it to do that I actually didn't mean? Then try again with a more specific prompt. Getting better at this is mostly just getting better at noticing what you forgot to say.

**Anything you do more than once:** Write down how you did it. As Matt describes in our community sessions: "Anything you do more than once, write down how to do it, and then tell Claude, here's how I want you to do it. Just go." That's the beginning of a real AI workflow, not just occasional use.

## You don't have to figure this out alone

The professionals who get good at this fastest aren't the ones who go off and figure it out in private. They're the ones who practice alongside other people going through the same thing, see how others are using these tools, and pick up techniques they would never have found on their own.
