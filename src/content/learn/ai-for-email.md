Open your inbox right now and count how many emails need a real response. Not a quick "thanks," but a message that requires you to think, draft, and revise. For most professionals, that list is longer than they want it to be, and it refills faster than they can clear it.

AI for email writing is one of the highest-return places to start. Not because it takes over your inbox, but because it removes the friction from the two hardest parts: starting a message from scratch and processing a pile of threads you haven't touched in three days.

This guide walks through exactly how to use Claude and ChatGPT for email, with specific prompts you can copy, common mistakes to skip, and a clear picture of where AI helps versus where your own judgment still drives the work.

## Why email is the ideal first AI habit

If you've been meaning to build AI into your daily work but haven't found a foothold, email is the right place to start. You already do it every day. The feedback loop is fast. And the stakes on most emails are low enough that a mediocre first draft is still useful.

## Step 1: Draft emails with context, not just a request

The most common AI email mistake is this prompt: "Write me an email to my boss about the project delay."

Claude and ChatGPT are capable enough to produce something from that. But it will be generic, formal, and probably wrong for your specific relationship and situation. The output improves dramatically when you give the model something to work with.

Here's a prompt structure that works:

```
I need to write an email to [recipient and relationship]. The situation is: [2-3 sentences of context]. My goal with this email is [what you want to happen]. The tone should be [professional/direct/warm/etc.]. Draft the email for me, then tell me if there's anything important I might be missing.
```

A real example: "I need to write an email to my project sponsor, who I talk to about once a month. The situation is: we're behind on the Q2 deliverable by about two weeks because a key vendor pushed their timeline. My goal is to flag this proactively before she hears it elsewhere, and propose a revised schedule. The tone should be direct and solutions-focused, not apologetic. Draft the email, then tell me if there's anything I might be missing."

That prompt gives Claude a recipient relationship, a specific situation, a goal, and a tone. The result will be significantly closer to what you'd actually send.

Your job is still to edit. AI is a starting point, not a finisher. Read what it gives you, shape it until it sounds like you, and send the version you'd be proud of.

## Step 2: Summarize long threads before you reply

Here's a use case that saves time before you even write a word: paste a long email thread into Claude and ask it to tell you what's actually going on.

Most email threads are inefficient. People reply to the wrong part of a message, loop in people late, or let a three-sentence question turn into a twelve-email chain. By the time you join or return to a thread, you're re-reading context you shouldn't have to.

Try this prompt:

```
Here is an email thread. Please tell me: (1) what the core question or decision is, (2) where it currently stands, (3) what action, if any, is being asked of me. Keep it to 3-5 sentences.

[paste the thread]
```

You can also ask Claude to pull out specific information: "What did Sarah agree to in this thread?" or "Is there anything unresolved that I need to address?" This is where AI for email writing crosses into something more powerful: using AI as a reading assistant, not just a writing assistant.

According to [Microsoft's New Future of Work Report 2025](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/new-future-of-work-report-2025/), Copilot usage in Microsoft 365 is showing consistent time savings on communication tasks, with one enterprise deploying it seeing a 10% reduction in email time. The gains are real, but they depend on knowing how to prompt. Generic summarization requests give generic summaries. Specific questions give useful answers.

## Step 3: Triage your inbox with a clear system

AI for inbox management works differently than drafting or summarizing. You can't paste 200 emails into a chat window. But you can build a simple triage system that makes the decisions faster.

The most useful thing AI does here is help you clarify your own criteria. Most professionals have a vague sense of what email deserves immediate attention, but they never write it down. That means every email becomes a small decision under pressure.

Try this exercise once, outside your inbox:

```
I manage email for [your role]. Help me write a simple triage rule for my inbox. The types of email I get are: [list 4-5 types]. The things that are actually urgent for me are: [list]. What I can batch and respond to at the end of the day: [list]. What I can safely delay or ignore: [list]. Help me turn this into a short decision framework I can keep open next to my inbox.
```

You write this once. Then you use the output as a reference every morning. It sounds simple, and it is. But having your own criteria written down is different from having them in your head. The process of writing the prompt forces clarity you didn't know you were missing.

For built-in AI inbox tools: if you're using Gmail, Google's Gemini features include summary cards that automatically surface the key point of long threads. In Outlook, Microsoft Copilot can summarize thread context before you reply. These work best as starting points for your own reading, not as replacements for it.

## Step 4: Write templates for the emails you send every week

If you find yourself writing variations of the same email repeatedly, that's a clear signal to build a reusable template with Claude.

Some examples: weekly status updates to your manager, meeting follow-up notes, project kickoff messages, request-for-information emails to vendors, check-ins with your team.

The prompt:

```
I write this type of email about once a week: [describe the type]. The audience is usually [recipient type]. The goal is always [purpose]. Can you write a template with [X] blanks I can fill in each time? Make it sound like a real person wrote it, not a form letter.
```

Once you have a template you like, keep it somewhere accessible. Google Docs, a Notion page, a text file. You'll be surprised how fast you can process recurring email when you're starting from a solid template instead of a blank page.

## Common mistakes with AI for email

**Sending the first draft.** AI generates. You edit. Every time. The first draft from Claude or ChatGPT is a starting point. Reading it back will almost always reveal something that sounds off, too formal, too long, or missing a nuance only you know. That editing is not extra work. It's the work.

**Asking for tone without showing an example.** "Make this more casual" is hard for AI to act on without knowing what casual means to you. If you have a past email you think struck the right tone, paste it in and say: "Write the new email in a similar style to this example." You'll get much closer results.

**Using AI for high-stakes emails without review.** For an email to your skip-level manager, a difficult conversation with a report, or anything that could affect a relationship, don't just clean up the AI draft. Write the core yourself, then use AI to review and sharpen it. The judgment layer on important communication should stay with you.

**Expecting AI to know your relationships.** Claude and ChatGPT have no idea who your boss is, what your history with a vendor looks like, or what language your team uses internally. The more relationship context you give, the better. Think of it as briefing a capable temp on your team dynamics.

## From the practice: what shifts when email stops being friction

One thing that comes up regularly in our community sessions is how much cognitive energy professionals spend on email they haven't started yet. The draft sitting in your head while you're in a meeting, the reply you're mentally composing while you're trying to focus on something else.

Using AI for email writing doesn't just save time on the email itself. It reduces the mental load of having emails pending. When you know you can sit down, give Claude a paragraph of context, and have a solid draft in 90 seconds, you stop carrying those drafts around in the background.

That shift, from email as something you brace for to email as something you process efficiently, is what professionals who've built this habit describe most often. It's not about the words on the screen. It's about getting the drafts out of your head.

## Where to start today

Pick one email you need to write in the next hour. Something real, not a test. Give Claude the full context: who you're writing to, what the situation is, what you want to happen, what tone fits. Read what comes back. Edit until it sounds like you. Send it.

That's it. One email. The point is not to get it perfect. The point is to build the habit of giving AI enough context to actually help.
