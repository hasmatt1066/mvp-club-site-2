Pick a task. Grammarly, Microsoft Copilot, ChatGPT, Claude: you probably have access to at least three of them right now. The real question is which one to open.

This guide tells you what each tool is actually good at, where each one falls short, and what we use at MVP Club when we're writing anything from a client email to a full proposal. We have opinions. We'll share them.

## The quick verdict (if you're in a hurry)

Before the breakdown, here's the table version:

| Tool | Best for | Skip it when |
|------|----------|--------------|
| **Claude** | Long documents, complex instructions, maintaining your voice | You need web search or real-time data |
| **ChatGPT** | Brainstorming, back-and-forth drafting, broader integrations | The writing quality needs to be excellent |
| **Grammarly** | Editing and proofreading, tightening existing text | You need to generate something from scratch |
| **Microsoft Copilot** | Writing inside Office 365 apps you already use | You want high-quality output without editing it |
| **Notion AI** | Summarizing and editing within your Notion workspace | Anything outside Notion |

Now the details.

## Claude: our pick for professional writing

For most professional writing tasks, Claude is the better choice. Its outputs are more structured and more readable than ChatGPT's, it handles long documents better, and it follows complex instructions more reliably. When you give Claude a detailed brief with tone requirements, audience constraints, and format expectations, it tends to follow all of them. ChatGPT sometimes simplifies.

The clearest example is when you're writing something where voice matters: a proposal to a skeptical executive, a recommendation memo with a political subtext, or an email that has to thread a needle between directness and diplomacy. Claude's outputs require less editing to sound like you actually wrote them.

Matt Hastings, one of MVP Club's co-founders, describes how this shows up in practice: "There's folks like me who have now become comfortable just doing everything through Claude Code. Whether I'm making a PowerPoint, rewriting an email, running an app, I'm just in Claude all day."

The practical limitation: Claude doesn't browse the web in real-time (meaning it can't pull in live search results or check current news while you're working). If you need to write something that requires current information (a blog post about last week's product announcement, a competitive summary that includes recent news), you'll need to paste that information in yourself or switch to a tool with live search.

**Best Claude uses for writing:**
- Executive summaries from your own documents and notes
- Recommendation memos and proposals where you supply the arguments
- Editing drafts for tone and clarity (paste it in, describe what's wrong, iterate)
- Emails that need to be precise without being cold
- Any document over 1,000 words where consistency across sections matters

Claude Pro is $20/month. If you write more than a few substantial documents per week, it's worth the cost.

## ChatGPT: the versatile workhorse

ChatGPT is where most people start, and for good reason. It's been around longer, has a larger ecosystem of integrations, and the free tier is genuinely capable. For professional writing, it's a strong tool, just not always the best one.

Where ChatGPT pulls ahead: brainstorming sessions where you want to generate a lot of ideas quickly, situations where you need to search the web as part of your research, and workflows that connect to other tools (ChatGPT has broader third-party integrations than Claude currently does). ChatGPT's Canvas feature is also useful for back-and-forth document editing, where you can see your document alongside the chat and make revisions iteratively.

Where it falls behind for writing: the output style tends toward the formulaic. The structure is usually solid (intro, three points, conclusion), but the prose can read as smooth and generic. Experienced readers notice. The editing step to make it sound like you rather than like a machine is often a bit more work with ChatGPT than with Claude.

## Grammarly: the editor, not the writer

Most professionals reach for Grammarly before they understand what it's actually built for. Grammarly is an editor, not a writer. It's designed to improve what you've already written, not generate from scratch. Once you know that, you know exactly when to open it.

Grammarly catches the things you stop noticing after you've read your own draft twice: run-on sentences, passive voice that's slowing down your point, punctuation errors, words used slightly wrong. The grammar detection is genuinely good for that specific job.

The Grammarly AI writing features (the ones that generate and rewrite text, not just flag errors) are less impressive. They work, but they produce the kind of smoothed-out, slightly bland output that comes from a tool optimized for correctness over voice. For generating new text, Claude or ChatGPT will give you better results.

Use Grammarly as a final pass before anything important goes out. Use it embedded in your browser for emails, LinkedIn posts, and anything else where you're typing directly into a web form. Don't use it to write your report from scratch.

Grammarly's free tier is good enough for most professional use. The Pro plan ($12/month or so) adds more style suggestions and tone adjustments. It's a reasonable add-on if you're doing a lot of writing, but it's not a replacement for a generation tool.

## Microsoft Copilot: the one you might already have

If your organization uses Microsoft 365, you may already have access to Copilot. It's embedded directly into Word, Outlook, Teams, and PowerPoint, and that integration is its main advantage over everything else on this list.

What Copilot does well: generating a first draft inside a Word document without switching tabs, summarizing a long email thread in Outlook before you respond, suggesting meeting notes from a Teams transcript. The convenience of having it inside the tools you already use is real. You don't have to copy-paste context between apps.

What it does less well: the writing quality. Copilot outputs in Word tend toward the generic. They're structured and complete, but they require significant editing to sound like a human wrote them. In our experience, Claude produces professional reports that need less cleanup before they're ready to send, while Copilot trades quality for convenience.

The cost varies. Copilot is bundled into some Microsoft 365 plans; the premium Copilot features (the ones embedded in Office apps) often require an additional per-seat license. Check with your IT team what you actually have access to before paying for a separate subscription.

If you're already in Office apps all day and just need faster first drafts, Copilot is the path of least resistance. If writing quality is important, use Copilot to get something started and then bring it into Claude for a proper pass.

## Notion AI: useful, but only if you live in Notion

Notion AI is a $10/month add-on to your Notion subscription. It does two things well: summarizing long Notion pages and databases, and generating content within the Notion structure (turning a bulleted list into a paragraph, expanding a sparse outline into a draft section).

If your team runs on Notion and you store meeting notes, project briefs, or research in it, Notion AI is worth considering. The ability to say "summarize this 20-page project spec" and get a one-paragraph answer, without leaving the tool you're already in, is genuinely useful.

Outside of Notion, it's not the right tool. For standalone writing tasks, Claude or ChatGPT will outperform it.

## What we use at MVP Club and why

For professional writing, Claude handles almost everything that matters. When we need live search results or want to generate a lot of ideas fast, we switch to ChatGPT. Before anything goes to a client or gets published, Grammarly gets a pass. That's it.

The editing instinct matters more than tool selection. Brian Lightfoot, an MVP Club member who coaches executives, put it this way: "When I use these tools, some things just sound a little bit more like AI. So I'm conscious: does this sound like AI?" That consciousness is what separates professionals who get good outputs from those who send the machine's draft and hope nobody notices.

That awareness, knowing when the output sounds right and when it still sounds like a machine, is the actual skill. The tool is almost secondary. Claude produces less of that correction work for professional writing in our experience, which is why it's our default. But we've met plenty of professionals who get excellent results from ChatGPT because they've learned to edit its outputs well.

## The writing workflow that actually works

Here's how to combine these tools:

1. **Gather your notes and context** before opening anything. The quality of AI writing output is almost entirely determined by what you give it.
2. **Draft with Claude or ChatGPT** using a specific directive: audience, format, key arguments, tone. Not "write me an email" but "write a 200-word email to my VP declining this meeting request, in a direct tone, and offer two alternative ways I can support this project."
3. **Edit for your voice.** Read every sentence. Add specifics only you know. Cut what sounds generic.
4. **Run Grammarly (or read it aloud)** before it goes out. Both methods catch different things.

That's the stack. It's not complicated. Do it a few times and it becomes muscle memory: brief, draft, edit, check.
