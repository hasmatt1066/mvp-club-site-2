Most people using AI at work are doing it quietly. They've figured out a Claude workflow that saves them an hour a week. They're using ChatGPT to cut their meeting prep in half. And then they say nothing about it. Not in their one-on-ones. Not in their performance reviews. Not on LinkedIn.

That silence is the actual career problem.

According to a [PwC Global AI Jobs Barometer](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/news-room/press-releases/2025/ai-linked-to-a-fourfold-increase-in-productivity-growth.html), workers in AI-exposed roles who actively develop and demonstrate AI skills earn 56% more than peers in the same roles who don't. [NEEDS CITATION: verify exact figure in linked document] The wage premium for AI capability has more than doubled in the past two years. The work isn't the issue. The packaging is.

## You're probably further along than you think

When I ran a workshop for a women's AI meetup group in March 2026, I asked participants to self-audit where they fell on a four-zone spectrum: Hidden (using AI but telling no one), Aware (knows it's an advantage, no language for it yet), Emerging (starting to name and share it), and Positioned (actively using AI capability in visibility moments).

Almost everyone landed in Zone 1 or Zone 2. Not because they weren't doing real things with AI. Because they hadn't made a habit of naming what they were doing.

The talk track I gave that day: "You're not behind. You're just not packaging it yet."

That distinction matters, because the skills conversation keeps getting framed as a technical one. Do you know prompt engineering? Have you taken any AI courses? Can you code? And for a lot of professionals (myself included, with zero technical background when I started), that framing makes the whole thing feel like a test you haven't studied for.

The AI skills that actually advance careers are behavioral, not technical.

## What employers are actually measuring

"AI skills" as a job requirement has grown 70% year over year in US job postings. [NEEDS CITATION] But the skills hiring managers are citing most often aren't about model architecture or API integration. They're about judgment, workflow application, and the ability to manage AI as a collaborative tool.

When I coach professionals through this, I tell them to stop thinking about AI as something to "learn" and start thinking about it as something to demonstrate. The three things that show up again and again in career conversations:

**1. Daily use, not occasional use.** The difference between someone who "uses AI" and someone who has built real capability is repetition. Using Claude once a week is exploration. Using it every day is a skill. Employers can feel the difference in how you talk about it. You either have actual examples or you don't.

**2. Applied to a specific workflow, not just writing.** Anyone can use ChatGPT to make an email sound better. What creates career signal is using AI to redesign a workflow: turning a 45-minute weekly report into a 10-minute process, speeding up data prep before a presentation, building a client intake system that used to live in your head. Those specific use cases are the stories that stick in a hiring conversation.

**3. Willingness to share it.** The professionals who move fastest in the AI era are the ones who show their work before they feel ready. A screen share with a colleague. A Slack message with a screenshot. A mention in a team standup. Visibility compounds in ways that staying quiet never does.

## The packaging gap

In that same Women in AI workshop, I watched one participant realize she had been using Claude to synthesize competitive research every week for three months. She'd never mentioned it to her manager. Never framed it as a skill on her LinkedIn. In her mind, it was just "how she worked now."

That's Zone 1: doing real work, telling no one.

The exercise that shifts this is simple: write down three things you've done with AI in the last month that made your work genuinely better. Not three things you tried. Three things that worked. Then ask yourself: does my manager know about any of these? Does my network?

For most people, the answer is no. Not because the wins aren't real, but because they haven't built a habit of naming them.

The habit sounds like this, in a one-on-one: "I've been using Claude to help with status reporting. I want to show you what I built." In a performance review: "One thing I've done this quarter to increase efficiency is..." In a LinkedIn update: a simple before-and-after of a deliverable.

None of that requires a course. It requires practice and the willingness to make your work visible.

## The three skills worth building (in order)

If you're thinking about where to actually focus, here's how I frame it with the professionals I coach.

### Daily use habit (start here)

Before any of the career-positioning work matters, you need a consistent practice. Not a weekly check-in. A daily one. Pick one task you do every day that involves writing, analysis, or research, and bring Claude or ChatGPT into it. Do that for 30 days.

This sounds too simple to be the advice. It's the whole advice. Understanding comes through doing, and nothing you read about AI (including this article) substitutes for the hour you spend iterating with a real tool on a real problem.

If you're not sure where to start, [How to Build AI into Your Daily Workflow](/learn/ai-daily-workflow) has a specific 30-minute structure you can follow starting tomorrow.

### Workflow redesign (where the career signal lives)

Once you have a daily habit, the next move is to take one recurring task from "I use AI to help with this" to "I've redesigned this with AI." The difference is specificity and repeatability.

Using Claude to help write something is fine. Building a repeatable system where you paste in raw notes and get a formatted, on-brand deliverable in five minutes is a workflow. Workflows are what you put in performance reviews. Workflows are what you demo for colleagues. Workflows are what interviewers ask you to describe.

T Gaines, who works in tech education and was part of the MVP Club portfolio workshop in early March 2026, built a complete GitHub portfolio page in two hours during a Getting Unstuck Friday session. Before the session, she didn't know portfolios existed as a concept for non-technical professionals. After: "They're asking for a portfolio, not just my repos. Here's what I'm sharing with people: a profile README, a portfolio repo, and then my resume links to it." [NEEDS VERIFICATION: confirm community member permission]

That two-hour session changed how she described herself to employers. Not because she learned something abstract. Because she built something specific and made it visible.

### Packaging and communication (the last mile)

The third skill is translating what you're doing into language that lands in a professional context. This is where a lot of people get stuck (not because they don't have wins, but because "I've been using AI" doesn't communicate anything useful).

Concrete packaging sounds like:

- "I built a Claude workflow that takes client intake notes and generates a first-draft proposal brief. It's cut our turnaround time from 48 hours to same-day."
- "I've been using ChatGPT for competitive research synthesis. What used to take me three hours I can now do in 45 minutes, with better source organization."
- "I automated our weekly status report format with Claude. I paste in my notes, it structures the output to match the template our director prefers."

Notice what those have in common: a specific task, a specific tool, a specific before-and-after. That's the format. Vague claims ("I'm good with AI") land differently than grounded ones.

> "There's something really interesting about telling a story that when somebody sees it, it's just compelling on its own. It's like, oh, I need this person. I don't know their title or what specifically they're going to do, but I need them to do this for me." — Matt Hastings, MVP Club co-founder

That quote was about portfolio building, but it applies here too. The goal isn't to have the AI skill. It's to tell the story of what you built with it in a way that makes someone say: I need that person.

## What doesn't work (and why)

A few patterns I see in coaching that feel productive but don't actually move careers forward.

**Collecting AI knowledge without applying it.** There are a lot of people who have read extensively about large language models, attended webinars, and could describe the difference between Claude and ChatGPT in detail. But they haven't built a single workflow that changed how they work. Employers don't hire AI readers. They hire AI users.

**Waiting until you're an expert to say anything.** The identity question ("Am I really qualified to call myself AI-capable?") is the biggest invisible barrier I see. Yes. You are. If you've built even one workflow that consistently saves you time, you are ahead of most of the workforce. The hesitation is real, but it isn't informative.

**Focusing on the wrong skills.** Prompt engineering as a standalone skill has largely commoditized. What employers want is demonstrated judgment: knowing when AI output needs revision, how to structure a task so the output is useful, and how to build AI into a workflow someone else could follow. That's not technical. That's managerial. (And the managerial frame is actually the more accurate one: using AI is about managing inputs, goals, and outputs, not about mastering a tool.)

## A practical four-week arc

Week 1: Identify the one daily task where you're already using AI (or could start). Do it every day this week. Don't skip.

Week 2: Write down your three best AI wins from the past month. Be specific. Then share one of them with a colleague or your manager. Informally. "Hey, I've been doing something with Claude you might find interesting."

Week 3: Build your packaging language. Draft two to three sentences that describe what you're doing with AI, in concrete before-and-after terms. This is your answer to "what's your AI experience?" when someone asks.

Week 4: Make it visible somewhere. Update your LinkedIn summary. Mention it in a review conversation. Write a brief LinkedIn post about a workflow win. You don't have to have all the answers. You just have to be the person who's paying attention.
