Your CRM is full of leads you haven't touched in three weeks, your pipeline review is tomorrow, and you just got a request for a proposal that needs to go out by Friday. This is Tuesday.

If that scenario feels familiar, you're in good company. Sales is one of the highest-volume, highest-repetition white-collar jobs out there: constant outreach, constant personalization, constant follow-up. And it's exactly the kind of work where AI tools like Claude and ChatGPT can buy back real hours, if you know where to apply them.

This guide walks through the three highest-impact places AI fits into a modern sales workflow: prospecting and research, proposals and presentations, and follow-up sequences. Plus a tool recommendation, a five-day starter plan, and answers to the questions we hear most from sales professionals in our community.

## Where AI Actually Fits in Sales

Before getting into specifics, it's worth naming what AI does well in a sales context and what it doesn't. This sets realistic expectations and helps you spend time where the return is real.

**Where AI earns its keep:**

- Researching a prospect and their company before an outreach call
- Drafting the first version of any written deliverable (emails, proposals, decks, follow-up notes)
- Synthesizing information from multiple sources (earnings reports, LinkedIn, news) into a coherent profile
- Generating variations on outreach copy so you're not sending the same message to 50 people
- Turning meeting notes into action items and next-step emails while the conversation is still fresh

**Where AI can't replace you:**

- Reading the room during a live call
- Building real trust and rapport with a buyer
- Knowing which objections are real versus smoke screens
- Making the judgment call on whether to push or give space
- Anything that depends on your institutional knowledge of the account

The pattern: AI handles the setup work. You do the selling. That division of labor is where the time savings actually happen.

## The Three Highest-Impact Use Cases

### 1. Prospect Research (The Quickest Win)

This is the use case that gets the fastest "oh" moment from sales professionals who try it. Pre-call research used to mean 20-30 minutes per prospect: their LinkedIn, their company website, recent news, Glassdoor for culture context, maybe an earnings call transcript if they're public. Now that work takes closer to five minutes.

The key is giving Claude enough context to do real research synthesis, not just a Google summary. A prompt that works:

> "I'm preparing for a discovery call with [Name], VP of Operations at [Company]. Before the call, I want to understand: what does the company do and who is their buyer, what recent news or announcements might affect their priorities right now, what operational challenges are common in their industry, and what kind of language do operations leaders in this space typically use when talking about their problems? I'll be selling [your product/service type]. Help me build a call prep profile."

What you get back is a starting point. You'll edit it, add what you know from the account history, and cross-reference against what's actually in your CRM. But instead of starting from scratch, you're reacting and refining. That's consistently faster.

### 2. Proposals and Presentations

Proposals are where sales professionals lose the most time to the blank page. Most proposal templates have a structure everyone knows, and a lot of the language is similar from deal to deal. The specific parts, the ones that actually win business, are the sections that speak directly to what this particular buyer cares about.

Claude and ChatGPT are genuinely useful here, but only if you give them the right inputs. The common mistake is pasting in your generic proposal template and asking AI to fill it in. That produces generic output. What works better:

1. Start with your discovery call notes (or a rough dump of what you know about the buyer's situation)
2. Add context about their stated priorities and any objections they raised
3. Specify the sections you need
4. Ask Claude to draft each section with the buyer's language and priorities woven in

The output will still need your editing. Proposal language often needs to match your company's voice, hit specific terms from the RFP if there is one, and reflect the nuances of your relationship with the buyer. But a first draft that reflects the buyer's actual situation beats a blank page by an order of magnitude.

For slide-based proposals, the same principle applies. Give Claude a structure and the key points you want to make per slide. Ask it to write slide headlines as complete sentences (not fragments), with one-paragraph speaker notes. Then import the structure into PowerPoint or Google Slides and build from there.

### 3. Follow-Up Sequences

The research on this is consistent: most deals require five to eight touches before a buyer moves forward, and most sales reps give up after two. The gap isn't motivation. It's time and creative energy. Writing the fourth follow-up email after someone has gone quiet is genuinely hard, and most salespeople either send something generic or don't send anything at all.

This is where AI makes a structural difference. You can give Claude the context of a stalled deal and ask it to generate a sequence of three to five follow-up emails, each with a different angle. It might come back with: a value reminder, a relevant case study hook, a check-in that leads with their specific situation, a low-friction "just closing the loop" note, and a soft break-up email.

You review, pick the ones that fit, adjust the tone so it sounds like you, and schedule them. What used to take 45 minutes of dreaded blank-page time now takes about 15 minutes. And critically, you're more likely to actually send them.

A prompt that works for generating follow-up sequences:

> "I'm following up with a prospect who went quiet after our second call. Here's what I know about their situation: [context]. Here's where we left things: [last touchpoint]. Help me write a sequence of four follow-up emails, each taking a different angle. Keep them short (under 100 words), direct, and low-pressure. They should sound like a peer, not a salesperson."

## Tool Recommendations: Start Here

For sales work, **Claude** is the better starting point for most use cases. A few reasons:

Claude's output tends to read more naturally, with less of the corporate-formal register that makes email outreach feel generic. It handles longer documents well, which matters when you're giving it a prospect's annual report or a 20-page RFP to synthesize. And its instruction-following is strong enough that if you tell it to keep an email under 80 words and avoid the word "excited," it actually does.

**ChatGPT** is also solid, particularly if you're already using it for other work and don't want to switch tools mid-day. The quality difference for most sales tasks is not significant enough to change workflows.

The paid tier ($20/month for either) is worth it for sales use. The free versions hit rate limits at the worst times, and the reasoning quality is noticeably better on the paid plans. Think of it as the same cost as one lunch with a prospect.

One practical note: do not paste confidential prospect information or internal deal data into any AI tool without checking your company's data policy first. Many companies have guidance on what can and can't go into external AI tools.

## What Sales Professionals in Our Community Are Building

The members of MVP Club in sales roles have found that the learning curve is short. Most people have a workflow that's saving them real time within the first week. A few patterns we're seeing:

**The "meeting brain dump" workflow:** Right after a call, dictate or type a rough dump of what happened into Claude. Ask it to extract: key pain points mentioned, objections raised, next steps agreed to, and a draft follow-up email to send within the hour. The whole thing takes five minutes, and the email lands while the conversation is still fresh.

**The personalized outreach template library:** Instead of sending the same email to different prospects, build five to six outreach frames in Claude that vary by buyer persona or industry. Then for each new prospect, give Claude the frame and the specific company context and ask it to personalize it. You get consistent structure with genuine personalization, faster than doing it from scratch each time.

**The RFP first-pass:** When a long RFP lands, paste relevant sections into Claude and ask it to flag: what they're actually asking for, what language from the RFP you should use in your response, and what they seem to value most based on how they've weighted sections. Use that analysis to prioritize where to spend your proposal-writing energy.

> "Anything you do more than once, write down how to do it, and then tell Claude, here's how I want you to do it. Just go." — Matt Hastings, MVP Club

That principle applies directly to sales workflows. Your best outreach email ever, your most effective follow-up sequence, the way you structure a discovery debrief: document those patterns and give them to Claude as context. You turn a generic tool into one that knows how you work.

## The Career Angle: Why This Matters Beyond Productivity

Getting good at AI-assisted sales work positions you differently inside a team or an organization. Not because you're "doing AI," but because you're doing more, doing it faster, and doing it with less visible strain.

The sales professionals who figure this out early tend to show up to pipeline reviews with better-researched accounts, send more proposals, follow up more consistently, and still have energy at the end of the week. Those patterns are visible to managers and to clients.

If you're in a quota-carrying role, AI capability can translate directly to more pipeline activity and, over time, more closed business. If you're in a relationship-selling role where the pressure is on deepening a few high-value accounts, it buys back the hours you'd spend on administrative work and lets you put more time into the calls and conversations that actually move things forward.

The [AI learning path](/learn/ai-learning-path) we recommend starts with your actual work, not with AI concepts. For sales professionals, that means picking one of the three use cases above, trying it on a real deal, and seeing what happens. You're not transforming your process overnight. You're adding one tool to one step and finding out if it saves you time.

## Your First Week With AI in Sales (5-Day Micro-Practice)

If you want a structured on-ramp, here's a five-day plan that builds the habit without overhauling your workflow.

**Day 1 (Monday): Research a prospect you already know**

Pick a prospect you're already working. Before your next touchpoint, run them through Claude using the prospect research prompt above. Compare what Claude gives you to what you already knew. Where was it useful? Where did it miss things only you would know? This calibration exercise helps you understand the tool's limits from the start.

**Day 2 (Tuesday): Turn your last meeting notes into a follow-up**

Find your notes from a recent call. Paste the rough version into Claude and ask it to extract: three key points the buyer made, any implied objections or concerns, and a draft follow-up email to send within 24 hours. Edit until it sounds like you, then send it. Note the time it took compared to writing from scratch.

**Day 3 (Wednesday): Write a cold outreach variation**

Take one of your current outreach emails and give it to Claude with this prompt: "This is my current outreach email. I want you to write three variations, each with a different angle: one leading with the business problem, one leading with a result we've helped similar companies get, and one that's shorter and more direct. Keep all three under 100 words." Pick the one you'd actually send and test it.

**Day 4 (Thursday): Draft a follow-up for a stalled deal**

Go into your pipeline and find a deal that's been stuck for two weeks or more. Give Claude the context and ask for a four-email follow-up sequence with different angles. You won't use all four, but having options removes the blank-page paralysis that usually kills follow-up consistency.

**Day 5 (Friday): Reflect and pick one workflow to keep**

Look at the week. Which of the four use cases saved you the most time or produced the most useful output? That's the one to build into your standard process next week. Don't try to adopt all four at once. One well-executed workflow change compounds over time. Four half-adopted ones don't.

## Common Questions from Sales Professionals

**Will my prospects be able to tell that AI wrote my emails?**

If you send what Claude gives you without editing, possibly. AI output has patterns that some people notice. The fix is simple: read everything out loud before you send it. If it doesn't sound like how you talk, edit it until it does. AI gives you a draft. You make it yours.

**I'm in enterprise sales with long cycles. Is this still relevant?**

Yes, maybe more so. Long-cycle sales involve more written communication (proposal documents, executive summaries, business cases), longer periods of follow-up, and more complex prospect research. All of those benefit from AI assistance. The personalization and human judgment still come from you. AI just handles the first draft faster.

**My company has restrictions on using external AI tools. What should I do?**

Check your policy before you start, not after. Many companies are developing guidance on acceptable AI use, and some have enterprise agreements with specific tools. If your company has Microsoft Copilot through an enterprise license, that may be the appropriate tool for work that touches client data. For research and writing that doesn't involve confidential information, external tools are usually fine, but verify first.

**What if I try this and the output is bad?**

It will be, sometimes. That's normal. The output quality is directly related to the quality of the context you give it. [Understanding what AI can and can't do](/learn/what-ai-can-and-cant-do) helps set the right expectations. When the output is bad, don't write off the tool. Write a better prompt, add more context, and try again. The iteration skill is more important than the prompting skill.
