AI pilots stall because the pitch landed wrong, not because the budget wasn't there.

Someone who's been quietly using Claude every day for three months finally gets a meeting with their manager to propose a team-wide experiment. They come in talking about large language models, automation potential, and productivity multipliers. The manager nods politely, says "that sounds interesting, let me think about it," and nothing happens.

The tool wasn't the problem. The framing was.

Before we get into how to fix that: pitching something you care about to people with power over your work is genuinely nerve-wracking. You don't have to feel confident to do this well. You just have to be prepared.

## What leadership actually needs to hear

When you pitch an AI pilot to a leader, they're hearing it through a particular filter: what's the cost, what's the risk, and what's the upside I can explain to my own stakeholders? They're not deciding whether AI is a good idea in the abstract. They're deciding whether *this proposal*, from *this person*, is worth putting time and political capital behind.

Most pitches lose before they start because they answer a different question. They make the case for AI as a category (interesting, growing, important) rather than the case for a specific thing your specific team can try in the next 90 days to produce a specific outcome. Those are very different conversations.

A large share of organizations are still stuck in the gap between individual experimentation and anything that counts as organizational alignment [NEEDS CITATION: verify directly from McKinsey 2025 State of AI report]. Your job in the pitch isn't to evangelize AI. It's to propose a small, scoped, low-risk experiment that answers a question your leadership already cares about.

## Start with the problem, not the solution

The most effective pitch structure is counterintuitive: don't lead with AI at all.

Start with a problem your organization is already paying attention to. Something your manager talks about in team meetings. A process that everyone agrees takes too long or produces inconsistent results. A bottleneck that's showing up in retrospectives or quarterly reviews.

That problem is your opening. "You know how we've been talking about how long our status report cycle takes, and how half of them get ignored anyway? I've been experimenting with a different approach, and I'd love to show you what it looks like."

Now you're in a conversation they already wanted to have. The AI is the mechanism, not the lead.

This matters because of how organizational attention works. Leaders have a shortlist of active problems they're trying to solve. If your pitch connects to something already on that list, it gets real consideration. If it's positioned as something new they should add to the list, it almost never makes it.

## The anatomy of a pitch that gets a yes

Once you've grounded the conversation in a real problem, the pitch itself needs four things. Not a deck. Not a business plan. Four clear answers to questions your leadership will have, in the order they'll have them.

**1. What exactly are we trying?**

Name the specific task or workflow. Not "AI for marketing." Something like: "I want to run a four-week experiment where three people on the team use Claude to draft our weekly client summary emails, and we measure whether we can cut draft time in half and improve client response rates."

Specificity reduces perceived risk. A well-scoped experiment feels fundable. "AI for marketing" feels like a commitment they can't evaluate.

**2. What does success look like?**

Name a number, a behavior, or a milestone. Time saved per person per week. Response rate on a particular type of communication. Number of deliverables completed in a sprint. If you can't name a metric, you haven't scoped it tightly enough yet.

**3. What's the actual cost and risk?**

For most AI pilots with Claude or ChatGPT, the cost is almost offensively small: a $20/month subscription per person, or a few hundred dollars for an API integration if you want something more robust. That number is important to say out loud because leadership often imagines AI experiments as expensive. The moment you name the real figure, the risk calculus changes.

The other risk question is about data: will employees be sharing anything sensitive? This is worth having a clear answer to. For a Claude Pro account, individual conversations aren't used for training by default on paid plans. For anything involving confidential company data, route that question through IT or legal first, and know that this is exactly what [AI workplace policies](/learn/ai-workplace-policies) cover.

**4. What's the timeline to a decision?**

Ask for 30 to 90 days, not indefinitely. "If we run this from May through July, we'll have enough data to know whether it's worth scaling." A defined endpoint makes a pilot feel safe. Leadership can approve a contained experiment they can't approve an open-ended program.

## The one slide that actually moves things

If you do need to show something visual, keep it to one page. More than one page usually means the idea isn't tight yet. That's fine to acknowledge, but tighten it before the meeting.

The one slide has three sections. Current state (what the problem costs you now, in time or money or quality). Proposed pilot (what you want to try, who's involved, for how long). Expected outcome (what success looks like, and what you'll do with the data).

That's it. Everything else is appendix, and appendix material usually means you're not sure your core case is strong enough.

The best proposals I've seen from professionals coaching through this process have one thing in common: they treat leadership like adults who are making a resource allocation decision, not audiences who need to be persuaded that AI is good. The difference in how those conversations land is significant.

## How to handle the most common objections

**"I'm not sure our team is ready for this."**

This objection usually means: I'm worried people won't adopt it, and I don't want to sponsor a thing that fails. The answer is to make adoption part of the pilot design. "That's exactly why I want to start small. Three people, four weeks, structured check-ins. We learn what works for this team before we expand from there."

The framing matters. Keeping it small and scoped signals that you're not asking for a big commitment, just permission to find out.

**"How do we know this is secure?"**

Have the data handling answer ready before they ask. Paid Claude plans (Pro, Team, and Enterprise) don't use your conversations to train models by default. If your organization uses specific tools through enterprise agreements, those have additional protections. If you genuinely don't know the answer for a specific tool, say so and offer to find out before the pilot starts. That answer, delivered calmly, lands better than a stumble.

**"What's the ROI?"**

This is the question most people fear, and it's also the most honest one. The truthful answer is: "We don't know yet, which is why we want to pilot." But you can give a directional estimate. If the pilot saves three people three hours per week for four weeks, that's 36 hours of recovered capacity. At whatever fully-loaded cost your manager uses for that estimate, that's a number you can put in the room. The ROI frame works when it's tied to a problem leadership already cares about. Which brings us back to where this started.

## What to do before you're in the room

The pitch is actually the last step. Before you get there, three things will make it significantly easier.

**Build your own evidence first.** The most credible pitches come from people who've already been using the tool for themselves. If you've been using Claude to draft communications and it's cut your time in half, say that. Your personal experience is evidence. Use it.

I came from a non-technical background and I was skeptical. Once I started using Claude for my own client work, I stopped wondering whether this was worth pitching. I knew.

**Find your allies.** Who else in your organization is already using AI? In MVP Club sessions, we hear this constantly: once one person starts talking about what's working, two or three others come out of the woodwork who've been doing the same thing quietly. Those people are your coalition. A pilot proposal from three people in different functions carries more weight than one from a lone enthusiast.

**Talk to IT early.** Not to get permission before the pitch, but to know the landscape. What tools are approved? What questions will legal or security ask? Having those answers in advance makes you look more prepared and makes the pilot design more realistic. It also surfaces any deal-breakers before you're in front of leadership.

## After the pitch

If the answer is yes: run the pilot with as much rigor as you promised. Document what you're measuring. Check in weekly. Be honest if something isn't working. The worst thing that can happen to a future AI initiative at your organization is a pilot that nobody followed up on.

If the answer is "let's revisit this later": ask what it would take to move forward. Sometimes "later" means "show me a stronger business case." Sometimes it means "get IT's sign-off first." Sometimes it means "I'm not the right person, try this person." All of those are navigable. "Revisit later" is rarely a hard no.

If the answer is no: that's information too. Some organizations aren't ready to run a formal pilot, and trying to force it rarely helps. The more durable path is to keep building your own skills, keep connecting with peers who are doing the same, and let your personal results make the case over time. The professionals who become the AI leads at their organizations are usually not the ones who got a formal pilot approved in their first meeting.

That combination is buildable. It just takes a few weeks of doing the thing before you ask for permission to do the thing together.
