Your company's AI policy is probably one of three things: nonexistent, vague enough to be useless, or so restrictive it was clearly written by someone who has never actually used these tools. And yet people in your organization are using ChatGPT, Claude, and a half-dozen other AI tools every day regardless.

That's not a knock on your colleagues. It's just the reality of where most workplaces are right now. The tools moved faster than the policies.

So what do you actually need to know before you use AI at work?

## What most companies actually mean when they say "use AI carefully"

If your company has any AI guidance at all, it probably says something like: "Employees should exercise caution when using AI tools and avoid sharing confidential information." That sentence sounds reassuring. It doesn't tell you much.

Here's what it's actually pointing at. The free and personal-plan versions of tools like ChatGPT (free and Plus tiers) are designed for individual users. By default, your conversations on those plans can be reviewed and used to improve the model. That's the core risk your company is worried about: an employee pastes a client contract or an employee performance review into ChatGPT on their personal account, and that information potentially ends up somewhere it shouldn't be.

This is a real risk. Research cited by enterprise security firms estimates that 22% of data pasted into AI chatbots by employees includes personally identifiable information, payment data, or other sensitive content, and the majority of that comes from unmanaged personal accounts. The stakes feel abstract until something goes wrong. Then they feel very concrete very fast.

## The actual things you should never put into a personal AI account

Regardless of whether your company has a formal policy, this is the practical list. It applies whether you're using Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or any other general-purpose AI tool on a personal account.

**Don't paste:**
- Client names, contact details, or any personally identifiable information
- Internal financial data that isn't publicly disclosed
- Employee compensation, performance ratings, or HR records
- Unreleased product plans, roadmaps, or proprietary methods
- Legal documents with confidential terms or settlement details
- Passwords, API keys, or system credentials

The workaround is simpler than it sounds: anonymize before you paste. Replace "Acme Corp" with "Client A." Swap the real budget number for a ballpark figure. Describe the situation without naming names. You keep 90% of the value. You eliminate nearly all of the risk.

This isn't about being paranoid. It's the same judgment call you'd make about any external tool. You wouldn't email client data to your personal Gmail. Same logic applies here.

## The plan you're on changes everything

One thing most employees don't know: the version of an AI tool you're using matters enormously for data handling.

For ChatGPT, OpenAI states clearly that Business, Enterprise, and API accounts do not use your conversations to train models. Your data is encrypted at rest and in transit. For free and Plus accounts, you can turn off model training in your settings (Settings > Data Controls > "Improve the model for everyone"), but it's opt-out, not opt-in, which means most people never change it.

Claude has similar tiers. Anthropic's standard and Pro plans include conversations in data handling practices that are different from Claude for Enterprise or Claude Team accounts, where conversations are not used for training by default. [Anthropic's privacy policy](https://www.anthropic.com/privacy) spells out what applies to which plan.

The practical implication: if your company provides access to an enterprise tier of any AI tool, use that one for work. It exists precisely because the data protections are meaningfully different. If your company doesn't provide one, use personal accounts only for non-sensitive tasks, and consider upgrading to a paid tier where you can control the data settings yourself.

## Three common situations and what to do in each

**Situation 1: Your company has no policy at all.**

This is more common than you'd think. A lot of organizations are in a gray zone where people are clearly using AI, nobody's said it's approved, and nobody's said it's forbidden. The practical path here is not to wait for policy before building the skill. Use personal accounts for your own work, stay off company systems until there's clarity, and be intentional about what you paste. The skill compounds whether you're using an enterprise account or not.

The other thing to do: start the conversation. Ask your manager or IT team what the current guidance is. This move almost always backfires on people's concerns that they'll look suspicious. In practice, it reads as thoughtful and proactive.

**Situation 2: Your company has banned or blocked specific tools.**

Some organizations block ChatGPT entirely. If yours does, that decision is worth taking seriously. Using banned tools on company devices creates real exposure for you personally, separate from any data risk.

But a ban on one tool doesn't mean a ban on all of them. Many companies that have blocked public ChatGPT have simultaneously approved Microsoft Copilot (which runs through their existing Microsoft 365 agreement), or Google's Gemini features embedded in Workspace, or a vendor-specific AI tool built into software they already pay for. Check [what AI tools you already have access to](/learn/ai-tools-you-already-have) before assuming your options are limited.

**Situation 3: Your company has a formal policy.**

Read it once, carefully, for two things: what's approved, and what the consequence language says. Most corporate AI policies name approved tools and account tiers, define what counts as sensitive data, and describe what happens if you use AI in a way that causes a breach. Knowing those specifics lets you work confidently inside the policy rather than nervously guessing at the edges.

If something's unclear, ask HR or legal. Asking for clarification is never a bad look.

## How to protect yourself when using AI for real work

Once you understand the risk categories, protecting yourself is mostly common sense with a few practical specifics.

**Use the right account for the task.** Personal account on personal device for personal-use tasks and experimentation. Company-approved account for anything involving real work data, even if it feels low-stakes.

**Anonymize before you paste.** Build this into the habit. Before any paste, spend 30 seconds swapping out proper nouns and specific numbers. If the work is worth doing with AI, it's worth 30 seconds to do it safely.

**Read the output before you use it.** AI tools can misstate facts, fill in gaps with plausible-sounding fiction, or produce something that's accurate in general and wrong in your specific situation. Before sending anything AI helped you write, read it the way you'd read a contractor's first draft: as a starting point, not a finished product.

One member from our Getting Unstuck sessions, Brian Lightfoot, described this habit well: "When I use these tools, some things just sound a little bit more like AI. So I'm conscious: Does this sound like AI?" That question, asked every time before hitting send, is worth the three seconds.

**Keep a paper trail for significant outputs.** If you use AI to help draft something important (a proposal, a policy recommendation, a client-facing document), note that somewhere for your own records. This is less about liability and more about the ability to explain your process if anyone asks. The professionals who build this habit early have an easier time when their organizations formalize AI disclosure expectations later.

## What's changing: the policy landscape is moving fast

If you're in a state like California, Colorado, or Illinois, it's worth knowing that new AI laws are specifically affecting how employers can use AI in employment decisions. Several states now require employers to notify employees when AI is used in performance reviews, hiring, or scheduling decisions, and to maintain the ability for a human to override those decisions.

This matters for you as an employee: you may have more rights around AI-based decisions than you realize. [SHRM's AI Policy Framework](https://www.shrm.org/advocacy/executive-order-impact-zone/ai-policy-framework) has current guidance on what's in effect where.

What's also shifting is company policy. Organizations that had no guidance two years ago are starting to formalize things. If your workplace is drafting or updating an AI policy, this is an ideal moment to be part of that conversation. The people who help shape early AI policies tend to become informal resources for the rest of the organization. That's a career position worth having.

## The bigger picture: using AI well is part of the job now

The best professionals I've coached through this aren't waiting for perfect policy clarity before building the skill. They're learning the tools on tasks where the stakes are low, building their own intuition about what's safe to share, and staying curious about what their organizations are doing. By the time formal policy arrives, they already know how to work within it.

That's the mindset that tends to work. Not "I can't use AI until my company figures this out." More like: "Here's what I know is safe today. I'll keep building from there."
