The proposal that used to take you half a day now takes a morning. Not because AI writes it for you, but because the blank page problem is gone.

Professional writing is one of the highest-leverage places to bring Claude or ChatGPT into your work. Reports, proposals, executive summaries, project briefs, recommendation memos: these are tasks that eat hours every week for most professionals. You were in the meeting. You have the email chain. You know the situation. But assembling it into something readable, structured, and persuasive takes time you often don't have.

This guide covers the practical workflow for using AI on professional documents that eat hours every week across most roles.

## Why professional writing is a strong fit for AI

The blank page is the enemy of professional writing. Most of the delay in writing a report or proposal has nothing to do with not knowing the content. You know the content. You were in the meeting, you have the data, you understand the situation. The delay is about getting started and then shaping what you know into the right structure.

AI is excellent at first drafts. Not because it writes better than you (it often doesn't), but because it gives you something to react to. A mediocre draft you can edit is faster to work with than a blank screen.

A Harvard Business Review study (December 2024) found that professionals using AI writing tools completed writing tasks more than 50% faster, with quality ratings that met or exceeded the unassisted versions. ([Source](https://hbr.org/2024/12/should-you-write-with-gen-ai)) The speed gain is real. But so is the catch: AI-generated text can introduce factual errors and a flat, generic tone that experienced readers notice immediately. Your job isn't to delegate the writing. It's to direct the draft and then make it yours.

## The mental model: think of it as a first-draft machine

Before you open Claude or ChatGPT, the most useful shift is to stop thinking of AI as a writer and start thinking of it as a capable assistant who can produce a rough draft from your notes. The key phrase is "from your notes."

The quality of what you get out is directly tied to the quality of what you put in. If you paste in your raw meeting notes, key decisions, audience context, and the outcome you need the document to achieve, you'll get something useful. If you type "write me a quarterly report," you'll get a generic outline that doesn't know your organization, your stakeholders, or what actually matters this quarter.

The biggest leverage comes from building a repeatable prompt for each document type you write regularly. As Matt Hastings, one of MVP Club's founders, puts it: "Anything you do more than once, write down how to do it, and then tell Claude, here's how I want you to do it. Just go." That's the shift from using AI occasionally to using it efficiently.

[Using AI is a managerial relationship](/learn/how-to-use-ai-at-work): you provide the inputs, goals, and context, and you evaluate and shape the outputs. Professional writing is one of the clearest examples of that dynamic in action.

## Step 1: Gather your raw materials before you open Claude

The single most common mistake people make with AI writing is starting from nothing. They open Claude, type a vague request, and then wonder why the output is generic.

Before you prompt anything, collect:

- Your notes, meeting summaries, or source data
- The audience for the document (their role, their priorities, their objections)
- The outcome you want (decision, buy-in, information, action)
- The format expected (memo, deck, report, proposal, email)
- Any constraints (length, tone, confidentiality)

This takes five minutes and changes everything. When you paste that context into Claude, the output shifts from a generic template to something that reflects your actual situation.

## Step 2: Write a directive prompt, not a request

Most people ask AI to write things. What works better is directing a draft.

A request sounds like: "Write me a proposal for a new vendor."

A directive sounds like: "I need to write a proposal recommending we switch our project management software from Asana to Monday.com. The audience is our VP of Operations, who is skeptical about switching costs. My main arguments are: (1) we've had persistent sync issues with our current stack for six months, (2) Monday.com integrates directly with Salesforce, which would save my team about 3 hours a week, and (3) the price difference is $200/month. Please draft a 1-page recommendation memo in a direct, professional tone."

That second prompt gives Claude a decision to support, a skeptical audience to address, specific evidence to work with, and format constraints to follow. The draft it produces will be genuinely useful as a starting point.

## Step 3: Edit for your voice, add your judgment

The draft Claude produces will be structurally sound but will probably sound a little flat. That's expected. AI writing tends toward the smooth and the generic.

This is where your expertise comes in. Read through the draft and:

- Add specifics only you know (names, dates, dollar amounts, the history of the issue)
- Cut anything that sounds like it was written for anyone rather than for your reader
- Rewrite sentences that don't sound like you
- Add the judgment calls AI can't make ("what I didn't include here is the political context around our CFO's concerns")

The need for this editing work shows up clearly in how people describe using these tools. Brian Lightfoot, an MVP Club member who coaches executives, puts it this way: "When I use these tools, some things just sound a little bit more like AI. So I'm conscious: does this sound like AI?" That consciousness is the editing instinct you want to develop. The goal is for the final document to sound like you wrote it with a lot of help, not like a machine wrote it and you approved it.

## Step 4: Iterate in the same conversation

One of the most common mistakes is treating the first output as the final draft. AI writing tools are designed for back-and-forth. After you get the initial draft:

- "The second paragraph is too long. Tighten it to two sentences."
- "The tone is too formal for this audience. Make it more direct."
- "Add a section on implementation timeline between the cost section and the conclusion."
- "The recommendation at the end is too hedged. Make it clearer that I'm recommending we move forward."

Each of these follow-ups takes 10 seconds. The cumulative effect is a document that's shaped around your actual needs, not a generic template.

## The three document types where AI adds the most value

### Status reports and project updates

Status reports are ideal for AI assistance because they follow a predictable structure (what was accomplished, what's in progress, what's blocked, what's next) and require synthesis of information you already have. The bottleneck is assembly, not thinking. (If you manage projects specifically, the [AI for project managers](/learn/ai-for-project-managers) guide covers this in more depth.)

**The workflow:** At the end of the week, paste your task list, meeting notes, and any relevant updates into Claude. Ask it to structure a status report for your manager or stakeholders, and specify how long the update should be and what format they prefer (bullets vs. paragraphs, formal vs. casual).

Carmella Thompson, a real estate agent in our community (from an MVP Club session), built a version of this for her market reports. She automated the process of pulling weekly market data into a structured narrative for her clients, cutting the time she spent on it from about an hour to fifteen minutes. The insights were still hers. The assembly was Claude's.

### Recommendation memos and proposals

These documents require you to make a case. The structure is usually: here's the situation, here's what I'm recommending, here's the evidence, here's what it will cost, here's the next step. AI is very good at generating this structure once you give it the arguments.

The critical piece is the evidence. Claude can't know your organization's specific data or political context. You provide that. Claude helps you arrange it into an argument that holds together.

### Executive summaries

The hardest thing about executive summaries is knowing what to cut. You've spent weeks on a project or report, and condensing it to one page feels like losing something.

Ask Claude to read your full document and produce an executive summary focused on the three things a senior decision-maker needs to know. Then read what it gives you and ask yourself: "Did it pick the right three things?" Usually it gets two of them right. Your job is to correct the third and make sure the framing matches what your audience actually cares about.

## Common mistakes with AI writing tools

**Giving no context and expecting good output.** "Write me a quarterly business review" gives Claude nothing to work with. Paste your actual data, notes, and audience context first.

**Accepting AI structure without questioning it.** AI tends toward the same formats: intro, three points, conclusion. That structure might be wrong for your situation. Question whether the format serves your reader before you commit to it.

**Not editing for your voice.** A document that reads as AI-generated is a document that signals you didn't care enough to make it yours. The editing step is not optional. It's where the work becomes yours.

**Using it for high-stakes first drafts without understanding the limitations.** AI can introduce confident-sounding errors, especially when it speculates about data or specifics. Anything that matters should be verified. The tool speeds up drafting; it does not replace your judgment.

## Which AI tool is best for professional writing?

For most professional writing tasks, Claude is the better choice right now. Its outputs tend to be more structured and readable, it handles long documents well, and it follows complex instructions more reliably. For writing tasks specifically, Claude Pro ($20/month) is worth the cost if you're writing more than a few substantial documents per week.

ChatGPT is a strong alternative and many professionals prefer it for interactive back-and-forth on complex proposals. The honest answer is that the best AI tool for writing depends on your specific workflow, and the differences between them matter less than the quality of context and direction you provide.

Both tools have free tiers that are good enough to start with. Try your first report or proposal on the free version and see if it changes how you work. If it does, the paid tier is worth considering.

## From the practice: what we see in coaching

Every week in our community sessions, professionals share what they're building and where they're getting stuck. On writing tasks specifically, a few patterns show up regularly.

The "aha" moment usually isn't about prompting technique. It's the moment someone pastes their actual meeting notes or project data into Claude and realizes it can produce a coherent first draft from material they already have. The tool stops feeling abstract and starts feeling useful.

The second pattern is that people underestimate how much editing the draft needs. The AI draft is not the finished document. Professionals who get the best results treat it as scaffolding: useful for structure, requiring real editorial work to become something worth sending.

The writing workflows that save the most time are the ones built around repeatable prompts: context, audience, and format expectations captured once, then reused every time. Paste the inputs, review the draft, make it yours.

## Where to go from here

For your next step: pick one document you have to write this week. It could be a status update, a project brief, or a recommendation email. Gather your notes, write a directive prompt (not a request), paste in your context, and see what comes back. Edit it until it sounds like you sent it. That's the whole workflow.

Once you've done it once with a low-stakes document, you'll know exactly where AI helps and where your judgment is irreplaceable. That's more useful than any guide.
