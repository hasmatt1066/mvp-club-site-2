## The three highest-impact use cases for consultants

Before diving into specifics, here's the honest picture of where Claude and ChatGPT are actually earning their keep in consulting work right now.

**Research synthesis:** Taking large volumes of source material (industry reports, interview notes, competitive analyses, earnings calls) and pulling out what's relevant to a specific question. This is where AI saves the most time for consultants, because synthesis is time-intensive and doesn't require the kind of judgment your clients are paying for.

**Deliverable drafting:** Getting from "notes and ideas" to "structured document" faster. AI is good at first drafts, outlines, executive summaries, and translating messy thinking into clean structure. The insight still comes from you. The scaffolding comes from Claude.

**Client communication:** Drafting emails, preparing talking points, anticipating objections, and sharpening messaging for different audiences. This is one of the most underused use cases in consulting, and one of the highest-leverage ones.

We'll go deep on each of these. But start with whichever one costs you the most time right now.

## Use case 1: Research synthesis (the fastest win)

A lot of consulting research follows the same basic pattern: gather sources, read everything, pull out what's relevant, synthesize into a point of view. The gathering and reading are yours. The synthesis is where AI can dramatically compress your time.

Here's what this looks like in practice. You're preparing for a client in the healthcare technology space. You have a 60-page industry report, three competitor annual reports, and a handful of analyst articles. You need to understand the competitive field and identify where your client has differentiated positioning.

Instead of reading every page front-to-back, you paste the content into Claude (or upload the documents) and ask a specific question: "Read this material and identify the three most significant competitive pressures facing mid-sized health tech companies right now, with evidence from the sources."

What comes back is not a replacement for your analysis. It's a starting point that would have taken you two hours to produce manually. You now spend forty-five minutes evaluating and refining Claude's synthesis rather than generating it from scratch.

The critical shift is how you ask. "Summarize this report" gets you a generic summary. "Tell me the three biggest risks to our client's current positioning based on this competitive landscape" gets you something you can actually use in client conversation.

### A prompt worth keeping

For research synthesis, this framing reliably produces useful output:

*"You are helping me prepare for a client engagement in [industry]. Here is the background material: [paste content]. My client's key question is [specific question]. Based only on the material I've provided, identify [specific deliverable: risks, opportunities, gaps, competitive factors]. Flag anything where the evidence is thin or where I should gather more information."*

The last instruction matters: asking Claude to flag weak evidence keeps you honest and keeps the synthesis trustworthy.

## Use case 2: Deliverable drafting

The blank page problem is real in consulting. You have the insight from your analysis. You have the client's situation in your head. You know what you want to say. Getting it onto the page in a structure that works for a senior audience is a separate skill that takes time even when the thinking is done.

This is where Claude earns its $20/month.

The approach that works: instead of asking Claude to "write the executive summary," give it the raw inputs and let it do the structural heavy lifting. Here's what that looks like.

You paste in your notes from a discovery workshop, your analysis of the data, and any specific conclusions you've reached. Then you ask: "Draft an executive summary for a CFO audience. The main finding is [X]. The supporting evidence includes [Y and Z]. The recommended action is [A]. Keep it under 400 words, lead with the finding, and assume the CFO has ten minutes to read this and will forward it to her leadership team."

That last sentence, about the CFO forwarding it, is the kind of audience context that makes the difference between a generic draft and one that's actually useful. The more specific you are about who's reading it and what they'll do with it, the better the draft.

What comes back still needs editing. The language may be too formal, or not formal enough. The structure may not match your firm's conventions. Certain points may be mis-weighted. That's all fixable in twenty minutes of editing. The alternative is starting from a blank page and spending two hours getting to the same place.

### For slide decks

The same approach works for presentation outlines. Give Claude the story you're trying to tell and the audience it's for, and ask for a slide-by-slide outline with one-sentence talking points for each slide. You'll still build the slides yourself (or use your firm's templates), but the outline gives you a structure to push back against rather than a blank canvas to fill.

## Use case 3: Client communication

This is the underrated one. Consulting involves constant written communication with clients: emails that need to strike exactly the right tone, updates that need to be brief without feeling dismissive, meeting follow-ups that capture decisions without being bureaucratic, scope discussions that are firm without being defensive.

Getting this communication right takes time and attention that many consultants underinvest in because they're focused on the analysis work. AI can help close that gap.

Here's an example. You've just finished a discovery workshop. The client asked three questions that your team doesn't have answers to yet. You need to send a same-day follow-up that acknowledges the open questions without making the client nervous, summarizes what was decided, and frames the next two weeks of work.

That email might take you thirty minutes to draft well. You could paste your notes from the workshop into Claude with this prompt: "Draft a follow-up email to a client after a discovery workshop. The tone should be confident and organized. We need to acknowledge three open questions [list them] without signaling that we're behind. We covered the following decisions [list them]. The next steps are [list them]. The audience is the VP of Operations and her chief of staff."

You'll still edit it. But you'll edit it in ten minutes, not write it in thirty.

The same applies to scope conversations, which are among the most delicate communications in consulting. Before you send any email that involves scope, timeline, or budget, you can run your draft through Claude with a simple prompt: "Read this email. Is there any language that could be read as defensive, ambiguous about our commitments, or that might make the client nervous? Suggest specific edits."

That's not outsourcing judgment. That's using a thinking partner to pressure-test your communication before it reaches the client.

## Tool recommendations

For most consulting work, Claude is currently the better choice over ChatGPT. The reasons are practical: Claude handles longer documents better (important for pasting in research material), its default writing style is closer to professional business communication, and it tends to stay on-task with complex, multi-part instructions.

For research synthesis specifically, Claude's ability to process long documents without losing track of the question is noticeably stronger than ChatGPT's free tier.

That said, both tools are worth trying for your specific workflow. Some consultants find ChatGPT's conversational style better for thinking out loud during the analysis phase. If you're already paying for ChatGPT Plus, try it for three tasks before buying a second subscription.

The paid tier is worth it for consulting work. The difference between Claude Pro ($20/month) and the free tier is meaningful when you're pasting in 60-page reports or asking for long-form drafts. Think of it as a monthly retainer for a research assistant who works at midnight and never complains about scope creep.

## The career angle

Here's what this is actually about for consultants, beyond the efficiency gains.

The consultants who are building real AI capability right now are not just doing their current work faster. They're changing what they can promise a client. When research synthesis takes ninety minutes instead of six hours, you can do more synthesis per engagement. When deliverables come together in half the time, you can iterate more. When client communication is sharper, you get fewer follow-up questions and more trust.

That compounds over time. Clients notice when they work with someone who is consistently well-prepared, whose documents are clean and clear, whose communication is timely and precisely calibrated. They don't know you're using Claude. They just know the work is good.

There's also the internal credibility angle. In a lot of consulting firms, the people who are figuring out how to use AI in their practice are becoming the people others come to for advice. That's a career position worth occupying. It doesn't require being technical. It requires being curious and willing to try things.

## Your first week: a five-day micro-practice plan

The fastest way to build AI capability as a consultant is to use it on real client work, not test cases. If you want a broader framework for building the daily habit, the [first week with AI guide](/learn/first-week-with-ai) covers the fundamentals. Here's the consulting-specific version.

**Monday:** Take a research document you already have. Paste it into Claude. Ask one specific question about it (not "summarize this," but a real question relevant to a current engagement). Spend fifteen minutes evaluating and editing the response.

**Tuesday:** Draft one client-facing email with Claude's help. Use the prompt structure above: give it the context, the audience, the tone, and the goal. Compare the draft to what you would have written. What did it get right? What needed fixing?

**Wednesday:** Pull your notes from a recent client meeting. Ask Claude to draft a one-page meeting summary that would make sense to someone who wasn't in the room. Edit it until it's accurate and sounds like you.

**Thursday:** Use Claude as a thinking partner on a current client challenge. Describe the situation, what you know, what you don't know, and where you're stuck. Ask it to generate the three most important questions you should be asking. Push back on the ones that aren't useful.

**Friday:** Reflect. Which task saved you the most time? Which felt most like useful output and which felt like generic output? That reflection tells you where to focus in week two.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I paste client documents into Claude or ChatGPT?**

Check your firm's policies before you do. Most major consulting firms have guidelines about client data and AI tools. Some have enterprise agreements with Anthropic or OpenAI that include data privacy protections. Others prohibit pasting client information into consumer AI products. When in doubt, anonymize the content before pasting: replace client names, remove identifying details, use placeholders for proprietary numbers.

**What if the AI output sounds too polished or corporate?**

Edit it. The most common issue consultants report is that Claude's default register is slightly too formal, or uses phrasing that doesn't match their firm's voice. That's a normal editing task, not a failure of the tool. Read the draft aloud. Every sentence that sounds like a press release needs rewriting. Every sentence that sounds like you can stay.

**How do I know when to trust the synthesis and when to check the sources?**

When it matters, always check the sources. For research synthesis, Claude can miss nuance, misweight evidence, or draw on its general training rather than the document you provided. The prompt "Flag anything where the evidence is thin or where I should gather more information" helps surface the uncertain parts. But for client-facing work, you should be reading the underlying sources on the points that matter most.

**How long does it take to get good at this?**

A week of daily practice. Not weeks. The learning curve for using Claude effectively on consulting work is short because the core skill is giving clear instructions, which is something consultants already know how to do. The prompts in this article are starting points, not final answers. Try them, adjust them for your work, and build from there. If you've hit early frustration with AI not producing useful output, the [common beginner mistakes](/learn/ai-beginner-mistakes) article covers the patterns that derail most people in the first few weeks.
