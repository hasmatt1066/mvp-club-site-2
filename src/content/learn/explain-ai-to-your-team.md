A colleague forwarded you an article about AI. You said, "Yeah, I've been using Claude for a few weeks now, it's actually really useful." And they looked at you like you had just told them you were training for a marathon in Antarctica.

You know the look.

The thing is, you probably can't convince them of anything in that moment. And trying to usually makes it worse.

## The first thing to understand: you're not their teacher

When someone is skeptical about AI, launching into an explanation of large language models or listing off use cases is rarely what they need. Most of the time, they don't have an information problem. They have a permission problem. They're wondering: *Is this worth my time? Am I already behind? Will this make me look foolish?*

Those are not questions that a list of features answers.

Jill's most useful insight from coaching professionals through this: the people who are quickest to adopt new tools aren't the ones who got the best explanation. They're the ones who felt safe enough to try something imperfect. Your job, when you're talking to a skeptic, is to make the idea feel lower-stakes, not to deliver more information.

That reframe changes everything about how you have the conversation.

## How to talk to your skeptical colleague

The classic mistake is starting with the technology. "AI can do X, Y, and Z" positions you as someone selling something, and nobody likes being sold to.

Start with a problem instead.

Think about something they complain about regularly. The weekly status report that takes forever. The mountain of emails before a big meeting. The presentation deck they're always scrambling to finish. Now tell them about a time you had that same problem and what you actually did. Specific beats general, every time.

Something like: "You know how I always dread writing the recap for Monday's sync? I pasted my notes into Claude last week and had a draft in four minutes. It wasn't perfect, but I spent another five minutes editing it and it was done. I don't know why I waited so long to try it."

Notice what that does. It doesn't tell them AI is great. It shows them what it did for a real task they recognize. It admits the output wasn't perfect (which actually builds trust). And it ends with a little self-deprecation, not a sales pitch.

## How to talk to your boss

Talking to your boss about AI requires a different frame. The questions running through their head are usually: *What does this mean for my team? Am I supposed to be doing something about this? What if someone uses it wrong?*

They're less worried about whether it works and more worried about what it means organizationally. So your approach needs to speak to that.

A few things that tend to land well:

**Start with a win you can show, not tell.** If you've used Claude or ChatGPT to speed up a real work task, bring that to the conversation concretely. "I've been using Claude for the past three weeks. I used it to prep for the Anderson account review, and it took about half the time. Thought you'd want to know I'm experimenting with it." That's not asking for permission. It's sharing a result. Big difference.

**Don't suggest a team-wide initiative.** At least not yet. The moment you propose a "team rollout" (especially without a plan), you've just handed your boss a decision that requires IT, legal, and a committee. The [most common mistake organizations make with AI](/blog/4-well-intentioned-ai-approaches-that-miss-the-mark) is treating adoption as a technology decision instead of a behavior change problem. Start with your own practice. Let results speak.

**Answer the "what if someone misuses it" question before they ask it.** You can say: "I'm not suggesting we change anything formally. I just wanted to be transparent that I'm using it for my own work and it's genuinely helping. Happy to share what I've learned if it's useful." That turns a potential concern into an offer.

## How to talk to your team

If you manage people, the conversation is different again. Your team is watching how you engage with AI as a signal of what's expected of them.

If you show up skeptical or dismissive, they'll stay quiet about their own experimentation. If you show up as the enthusiastic evangelist who's insisting everyone "get on board," you'll create anxiety for people who don't know where to start.

The approach that works is somewhere in between: curious, low-pressure, and honest about your own learning curve.

Try running a short "what I'm trying" share in a team meeting. Not a formal presentation. Just two minutes: "I've been messing around with using Claude for writing first drafts of our weekly report. Here's what worked, here's what didn't." Then open it up. "Curious if anyone else has been trying anything."

You'll usually find that someone on your team has already been using AI quietly and just hasn't brought it up. [That's more common than people think.](/blog/what-to-actually-do-about-something-big-is-happening)

Once that person speaks up, everything shifts. The conversation becomes peer-to-peer instead of top-down.

## The questions they'll ask (and honest answers to give)

**"Isn't AI just going to give me wrong information?"**

Yes, sometimes. Claude and ChatGPT can get things wrong, especially for anything that requires current facts or specific data. The right way to use them isn't to trust outputs blindly; it's to treat the output like a first draft from a capable colleague who might have made mistakes. You still review it. You still apply your own judgment. The tool speeds up the work; you're the quality check.

**"Isn't this just going to take people's jobs?"**

This is the question underneath most AI anxiety. An honest answer acknowledges the real uncertainty: no one can predict exactly how work will change. But the professionals who are figuring out how to work well *with* AI tend to be the ones who feel most secure, not least. Not because AI can't change roles, but because they're ahead of the shift instead of behind it. That's a real advantage.

**"I don't have time to learn a new tool."**

This one is actually worth taking seriously instead of dismissing. They're right that most tool adoptions require significant time investment. AI is genuinely different because you can start getting value in the first session. The bar to entry is knowing how to type and having a task. If you have 15 minutes and a real work task, that's enough to start. The [AI time paradox](/blog/ai-time-paradox): using AI is actually what creates more time.

**"I'm not very technical."**

Neither was Jill when she started. Neither were the majority of MVP Club members who now use Claude daily. The tools are built for people who type in plain English and describe what they need. You don't configure anything. You just talk to it.

## One thing worth saying out loud

If you're trying to bring someone along on this, the most useful thing you can do is invite them to try it next to you.

The demystification happens in the doing, not the explaining. That's something the MVP Club founding team learned from running dozens of webinars and coaching sessions with non-technical professionals. As Matt put it during one of our webinars: "The goal is to make one reusable process feel accessible. It's just a chat window doing stuff."

When someone types their first real work task into Claude and gets back something genuinely useful, no explanation was ever needed. The experience is its own argument.

## If you want to practice the conversation itself

Some of the most useful moments in our community sessions happen when people share how they talked about AI with someone who was skeptical. What they said, how it landed, what they'd do differently. Because this is a skill too, not just knowing how to use the tools, but knowing how to bring the people around you along.

If that sounds useful, it's exactly the kind of thing we work through together at [MVP Club](/community). The people who get good at this fastest aren't doing it in isolation.
