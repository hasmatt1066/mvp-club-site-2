Your company is probably paying for AI tools you've never opened. Not because anyone hid them from you, but because nobody told you they were there.

Microsoft 365 now has Copilot built into Word, Outlook, Teams, and Excel. Google Workspace Business and Enterprise plans include Gemini across Gmail, Docs, Slides, and Meet. Adobe Creative Cloud has Firefly baked into Photoshop and Illustrator. Salesforce ships Einstein features with most enterprise licenses. These are not future upgrades. They are already in the software you open every day.

The catch: most employees have no idea. A [BCG survey](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/ai-at-work-momentum-builds-but-gaps-remain) found that despite nearly 90% of knowledge workers using AI at work in some form, only 5% say they're using it to meaningfully change how they work. The tools exist. The gap is in knowing where to look.

## How to find out what you actually have

Before you buy a separate Claude or ChatGPT subscription, spend 20 minutes figuring out what's already activated in your current stack.

The simplest move: open Word or Google Docs and look for an AI icon in the toolbar. In Microsoft 365, it will often be a small Copilot icon near the top right. In Google Docs, look for a "Help me write" option when you click in a blank document. If either appears, the feature is turned on.

For Outlook, look for a Copilot button when composing an email. In Gmail, look for a pencil icon in the compose window labeled "Help me write."

Not sure whether your company has paid for these add-ons? Ask your IT department or your manager. "Are we paying for Microsoft Copilot or Google Gemini?" is a completely reasonable question. Asking it tends to surface information that nobody thought to communicate proactively. In some organizations, licenses have been active for months and no announcement ever went out.

## What Microsoft Copilot can do for you right now

Microsoft 365 Copilot is embedded across the apps most professionals use all day. If your organization has purchased Copilot licenses (separate from a base M365 subscription), here's what you can do without installing anything new.

**In Outlook:** Ask Copilot to summarize a long email thread before you respond. Ask it to draft a reply based on the conversation context. Ask it to suggest a meeting time based on your availability. These are small tasks but they compound quickly when email is eating two hours of your day.

**In Word:** Paste in rough notes and ask Copilot to turn them into a structured document draft. Ask it to rewrite a paragraph in a different tone. Ask it to summarize a long report into three bullet points for an executive audience.

**In Teams meetings:** Copilot can take notes during a live meeting, generate a summary of the key decisions, and list the action items. You don't need to stop and take notes while also trying to follow the conversation. That alone is worth a significant amount of time per week for anyone in back-to-back meetings.

**In Excel:** Describe what you want to see in plain language and Copilot will suggest a formula or chart. "Show me which region had the highest growth last quarter" is the kind of prompt that actually works.

One honest caveat on Copilot: [NEEDS VERIFIED STAT: enterprise AI tool adoption rates from Gartner/Forrester] when you have access to both Copilot and a separate ChatGPT subscription, many people default to ChatGPT. Copilot's strength is its integration with Microsoft 365, not its model quality. Use it for tasks where being inside Word or Outlook is an advantage. For more complex writing and reasoning, Claude or ChatGPT may give you better results.

## What Gemini does inside Google Workspace

As of early 2025, Google included Gemini AI features in Workspace Business and Enterprise plans without requiring a separate add-on. If your company is on one of these plans, you have access to a meaningful set of features across Gmail, Docs, Sheets, Slides, Meet, and Chat.

**In Gmail:** The "Help me write" button drafts an email from your bullet points. Give it the recipient, the context, and the tone you want, and it produces a starting draft in seconds. You can also ask Gemini to summarize a long email thread before you respond, which is useful when someone drops a five-paragraph email in your inbox at 4pm on a Friday.

**In Google Docs:** The Gemini side panel lets you work alongside the document without leaving it. Ask it to rewrite a section, generate an outline from a brief, or summarize the document for a specific audience.

**In Google Meet:** Gemini can generate meeting notes and action items automatically. For teams running on Google Workspace, this replaces the person who always gets stuck writing the recap.

**In Google Sheets:** Describe what you want to analyze and Gemini will suggest formulas and structure. Similar to Copilot in Excel, the value is in not having to remember syntax when you need a quick calculation.

## Adobe Firefly: the one marketers keep missing

If your organization uses Adobe Creative Cloud, you likely have Firefly access built in. Firefly is Adobe's generative AI for images, and it's integrated directly into Photoshop, Illustrator, and Adobe Express.

What makes Firefly different from other image AI tools is that it's built specifically for commercial use. Adobe trained Firefly on licensed stock images and Adobe Stock, which means the outputs are cleared for commercial use in ways that some other generative image tools are not. For marketing teams producing visual assets for clients or campaigns, that distinction matters.

**In Photoshop:** Firefly powers the Generative Fill feature. Select an area of an image, describe what you want to add or replace, and Photoshop generates it in context. Removing a distracting background element, extending an image to fit a wider format, or adding an object that wasn't in the original shot are all practical tasks, not demo tricks.

**In Adobe Express:** For people who don't have Photoshop skills, Express gives access to Firefly's image generation and editing through a simpler interface. Marketing coordinators and social media managers can generate on-brand visual variations without waiting on a designer.

If you're on a Creative Cloud plan and you haven't explored Firefly inside Photoshop yet, that's worth fifteen minutes this week. The Generative Fill tool in particular tends to surprise people when they use it for the first time.

## Salesforce Einstein: what's actually included

Salesforce has attached "Einstein" to a range of AI features across its platform, which makes it genuinely confusing to know what you have access to. The short version: basic Einstein features come with Enterprise and Unlimited edition licenses, but the more advanced generative AI capabilities typically require add-ons.

If your company is on Salesforce Enterprise or Unlimited, you likely have access to Einstein Lead Scoring (which predicts which leads are most likely to convert), Case Classification (which routes support cases automatically), and Opportunity Scoring. These aren't flashy features, but for sales and service teams, they're useful and already running in the background.

The more advanced Einstein Copilot and Agentforce features require separate licensing at around $125 per user per month. These are worth asking your Salesforce admin about if your team uses Salesforce heavily, but they're not automatically included.

The practical ask: check with your Salesforce admin what Einstein features are turned on in your org. Basic predictive features may already be active and surfacing recommendations you haven't noticed yet.

## A step-by-step approach for getting started

### Step 1: Audit what you have

This week, open each of the tools your company pays for and look for AI features. Word, Outlook, Teams, Gmail, Docs, Photoshop, Salesforce. Spend five minutes in each one looking for AI buttons, side panels, or "Help me write" prompts. Document what you find.

### Step 2: Pick one task and run it through the built-in tool

What's the most repetitive thing you do each week? Pick that one thing and try it through whatever AI feature is closest to that task. If you write a lot of emails, try Copilot or Gemini for one. If you clean up images regularly, try Firefly's Generative Fill on one image. One task, one tool, one try.

The goal here is a single before-and-after comparison you can evaluate honestly. Did it save time? Was the output usable? That one comparison tells you more than any amount of reading about the feature.

### Step 3: Decide whether the built-in tool is enough

Built-in tools have a real advantage: they're already connected to your workflow and your company's data security policies. If Copilot or Gemini handles 80% of your use case well enough, that's the right call. You don't need a separate subscription for every task.

But built-in tools also have limits. Microsoft Copilot's underlying model is not as strong as Claude or ChatGPT on complex reasoning and nuanced writing. Google Gemini is solid for Workspace-connected tasks but is not yet the default choice for professional writing that needs to sound genuinely thoughtful. If you're hitting walls with the built-in tools, that's when a separate Claude or ChatGPT subscription is worth the $20/month.

### Step 4: Tell one colleague what you found

The fastest way for AI adoption to spread inside a company is peer-to-peer. When you find something that works, tell one colleague. "I found out we have Gemini in Docs and it saved me 20 minutes on the project summary today" is the kind of specific, low-stakes information that actually changes behavior. Not a training presentation. A conversation over Slack.

## What to do when your company hasn't turned these features on

Your company may be paying for Microsoft 365 Copilot licenses but have the feature disabled for most users. This is more common than you might expect: IT and security teams often roll out AI features cautiously while they work through data governance questions.

If you find that AI features aren't available in your tools, the most productive move is to ask directly whether they're being evaluated and when access might expand. Framing the question as "I'd like to help test this and give feedback" tends to land better than "why don't we have this yet." It positions you as a resource rather than a complaint.

## A note on data and privacy inside enterprise tools

One practical advantage of using Copilot, Gemini, or Firefly through your company's enterprise agreements: the data handling is already covered. Microsoft and Google both state clearly in their enterprise terms that they do not use your prompts or outputs to train their models without your organization's permission. Your IT team has already vetted these agreements as part of your software contracts.

That's meaningfully different from using a personal ChatGPT free account on your work computer, where the default settings allow OpenAI to use your conversations for model improvement. If your company has enterprise AI tools available, using them is often the lower-risk path from a data security standpoint, not the higher-risk one.
