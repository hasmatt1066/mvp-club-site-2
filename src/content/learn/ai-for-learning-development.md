Your content request queue has twelve items on it. You have one direct report, a shared contractor you can book four hours a month, and a budget that got cut in January. The SME you need is available for a thirty-minute call next Thursday. The launch date is in three weeks.

This is the reality L&D professionals have been working inside for the better part of a decade. The gap between what organizations need and what learning teams can produce has only gotten wider. AI doesn't close that gap entirely. But it changes the math in ways that matter.

This guide covers how L&D professionals are using Claude and ChatGPT today: the three highest-impact use cases, how to actually start, and what to watch out for.

## The Three Highest-Impact Uses of AI for L&D

Not everything in L&D benefits equally from AI. Some tasks are genuinely transformed. Others are incrementally faster but still require the same human judgment. Here's where L&D professionals in our community are getting the clearest wins.

### 1. First-Draft Content at Scale

Writing is the bottleneck in most L&D workflows. You have the subject matter expertise, the learning objectives, the SME notes. The actual drafting of facilitator guides, learner workbooks, scenario scripts, knowledge check questions, and job aids eats the time you'd rather spend on design and strategy.

Claude and ChatGPT are genuinely good at producing first drafts from structured input. The key word is "first." You are not asking the tool to replace your judgment. You are asking it to eliminate the blank page and give you something to react to.

Here's how the workflow looks in practice. You gather your SME notes and learning objectives. You paste them into Claude with a prompt like: "Here are my notes from a 30-minute SME interview about [topic]. Using these as source material only, draft a 10-question knowledge check with one correct answer and three plausible distractors per question. Target audience: mid-level managers with no prior background in [topic]."

What comes back is a working draft in five minutes instead of two hours. Your job is then to edit for accuracy, tone, and alignment to your organization's standards. That editing process goes faster than starting from zero.

### 2. Scenario and Case Study Writing

Scenario-based learning is widely understood to be more effective than content dumps. It's also time-consuming to write well. Good scenarios require realistic characters, plausible stakes, branching logic, and enough specificity to feel real without being so specific they alienate half the learner population.

AI is useful here in a specific way: generating scenario variations. Once you've written one strong scenario, you can ask Claude or ChatGPT to generate three variations on the same learning objective, changing the context, the characters, or the stakes. This gives you the range you'd normally spend a full writing sprint producing.

The prompt structure matters. "Write me a scenario about workplace conflict" produces generic results. "Write me a branching scenario where a team lead named Dana discovers that two direct reports have been giving each other negative informal feedback in front of customers. Dana has to decide whether to address it in the moment, wait for a formal 1:1, or escalate to HR. Write three decision points, each with two choices and a consequence. The learner should make mistakes and recover from them." That level of specificity produces something you can actually use.

### 3. Rapid Workshop Design and Facilitation Materials

Designing a half-day workshop from scratch, including the agenda, facilitator guide, slide notes, and participant materials, used to take a week. With AI as a writing partner, that timeline compresses significantly.

The process we've seen work well: start by giving Claude a full context brief. Not "design me a workshop on feedback" but: "I'm designing a 4-hour in-person workshop for 20 frontline supervisors at a healthcare organization. Learning objectives: [list]. Participants range from 2 years to 15 years of supervisory experience. We have access to breakout rooms and can do small group activities. We don't have a budget for printed materials. Draft a workshop agenda with timing, a description of each activity, and the facilitation question I should use to debrief each section."

What comes back needs editing. The timing is usually optimistic. The facilitation questions sometimes feel generic. But you have a skeleton in an hour instead of a day, and your editing is substantive rather than generative.

Matt Hastings, one of MVP Club's co-founders and a learning designer with 13 years of experience designing enterprise AI content for companies like Google and Adobe, puts it this way: "Anything you do more than once, write down how to do it, and then tell Claude how you want it done. The next time, you can just invoke that approach and say, here's the input, go do it. It turns a generically useful tool into a very specifically useful tool."

## The Quickest Win: AI-Assisted Needs Analysis Documentation

If you're new to using AI for L&D work, start here. Needs analysis documentation, synthesizing SME interview notes, survey responses, and performance data into a clear problem statement and recommendation, is time-intensive and doesn't require creative judgment in the way content design does. It's synthesis work, and synthesis is one of AI's clearest strengths.

Here's the workflow step by step.

**Step 1: Gather your raw inputs.** This might be notes from three SME interviews, a summary of a performance management report, and a handful of learner feedback comments from a previous program. Doesn't need to be perfect. You're going to feed it all to Claude.

**Step 2: Write a context prompt.** Tell Claude what you're trying to produce: "I'm an L&D professional at a 2,000-person healthcare company. I've conducted three SME interviews and reviewed six months of performance data. I'm trying to write a 1-page needs analysis document that my VP can read in five minutes. It should include: the performance gap, the root cause, and a recommended intervention. Here are my raw notes: [paste notes]."

**Step 3: Read the output critically.** Claude will produce a clean, readable synthesis. Your job is to check it for accuracy. Did it get the root cause right? Did it overemphasize something that isn't actually the central issue? Did it include anything you can't verify? Edit accordingly.

**Step 4: Use the document in your next stakeholder conversation.** Walk in with a one-page that took you ninety minutes instead of half a day. The quality is what matters, and if you've edited it well, the quality is yours.

This approach also works for program evaluation summaries, post-training survey analysis, and learning analytics reports. Any task where you're reading a lot of material and producing a shorter, structured synthesis of it.

## Tool Recommendations

For L&D work, two tools are worth knowing in depth: Claude and ChatGPT.

**Claude** (made by Anthropic) handles long documents exceptionally well. If you're pasting an entire SME transcript or a lengthy policy document, Claude tends to maintain coherence across a long conversation better than most alternatives. For scenario writing, facilitation guides, and anything where tone and nuance matter, Claude's output tends to require less editing. The Claude Pro subscription ($20/month) is worth it if you're doing this regularly.

**ChatGPT** (made by OpenAI) has a broad ecosystem of integrations and a feature called Custom GPTs that lets you build a configured assistant for a recurring task. If you're regularly producing the same type of content (weekly quizzes on the same topic, job aids in a specific format), a custom GPT saves setup time. The Plus tier is also $20/month.

You don't need both to start. Pick one, learn it for thirty days, then evaluate. Most L&D professionals in our community end up using Claude for writing and synthesis and ChatGPT for tasks where integration with other tools matters.

What you should not do: use the free tier for real work and expect consistent results. Both tools have usage limits and slower response times on free plans. Twenty dollars a month is less than one hour of contractor time and produces more output.

A few patterns keep showing up in our coaching sessions with L&D professionals.

The biggest early mistake is giving Claude or ChatGPT too little context and then deciding AI "doesn't understand learning design." It doesn't understand your organization, your learners, or your constraints unless you tell it. The quality of the output scales directly with the quality of the context you provide. An L&D professional who pastes a detailed brief with audience background, learning objectives, and constraints will get a fundamentally different result than one who types "write me a facilitator guide for a leadership workshop."

The second pattern: people underestimate how much value there is in the editing pass. The instinct is to want AI to produce finished content. That's not the right mental model. Think of Claude or ChatGPT as a fast first-draft generator that doesn't know your organization. Your job is to edit for accuracy, voice, and cultural fit. That editing pass is where your expertise shows, and it usually takes a fraction of the time the original drafting would have.

The third pattern is that L&D professionals are often better positioned than most roles to use AI effectively, precisely because they already think in terms of audience analysis, learning objectives, and structured content. Those skills transfer directly. When you write a prompt, you're essentially doing a micro needs analysis: who is the "learner" (in this case, Claude), what do they need to know, what should they produce?

## Your First Week: A 5-Day Micro-Practice Plan for L&D

Start with real work, not experiments. The fastest way to build capability is to apply AI to something you actually need to produce.

**Monday: Needs analysis synthesis.** Take any raw notes from a recent stakeholder interview or survey results sitting in a document. Paste them into Claude with the prompt: "Synthesize these notes into a 3-paragraph needs analysis summary that identifies the performance gap, likely root cause, and a possible learning intervention. Here are the notes: [paste]." Read the output. Edit for accuracy. Note what surprised you.

**Tuesday: Knowledge check draft.** Pick any topic you've been asked to address recently. Write your learning objectives (even rough ones). Then prompt Claude: "Based on these learning objectives, write 5 multiple-choice questions with one correct answer and three plausible distractors each. Avoid trivially easy distractors. Here are my objectives: [paste]." Edit the questions against your content.

**Wednesday: Scenario first draft.** Think of a performance problem you're currently designing for. Describe the situation, the characters, and what decision you want the learner to practice. Give that to Claude and ask for a scenario with two decision points and two choices each. See how close it gets without coaching from you on the first pass.

**Thursday: Facilitator debrief questions.** Take an activity from an existing workshop you've designed or delivered. Paste a description of the activity into Claude and ask: "Write five debrief questions for this activity that move learners from the experience to the principle to application in their own context." Edit for the language and tone of your organization.

**Friday: Reflect.** Which of the four tasks felt most useful? Where did Claude give you something you could work with quickly? Where did you have to heavily edit? That pattern tells you where to focus your practice in week two.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can AI replace my SME interviews?**

No. AI doesn't know your organization, your learners, or the specific context that makes content accurate and relevant. It can help you prepare for SME interviews (drafting interview questions, synthesizing notes afterward), but it cannot replace the source material that comes from talking to real people in your organization.

**What if I paste internal documents into Claude or ChatGPT? Is that a security risk?**

This depends on your organization's data policies. Many organizations have guidance on what can and can't be pasted into consumer AI tools. Check with your IT or legal team before pasting anything marked confidential. Claude and ChatGPT both offer enterprise tiers with stronger data privacy commitments if your organization is ready for that conversation.

**Will AI produce content that's accurate for my specific industry?**

Not automatically. Claude and ChatGPT have broad general knowledge but don't know your organization's specific processes, regulations, or culture. Every piece of AI-generated content needs a human review pass for accuracy. The AI is a drafting tool, not a subject matter expert.

**How do I handle the "AI is going to take my job" anxiety some L&D colleagues are feeling?**

Acknowledge it rather than dismiss it. The honest answer is that AI is going to change L&D work, not eliminate it. The skills that matter most in this field, understanding how people learn, designing for behavior change, facilitating conversations that shift perspective, are all things AI cannot replicate. What AI changes is the production layer: first drafts, variations, synthesis. Your expertise is in the judgment layer above that.
