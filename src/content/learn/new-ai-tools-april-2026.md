Anthropic shipped something big for office workers in late January, Perplexity started letting you build custom automations in March, and Google's NotebookLM can now turn your documents into narrated video. It's been a fast few weeks. This is our monthly roundup of what's new, what's actually useful, and what you can skip.

We focus on tools built for working professionals, not developers. If you need a terminal to use it, we'll say so upfront.

## What we're covering this month

Four tools had updates in March-April 2026 that are worth your attention:

1. **Claude Cowork** (Anthropic): autonomous task completion for knowledge workers
2. **Perplexity Custom Skills**: research that also creates deliverables
3. **NotebookLM Cinematic Video Overviews** (Google): turning documents into video
4. **Gemini 3.1 in Google Workspace**: reasoning upgrades inside Docs and Drive

We've been testing these in our weekly sessions and with community members. Here's the honest take.

## Quick verdict

If you only have time to try one thing: **Claude Cowork**, if you're on a Pro or Team plan. It does more than Claude Chat and is easier to get started with than Claude Code.

If you're already a heavy Claude user, **Perplexity's Custom Skills** are the other one worth half an hour.

The other two are worth knowing about, especially NotebookLM if you work with a lot of documents. Gemini 3.1 in Workspace is legitimately improved, but only if your organization already runs on Google.

## Claude Cowork: autonomous work for non-technical users

Anthropic launched Cowork in research preview for Pro subscribers in January and has been adding features through March. The short version: it brings Claude Code-level capabilities to people who have never opened a terminal.

With Claude Chat, you type something, Claude responds, and you copy-paste the output somewhere else. Cowork works at a different level: give it a goal, and it makes a plan, works through it, and delivers a finished result. It can open files on your computer, work with your documents, schedule recurring tasks, and connect to tools like Google Drive, Gmail, and Docusign.

The key thing that sets it apart from the standalone chat interface: it has a sense of context over time. You can point it at a folder and it remembers the project. You can schedule it to run a report every Monday morning.

### What our community is doing with it

In our New AI Tools session this spring, Carmella Thompson shared how she built a portfolio site using the Claude Code desktop app: "I built a GitHub portfolio site for myself, and I did it all in Claude Code. I didn't need to look at the folder structure."

Cowork is designed for people who had that same reaction to the terminal that Angela Frances Peat described in the same session: "The desktop is probably what I prefer, versus the terminal looks a little bit scary."

The permission model is also worth understanding. Cowork is intentionally constrained, closer to giving someone intern-level access than root access to your machine. That's a feature, not a limitation. For most office workflows, you want something that can do the work without having the keys to your entire system.

### Worth trying if:

- You're on Claude Pro, Team, or Enterprise
- You have a repeating task you'd like automated (weekly reports, summarizing documents, research roundups)
- You've been curious about AI that can take multi-step actions on its own (sometimes called agentic AI) but didn't want to deal with setup

### Skip for now if:

- You're on the free plan (not available yet)
- Your organization has strict IT policies around AI agents accessing local files (check with your IT team first)

**Getting started:** Download the Claude desktop app (not the browser version, which is more limited). Open the Cowork section, create a project, point it at a folder. Give it a task and watch what it does.

## Perplexity Custom Skills and Deep Research deliverables

Perplexity has been the steadiest improver in this space over the past year. In March, it shipped two updates that work well together.

**Custom Skills** let you define a repeating task once and invoke it with a single command. Anything you find yourself asking Perplexity to do more than once, whether it's monitoring a topic, reformatting research, or running the same analysis on new inputs, can become a skill. Think of it as your own personal workflow template inside the tool.

**Deep Research deliverables** are the other update worth noting. Previously, Perplexity Deep Research would return a long report. Now it can turn that research into a presentation, a spreadsheet, a structured summary, or a website. Research and creation in one place.

Perplexity built its reputation on "answers with sources," which has always been its edge for research work. These two features extend that into actual work output rather than just research output. The combination means less switching between tools.

The Comet iOS app (Perplexity's mobile agent) also hit broad release in late March. It's Perplexity's attempt at a full mobile AI agent. Worth downloading if you use Perplexity heavily on your phone.

### What works well

Perplexity's source citations have always been its edge over Claude and ChatGPT for research tasks. When you need to write something with references, or when accuracy matters and you want to verify where a claim came from, Perplexity handles this more naturally than any of the alternatives right now.

Custom Skills add useful repeatability to that. Matt noted in our community session: "Anything you do more than once, write down how to do it, and then tell Claude, here's how I want you to do it. Just go." The same logic applies in Perplexity now.

### Worth trying if:

- You do regular research work (competitive analysis, market monitoring, client briefings)
- You use Perplexity already and want to reduce repetitive setup
- You're on Perplexity Pro or Max (some features are limited to paid plans)

**Getting started:** Go to Perplexity, look for the Skills section in your profile settings. Define one task you do regularly and build your first skill. Five minutes to set up.

## NotebookLM Cinematic Video Overviews

Google rolled out Cinematic Video Overviews in NotebookLM in early March. The existing Video Overviews feature could already turn your documents into a narrated video explanation. The cinematic update adds fluid animations and visual storytelling on top of that.

The use case: you upload a document or set of sources, and NotebookLM generates a short video that explains the content. It uses the same AI-generated audio overview format that made NotebookLM's podcast feature popular, but now with visuals.

Document-to-video has real applications: onboarding new team members, presenting research findings to non-technical stakeholders, or creating explainer content without spending time on slides. It's a workflow that didn't exist six months ago.

### Honest caveats

The video quality is still in an earlier-generation feel compared to something like a polished explainer video. Don't expect it to replace a professional video production. Think of it as a useful first draft, or a tool for internal use where polish matters less than speed.

Also: this works best when your source documents are well-structured. Messy notes produce generic videos with vague narration. A clean, section-by-section document gives the model enough structure to produce a coherent narrative with accurate chapter-level coverage.

### Worth trying if:

- You already use NotebookLM for research synthesis
- You need to explain a complex topic to stakeholders who won't read the full document
- You're creating internal explainers or onboarding materials

**Getting started:** Go to [notebooklm.google.com](https://notebooklm.google.com), upload a document or paste in some sources, and look for the Video Overview button. Free to use.

## Gemini 3.1 in Google Workspace

Gemini 3.1 Pro launched in February 2026, and Google has been rolling out its improvements through the Workspace integration since. If you use Docs, Sheets, Slides, or Drive at work, there are upgrades worth knowing about.

The model-level improvement that matters most for everyday use: hallucination reduction. Google reports that Gemini 3.1 Pro makes fewer factual errors than the previous version [NEEDS CITATION: verify Google's published benchmarks]. When you're using AI to help research or draft documents, getting fewer things wrong changes how much you can trust the output without checking everything manually.

The Workspace integration improvements go alongside this. Gemini can now search across your Drive files and Gmail threads to answer questions with context from your actual documents. You can ask "what did we agree on in the Q4 planning doc?" and it will find the answer rather than making something up.

Cross-platform chat import (for users switching from ChatGPT or Claude to Gemini) and personal memory improvements are also live for Workspace users.

### What we use at MVP Club

Our community members are split on this one. People who live in Google Workspace already have a clear reason to try the Gemini integrations. People who rely on Claude or ChatGPT for their daily writing work have less reason to switch. Gemini 3.1 Pro leads on benchmarks in some categories, but benchmarks and daily feel don't always match. The practical difference on professional writing tasks between Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 3.1 Pro is not dramatic enough to justify switching your main tool.

The reason to try it: if you're already inside Docs or Drive for most of your day, having Gemini search your files means fewer trips to a separate chat window.

### Worth trying if:

- Your organization uses Google Workspace
- You regularly need to reference past documents or emails while writing
- You want AI that's already connected to your file system without additional setup
- You've been looking for a reason to cut down on switching between your email, Drive, and a separate AI chat window

**Getting started:** Look for the Gemini button inside Google Docs or Google Drive. If your organization has a Workspace account with Gemini enabled, it should already be there.

## What we're keeping an eye on

The biggest story we're watching is Claude Cowork's enterprise rollout. As of now it's a research preview. If you're thinking about how AI agents might work in your organization, Cowork is worth understanding now, before it becomes a procurement question.

Also: Perplexity's MCP integration is potentially significant for teams. MCP (Model Context Protocol) is a standard that lets AI tools connect directly to external apps and data sources. Perplexity now has 400+ prebuilt connectors, meaning you can hook Perplexity into your CRM, project management tool, or internal knowledge base and run research without leaving those systems.
