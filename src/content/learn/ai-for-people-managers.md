Your performance review cycle is three weeks out. You have 12 direct reports. You haven't looked at your notes from anyone's last check-in since January. And your calendar has 14 hours of back-to-back meetings between now and the deadline.

This is exactly the kind of situation where AI for people managers stops being a productivity experiment and starts being a genuine lifeline.

People managers carry a specific kind of cognitive load that most AI advice completely ignores. You're not just managing your own work. You're tracking the progress, moods, goals, blockers, and development arcs of multiple people at once, while also writing your own deliverables, attending every meeting, and somehow being "present" in your 1:1s instead of distracted. That's an enormous amount to hold.

## Where AI actually helps for people managers

There are three areas where managers in our community have found the most consistent value. Performance review writing. 1:1 preparation. And team communication.

None of these are about AI replacing your judgment. You're the one who knows your team. You're the one who notices that Sarah seemed off in last Thursday's call, or that Marcus has been killing it on a project nobody else has noticed. What AI does is help you turn what you know into something useful, faster.

Before diving into each one, it's worth naming the mental model that makes all of this work. Matt Hastings, one of MVP Club's co-founders, put it this way in a community session: "When I'm managing people, I make a ton of process documentation, because I find that managing the how work gets done is a great way to set people up for success. And that's basically what working with Claude is: you're managing the how you want it to do things."

That framing changes how you prompt. You're not asking AI to write something for you. You're managing it the way you'd manage a capable new team member who doesn't know your context yet.

## Use Case 1: Performance reviews (the highest-impact place to start)

Performance reviews are the most universally painful part of people management. Not because managers don't care about their teams. It's the opposite: most managers care deeply and feel the weight of getting this right. The blank page, the pressure to be fair, the fear of saying the wrong thing, the sheer volume of writing required. That's what makes reviews so hard.

Here's what actually helps.

### Before you open Claude, gather your raw material

This is the most important step, and it's the one most people skip. Claude can only work with what you give it. Before you start writing anything, spend 15-20 minutes pulling together your actual notes:

- Any 1:1 notes or check-in summaries from the review period
- The employee's self-review, if your organization uses them
- Key projects they worked on, with outcomes (specific is better: "launched the redesign in Q3, which reduced support tickets by 15%" not "worked on the redesign")
- One or two concrete examples of how they handled something difficult
- Any feedback you gave them informally throughout the year, if you can remember it

The richer your input, the better the output. If you give Claude a bullet-pointed brain dump, you'll get a usable draft. If you give it a single sentence, you'll get a generic one.

### A prompt that works

Here's a starting structure you can adapt in Claude or ChatGPT:

> I'm writing a performance review for [role title]. Here's what I know about their performance this review period:
>
> [paste your notes, bullet points, key projects, examples]
>
> The tone should be direct and specific. Please draft a performance review that:
> - Opens with an overall assessment
> - Highlights 2-3 specific strengths with concrete examples
> - Names 1-2 areas for development with specific, constructive language
> - Closes with a forward-looking statement about growth
>
> Do not use corporate jargon. Write as if a thoughtful manager is speaking directly to their employee.

Read the draft critically. It will almost always get some things right and some things wrong. The development areas in particular tend to come out too soft or too vague. Push back: "Make the development feedback more specific. What behavior should change and why does it matter?" That conversation is faster than writing from scratch.

### A common mistake: giving AI too little context

The single most frequent thing we see in coaching sessions is managers pasting a name and a job title into Claude and expecting something usable to come out. It doesn't. And then they conclude "AI doesn't work for performance reviews."

What they've actually discovered is that AI requires the same thing good management requires: knowing your people well enough to say something specific about them. If you can't fill in your notes before writing the prompt, that's useful diagnostic information about where your 1:1 practice needs to improve, not an AI limitation.

## Use Case 2: 1:1 preparation

The best 1:1 conversations happen when both people come prepared. In practice, most managers are joining a recurring invite with a vague memory of what happened last week and a vague hope that something important will surface.

AI can help you build a better 1:1 habit in two ways: preparing before the meeting and capturing after it.

### Before the meeting

At the start of each week, spend 5-10 minutes per direct report building a context brief in Claude. You can do this for all your reports at once if you set it up right.

Here's a simple prompt format:

> I have a 1:1 tomorrow with [name], who works in [role]. Here's context on where things stand:
>
> [Paste any recent notes, project updates, things they mentioned last week, anything you've been meaning to follow up on]
>
> Help me prepare:
> 1. What are the 3 most important things to check in on?
> 2. What questions would help me understand how they're doing beyond the task list?
> 3. Is there anything in this context that suggests I should proactively address something?

The third prompt is the one that pays off most often. It surfaces things you've been unconsciously tracking but haven't named explicitly.

### After the meeting

Right after your 1:1, paste your notes into Claude and ask it to summarize the key takeaways and any commitments made. This takes about two minutes and creates a record that's genuinely useful the next week, rather than a set of cryptic bullet points you can't decode three weeks later.

This also becomes the raw material for performance reviews. If you build this habit now, your next review cycle is dramatically easier.

## Use Case 3: Team communication

This is the category where managers are most hesitant, for understandable reasons. Communication is the thing people most associate with the "human side" of management. It feels wrong to use AI to write something to your team.

But there's an important distinction: using AI to help you communicate more clearly and consistently is different from outsourcing your voice.

### Where it genuinely helps

**Difficult feedback.** When you need to give someone feedback that you're worried about landing wrong, write a first draft in Claude. Tell it the situation, your relationship with this person, what the behavior is, and what you want the outcome to be. Ask it to draft language that's direct without being harsh. You'll almost always edit it significantly, but having a starting point gets you past the paralysis of "how do I even say this?"

**Team updates on complex topics.** When there's been an org change, a project pivot, or something ambiguous you need to communicate, Claude can help you think through what your team actually needs to know and in what order. Ask it: "I need to communicate [situation] to my team of [n] people. What are the key questions they're likely to have? Help me draft an update that addresses those."

**Recognition messages.** It sounds counterintuitive, but specific and genuine recognition is hard to write well. You know what someone did was great, but putting it into words in a way that actually lands can be surprisingly difficult. Give Claude the specifics and ask it to help you articulate why it mattered. Then make it sound like you.

### The voice check

Any time you send something to your team that started with AI, read it aloud before you send it. If it doesn't sound like how you actually talk, fix it. The goal is clarity and speed, not a communication that feels like it came from corporate HQ.

## Tool recommendations

For people management work specifically, Claude tends to be the better choice for writing tasks that require nuance and tone. Its outputs are more conversational by default, and it's better at following style instructions than ChatGPT in most direct comparisons our members have done.

For quick 1:1 prep when you have 5 minutes before a meeting, ChatGPT works just as well and is slightly faster. The $20/month cost for either Claude Pro or ChatGPT Plus is worth it if you're using this regularly. The free tiers have context limits that will frustrate you with longer performance review drafts.

Don't try to use both at the same time. Pick one, learn it well for two or three weeks, then decide if you want to compare. Jumping between tools slows down the learning.

## Your first week: a 5-day plan

You don't need to overhaul everything at once. Here's a realistic starting schedule.

**Day 1: Performance review draft for one person (45 minutes)**
Pick one direct report. Pull together your notes. Run through the prompt format above. Read the draft, edit it, and notice what Claude got right and what it missed. Don't use this draft yet. Use it to understand how the tool thinks.

**Day 2: 1:1 prep for two people (20 minutes)**
Before two of your 1:1s, try the preparation prompt. See if the questions it surfaces are ones you wouldn't have thought of yourself.

**Day 3: 1:1 capture for one meeting (10 minutes)**
After one 1:1, paste your notes and ask for a summary. See if it catches anything you'd already forgotten.

**Day 4: Draft a difficult message (30 minutes)**
Think of a piece of communication you've been putting off. A tricky feedback conversation, a team update on something ambiguous. Draft it in Claude. Edit it heavily. Send it if it's ready, or save it as a template.

**Day 5: Reflect on the week (15 minutes)**
What saved you time? What felt off? What would you change about how you're prompting? Write down three observations. This reflection is what separates people who build a real practice from people who try it once and move on.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is it okay to use AI to write performance reviews?**
That depends on your organization's policies, and it's worth checking. In most companies, there's nothing in the policy that prohibits it, because the policies were written before this was a practical question. The relevant question is whether the output is accurate and yours. If you're using Claude to help articulate what you already know about someone, and you're reviewing and editing the output carefully, the review reflects your judgment. If you're making up context and letting AI fill in the blanks, that's a different situation, and a problem with or without AI involved.

**What if I don't have good notes from the review period?**
Start building them now. Even if you're two weeks from a deadline, a quick brain dump of what you remember, organized by person, is better than nothing. For next cycle, the 1:1 capture habit described above is the most reliable way to have good material when you need it.

**Will my team notice that I'm using AI?**
If you edit the output to sound like you, probably not. If you send Claude's draft unchanged, they might. The goal is to use AI to think more clearly and communicate more effectively, not to outsource the human parts of management. Those parts are still yours.

**What if Claude writes something I disagree with?**
Push back. That's the core skill. Tell it what's wrong: "That development feedback is too vague. This person struggled with following through on commitments and I need to name that specifically." Work the conversation until you have something true. The first draft is a starting point.
