Most organizations that try to expand AI company-wide hit the same wall about three months in. The pilot was promising. A few people loved it. Leadership signed off. And then... nothing spread. The folks who were curious stayed curious. Everyone else went back to doing things the way they always had.

The tool wasn't the problem. The approach was.

What changes outcomes has nothing to do with a better platform or a bigger launch announcement. It comes down to people. Specifically, having the right people inside departments, embedded in real workflows, who are already trusted by their colleagues and willing to say, "Here's what I've been doing with this. Want to try it?"

That's the case for building an AI champions network.

## What an AI champions network actually is (and isn't)

An AI champion is a peer: someone in finance, operations, HR, or marketing who has figured out how to use Claude or ChatGPT in ways that actually help with the work they and their teammates do every day. They live inside the normal organizational structure. They're not a trainer, and they're not a dedicated "AI lead" set apart from the rest of the team.

They're the person who shows their colleague how they use Claude to draft the first pass at a weekly status update. The one who saves a template prompt they've been using and shares it in a Slack channel. The one who troubleshoots when someone says "I tried it and it gave me something weird."

What makes champions effective is proximity and trust, not technical expertise.

A champions network is a structured way to identify those people, give them light support, and help their knowledge travel across the organization instead of staying siloed in one person's head.

## Why peer-led beats top-down

In organizations where AI adoption stalls, leadership mandates it, IT provides access, and then HR sends an email with a link to an online resource. Adoption rates hover around 15-20%. People click through the content but don't change how they work.

In organizations where it actually spreads, one person in accounting figures out a genuinely useful workflow. Their manager notices they're finishing things faster. A few colleagues ask what they're doing. A month later, half the team has tried it.

The difference is social proof from a trusted peer, not instruction from an authority.

## Who makes a good champion (it's not who you think)

The instinct is to find your most technically curious employees. The ones who've already been experimenting, who ask IT questions, who read about new tools on their own time. And yes, early adopters often make good champions.

But the most effective champions are the people who are already trusted, regardless of their technical background. They may not have been the first to try AI. What they have is credibility with their team. When they say something is worth trying, people actually try it.

Look for people who:

- Are known as problem-solvers and "go-to" people in their departments
- Communicate clearly and don't condescend
- Are genuinely curious (not performatively enthusiastic)
- Have some flexibility in how they structure their workday
- Are comfortable saying "I don't know" alongside "let me find out"

The non-technical background is often a feature (and I say that as someone who came to Claude without a coding background). Someone who figured out how to use it for real work without technical scaffolding has already done the translation work. They're more relatable, not less.

The average person is not going to push through friction. They just won't. That applies to would-be champions just as much as it applies to the colleagues they're trying to bring along. If you put the wrong people in the role, or overburden the right ones, the network goes quiet.

## How to structure the network without making it a second job

The biggest risk with a champions program is asking too much. Champions have real jobs. If you're expecting them to lead formal training sessions, create content, attend weekly committee meetings, and respond to every ad-hoc question in Slack, you'll burn them out or they'll quietly step back.

A practical structure that works:

**A steering layer (small):** 2-3 people who hold the overall strategy, maintain the resource library, and run the monthly champions sync. This is typically an internal comms or ops person plus whoever owns AI adoption at the leadership level. Their job is coordination, not content creation.

**A champions layer (distributed):** One champion per department or team of 50-75 people. Their minimum viable commitment is 30-60 minutes per week, doing what they'd probably do anyway: using AI in their work, sharing what they're learning with colleagues, and showing up when someone gets stuck.

**Informal community:** An internal Slack or Teams channel where champions and their teammates can share prompts, ask questions, and celebrate small wins. Low overhead, high value.

The key framing for recruiting champions is that this is optional and additive, not compulsory. "We'd love for you to be someone who shares what you're learning with your team" lands very differently than "we're adding this to your role description." (That distinction matters more than it sounds. One feels like an invitation. The other feels like surveillance.)

## What champions actually do week to week

This is where most programs get vague. Here's what it actually looks like in practice:

**Share one thing per week.** Not a formal presentation. A message in the team channel: "I've been using Claude to draft my talking points before 1:1s. Here's the prompt I've been using." That's it.

**Be available for short side-by-side moments.** When a colleague is curious but stuck, the champion spends 15 minutes with them, not to teach a class but to show them what they actually do. This is where adoption happens.

**Collect friction.** Champions are the best early warning system for what's not working. When a workflow breaks, when policies are unclear, when colleagues have the same question repeatedly, they surface it to the steering layer. This feedback loop is genuinely valuable for leadership.

**Connect people across teams.** One of the best things a champions network enables is cross-departmental learning. The champion in marketing who figured out a research workflow might share it with the operations champion who has the same underlying need.

## The governance piece you can't skip

Champions need cover. This is one of the things that gets left out of most champions playbooks and then bites organizations six months later.

When a department head pushes back on a champion who's spending time experimenting with Claude instead of "real work," or when someone raises a concern about data privacy and the champion doesn't know the policy, the program unravels fast.

Before you launch a champions network, make sure:

**There's a clear policy on approved tools.** Champions can't confidently share workflows if they don't know what's allowed. If your organization doesn't have an AI policy yet, the champions program actually becomes a good reason to create one. [AI workplace policies: what you need to know](/learn/ai-workplace-policies) breaks down what most workplace policies actually say and where the gaps usually are.

**Champions have explicit top cover.** They should be able to point to a leadership statement or an internal document that says: "This work is encouraged. Time spent on this is time well spent." Without that, champions operate in a gray zone and will hedge.

**There's a clear escalation path.** When questions come up that champions can't answer (data security, vendor contracts, policy exceptions), they need to know who to direct people to. Champions should never have to guess on governance questions.

## Getting the first cohort started

You don't need a perfect program design before you start. You need five people who are already doing the work informally and a reason to bring them together.

**Week 1-2:** Identify 3-5 people across different departments who are already using AI tools in their daily work. Talk to them individually. Ask what they've figured out. Don't pitch the program yet. Just learn.

**Week 3-4:** Bring them together for an informal 60-minute conversation. Share what you heard from each of them. Ask what they'd find helpful in being more deliberate about sharing what they know. This group should design the program, not just receive it.

**Month 2:** Soft launch. Give the group a shared space (Slack channel, shared doc library), a light cadence (monthly check-in, no more), and one simple expectation: share something useful with your team once a week.

**Month 3:** Evaluate and expand. What's working? Who in the organization has been most receptive? Where are the natural next champions?

## The mistake that kills most programs before they start

Announcing it too big.

The instinct when you're excited about an initiative is to brief leadership, send an all-staff email, and create a formal nomination process for champions. That sequence adds pressure before value has been demonstrated and puts champions in a spotlight they didn't sign up for.

The programs that work tend to start quietly, inside one or two teams, until they have enough real wins that people start asking why their department doesn't have something like it. Grassroots momentum is more sustainable than top-down mandate.

As one of our community members put it when she started sharing Claude workflows with her HR team: people don't need to be convinced that AI is important. They need to see that someone just like them has figured out something useful and is willing to show them.

## The career angle: why being this person matters

If you're reading this, you may be thinking about whether you want to be this person at your organization rather than how to build the program from above. That's a legitimate position, and it's probably the more common one. (You don't have to wait for someone to hand you the title.)

Being an informal champion before any formal structure exists is actually the most valuable version of this. You're doing the real work, building real credibility, and positioning yourself as the person who helped your organization figure this out.

That kind of reputation doesn't show up on a job description. It shows up in who leadership turns to when they need someone who understands how AI actually fits into the work people do.
