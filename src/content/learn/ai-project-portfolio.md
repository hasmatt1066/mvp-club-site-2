I shipped five products in roughly two months. Geddit Together is live in the App Store. Rendition Video converts any document into a narrated video. TasteMaker helps you build a design style guide in about ten minutes. I built all of them using Claude, Cline, and VS Code, with no engineering team and no outside budget.

I didn't do that to have something to brag about. I did it because the work itself taught me more about AI collaboration than any article I'd read. And somewhere along the way, the collection of projects started to tell a story about what I can do now that I couldn't do two years ago.

That's what a portfolio does. It shows the before and after. Not through a claim on a resume, but through actual things that exist.

## Why Your Projects Are Evidence, Not Just Practice

There's a real gap between professionals who are "learning about AI" and professionals who are building with it. The gap shows up in hiring conversations, client pitches, and team decisions. Managers and hiring teams have been burned enough times by people who could talk about AI but couldn't apply it to an actual problem.

A portfolio closes that gap. Not with credentials or course completions, but with artifacts: a working tool, a documented process, a deployed page that anyone can visit. When someone asks what you've been doing with AI, you stop explaining and start showing.

## What Counts as an AI Project

This is where most people overthink it. A portfolio project does not need to be a SaaS product or a funded startup idea. It needs to show that you can take a real problem, use AI to work toward a solution, and produce something that functions in the world.

Some examples from the MVP Club community:

- A GitHub portfolio site built entirely in Claude Code, without touching the terminal or the folder structure
- A custom reporting template generator for weekly status updates, built in an afternoon
- An automation that takes a raw transcript and outputs a formatted meeting summary with action items
- A client intake form workflow that feeds into a branded deliverable automatically

None of these took weeks. Most took a few hours spread across a couple of sessions. The point is that they work, and the person who built them now has something to show.

## The Project Selection Framework

When you are picking what to build, three questions are worth asking before you scope anything.

Does it solve a real problem you actually have? The best portfolio projects come from friction you experience yourself. A template you use every week. A manual process that eats two hours you would rather spend on something else. A report your team always asks for. Real problems make better projects because they force you to get specific, and specificity is what separates a portfolio that impresses from one that fills a page.

Can you explain the before and after? Every project should have a two-sentence story: here is what the process looked like before, and here is what it looks like now. Before: I spent 45 minutes formatting meeting notes manually every Friday. After: I paste the raw transcript into a Claude workflow and get a formatted summary in under five minutes. Concrete. Measurable. Believable.

Can someone else see it? A tool that runs on your machine and nobody else can access is worth less than one that lives somewhere. A GitHub repo. A deployed page. A Loom walkthrough. Even a screen recording of it working. Showing beats explaining every time.

## Building Your First Three Projects

I started with five projects and that was too many. By project four I was context-switching constantly and not finishing anything well. Three is what I'd tell someone starting now: enough to show range, not so many that you're maintaining a backlog instead of building.

**Project 1: Something you use every week**

Your first project should be immediately useful to you. Pick the most repetitive text-based task in your current role and handle the most time-consuming part of it with Claude. This is your confidence builder. It gives you a result you care about, which means you will iterate on it, which means you will learn more than any walkthrough video would teach you. My first real project was a reporting template I used at DualEnroll. Unglamorous, but it ran every week and I kept making it better.

**Project 2: Something that showcases a specific skill**

Your second project should target the work you want to do more of. If you are in marketing, build something that touches copy or audience research. If you are in operations, build something that touches documentation or process mapping. Match the project to the audience you are trying to impress, not the audience you already have.

**Project 3: Something slightly outside your comfort zone**

Your third project should push into territory that feels a little uncomfortable. If the first two were Claude chat workflows, try deploying something. Try Cline with VS Code. Claude Pro runs about $20 a month, and Cline charges by token usage, so you're realistically looking at $30 to $50 a month to run a serious build practice. That's it. The goal is not to become a developer. The goal is to show that you learn by doing, which is the actual skill employers and clients want evidence of.

## How to Document What You Build

Building the project is half the work. Documenting it is how it becomes career evidence.

For each project, write a short README or project description that covers:

1. The problem you were solving (one sentence)
2. The approach (what tools, what workflow, what you tried)
3. The result (what it does now, how long it takes, what changed)
4. What you would do differently (shows self-awareness and iteration instinct)

The fourth one is the one most people skip. It is also the one that makes your portfolio feel real instead of curated. Perfect projects do not exist. Showing that you know where your work has edges is a more credible signal than presenting everything as a polished success.

Addy Spiller, a consultant in our community building her portfolio, put it well: "There's something of value to show, like, how I think, how I work." ## Where to Put Your Portfolio

GitHub is the standard for a reason: it is free, it is where technical hiring managers look, and it forces a level of organization that makes your work easier to explain. You do not need to know git to start. Claude can walk you through setting up a portfolio page in an afternoon.

If GitHub feels too technical right now, a simple Notion page, a personal website built with Claude Code, or even a clean PDF with screenshots and descriptions works. The format matters less than the habit of making your work visible.

## The Compounding Effect

The tenth project feels categorically different from the first. Not just better. Different in kind.

By the time you have built ten things, you have an intuition for what makes a good problem scope. You know which tools to reach for. You have a sense for when to push and when to start over. You have made enough mistakes in front of your own screen that failure does not slow you down the way it did at the start. A portfolio of real work is how you demonstrate that intuition to someone who has never seen you build anything.

Most people assume AI helps you start faster. That is true. The bigger shift, the one that changed how I work, is that it helps you finish. The gap between "working prototype" and "real thing that exists in the world" used to feel enormous, and for most of my career it was. I shipped more in two months than in the previous twenty years. Not incrementally more. Categorically more. AI collapses that gap, which means professionals who build with it regularly end up with portfolios that, even six months ago, would have required a team.

## A Six-Week Build Plan

If you are starting from zero: spend the first two weeks on the project you use every week. Build it, iterate on it at least twice, document it, and get it into a shareable format. Week three and four go to the second project, the one targeting a specific audience. The last two weeks are for the stretch project. If it is not done by week six, document it anyway, including where you got stuck and what you learned. Incomplete projects with honest documentation are still portfolio material.

Six weeks from now you will have three concrete things to point to.

For the tools: Claude is where I spend most of my building time. For more complex workflows, Cline with VS Code gives you more control over file structure and iterative changes. ## Where People Get Stuck

A few patterns come up repeatedly in our community sessions. I have hit most of them personally.

**Scope creep on the first project.** You start trying to automate your meeting notes and end up wanting to build a full knowledge management system. I did this with TasteMaker. The original idea was a ten-minute tool; I kept adding features until it had a preferences engine I never used. Cut it back. The goal of the first project is to ship something, not to build the ideal version of something.

**Perfectionism before sharing.** Waiting until something is polished enough to show is how portfolios never get built. Share early. Ugly works. Functional matters more than beautiful.

**Solving a problem that does not exist.** The most common portfolio mistake is building something technically interesting that very few people would actually use. Start with your own friction. Build for a one-person audience first: yourself.

**Not iterating after the first result.** Claude's first output is almost never the best version. The professionals who get the most out of AI are the ones who treat the first draft as a starting point and keep pushing. That iteration instinct is also the most important thing a portfolio can demonstrate.
