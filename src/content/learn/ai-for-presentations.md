One MVP Club member spent four hours building a twelve-slide deck for a leadership update. She knew what she wanted to say. The slides took four hours anyway. The next week, she tried it with Claude. The whole thing was done in forty minutes, and her director said it was the clearest update she'd given all year.

That gap is real. AI for presentations is one of the highest-return places to start if you haven't already, and the workflow is simpler than most people expect.

## Why presentations take so long (and where AI helps most)

The actual time sink in most presentation builds is not design. It is the structural thinking: figuring out what to say, in what order, to make a specific point land for a specific audience. That is also exactly where AI tools like Claude and ChatGPT are most useful.

Most people open PowerPoint or Google Slides and start building slides before they have a clear story. The result is a deck that looks like notes, not a narrative. AI reverses the order. You build the argument first, in plain language, and then translate it into slides. That shift alone saves an hour on a typical presentation.

## The two-step workflow that actually works

### Step 1: Get the structure right with Claude or ChatGPT

Before you touch a slide deck, open Claude or ChatGPT and describe what you need. Give it context: who the audience is, what decision or reaction you want from them, what you already know, and how long you have to present.

A prompt that works well:

> "I'm presenting to my leadership team on Tuesday. The topic is our Q2 marketing results. The audience already knows the basics. I want to walk out with approval for two new campaigns. I have 15 minutes. Help me outline a deck that makes a clear case for the campaigns without overwhelming them with data. Then give me a talking-points version for each slide."

Claude will draft a slide-by-slide outline with suggested headings and supporting points. It takes about ninety seconds. What you get back is a skeleton you can disagree with, reshape, and make your own. That is the part most people skip: the AI gives you something to react to, which is much faster than starting from nothing.

Paste the outline back and ask Claude to tighten it, or flag the slides where you want to lead with data versus story. Treat it like a fast back-and-forth with a capable colleague who has no ego about being wrong.

### Step 2: Build the deck (with or without a dedicated AI tool)

Once you have the structure, you have two paths.

**Option A: Use Claude or ChatGPT to draft slide-level content, then paste into your preferred tool.** This works in any slide software. You write the deck content in the AI conversation, slide by slide, and then manually assemble in PowerPoint, Google Slides, or Keynote. It takes longer than Option B but gives you full control over templates and formatting.

**Option B: Use a dedicated AI presentation tool.** Gamma (gamma.app) is the most widely used option right now, with over 70 million users as of 2026. You give Gamma a prompt or paste in your Claude-drafted outline, and it generates a complete deck with layout choices, design consistency, and images in under a minute. The result is not always final, but it is a strong starting point that would have taken a designer an hour to mock up.

## A practical example: the status update deck

Say you have a standing monthly update to deliver. You give stakeholders project status, flag blockers, and share what is coming next. This type of deck takes most people forty-five minutes to an hour to put together, mostly because you have to pull from notes, emails, and your own memory to reconstruct what happened.

Here is a faster version using Claude:

1. Open Claude and paste in your raw notes from the month (meeting notes, Slack messages, status emails, anything you have).
2. Ask: "Based on these notes, create a status update deck outline. Audience is senior leaders who care about progress, blockers, and what help I need from them. Six slides maximum."
3. Review what comes back, add anything missing, and adjust the framing.
4. Paste the final outline into Gamma (or back to Claude for slide text) and build the deck.

The whole process runs twenty to thirty minutes instead of an hour. You spend your time reviewing and adjusting, not assembling from scratch.

This connects to the broader idea behind [how to use AI at work](/learn/how-to-use-ai-at-work): the highest-return habit is feeding AI your raw material and letting it do the assembly. Your judgment stays essential. The mechanical parts get handled.

## Common mistakes people make the first time

**Prompting without context.** "Write me a presentation about our Q2 results" gets you a generic deck. "Write me a ten-minute presentation for a CFO audience who needs to understand why we missed our lead target and what we're changing in Q3" gets you something actually usable. The more specific you are, the less editing you do.

**Accepting the first output without questioning it.** Claude's first draft is a starting point, not a final answer. Read the structure critically. If the slide order does not match the story you need to tell, push back. Ask Claude to reorder, reframe, or cut slides. The conversation is the work.

**Skipping the outline step and going straight to design.** AI design tools are fast but they are not magic. If you paste a vague prompt into Gamma without a solid outline, you get a fast mediocre deck instead of a slow mediocre deck. Spend five minutes on structure first. It changes everything about the output.

**Using it only for big decks.** AI for presentations is just as useful for a three-slide summary you drop in Slack or a quick talking-point list before a hallway meeting. The habit builds fastest when you use it on small things, not just major deliverables.

## From the Practice

In MVP Club's weekly sessions, presentations come up constantly as a use case where people see immediate results. The pattern that works best: bring the AI into the narrative stage, before any slide software opens. Members who skip directly to Gamma or PowerPoint with only a vague topic tend to get a deck that looks polished but needs a full restructure. Members who spend ten minutes with Claude on the story first, then generate visuals, consistently get decks that require less revision.

One pattern that has shown up repeatedly: using Claude to write the "So what?" sentence for each slide (the one-sentence takeaway you would say out loud for each section), and then building the slide content backward from that. It forces clarity that improves both the deck and the live delivery.

## What this practice actually builds

Getting good at AI for presentations is not just about saving forty minutes. It changes how you think about presentations. When building a deck takes four hours, you avoid making them unless you have to. When it takes forty minutes, you make them more often. You pitch ideas you would have left in email. You share updates that would have stayed in your head.

[Project managers](/learn/ai-for-project-managers) in particular report that faster deck-building changes how often they communicate upward. The threshold for "worth presenting" drops. That turns out to matter a lot for career visibility.

The skill that compounds fastest is learning to write a clear, specific prompt. If you can describe your audience, their existing knowledge, the decision you want them to make, and your time constraint in two sentences, Claude or ChatGPT will give you something genuinely useful in about ninety seconds. That prompt discipline transfers to every other AI task you pick up.
