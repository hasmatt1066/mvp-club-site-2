Claude told Lesley the wrong year on her own bio. Pulled it from the web, stated it with total confidence, and moved on. She caught it. But only because she was paying attention.

That moment captures something important about working with AI tools. They are genuinely useful, and they are genuinely fallible, often at the same time. The output looks correct. It reads well. And some piece of it is just wrong.

None of this is a reason to stop using Claude or ChatGPT. But it is worth understanding your role more clearly. You are not a passive recipient of AI output. You are the person responsible for it.

## Why AI gets things wrong (and why it doesn't know when it does)

AI tools generate responses by predicting what text should come next, based on patterns in enormous amounts of training data. They are not looking facts up in a database. They are not checking their work. When a model produces something confidently wrong, it is not lying. It genuinely cannot distinguish between "a fact I learned from reliable sources" and "a plausible-sounding thing I generated." That distinction lives in your judgment, not the model's.

This matters for professional work because the outputs look polished. A hallucinated statistic, a wrong date, a fabricated citation: these do not announce themselves. They sit in the middle of otherwise solid paragraphs and wait for someone to notice. According to [PwC's overview of AI hallucinations](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/tech-effect/ai-analytics/ai-hallucinations.html), confident but false statements are among the hardest errors to catch precisely because the surrounding context is accurate.

The types of errors that catch professionals off guard tend to cluster around a few categories. Factual specifics (dates, numbers, names, citations). Anything about your organization or your own background, which the model may have learned nothing about or learned outdated information about. Interpretive leaps, where the model fills a gap in your instructions with a reasonable-sounding inference that does not match your intent. And domain-specific judgment calls, where the right answer depends on context the model does not have.

## The human judgment layer in practice

Here is something I said in a recent MVP Club Getting Started session, when a community member found inaccurate information Claude had pulled into her portfolio site:

> "This is the really important step. You gave it the requirements, it built it, and now you're managing the output through evaluation. You're evaluating the output, you're like: this is wrong. So how do you correct it?" — from a Getting Started session

The word "managing" is doing real work in that sentence. You are not just using a tool. You are in a managerial relationship with it. You specify the goal, you review the output, you catch the gaps, and you give feedback. That cycle repeats until the output is something you'd put your name on.

This shift changes what "using AI" actually means. The skill is not writing a perfect prompt. The skill is evaluating what comes back. And that evaluation is yours to do, every time.

## Three categories of output that need closer review

Not every piece of AI output carries the same risk. A brainstormed list of meeting agenda items is low-stakes. A compliance policy draft is not. Knowing which category you are in helps you calibrate how carefully to read.

**Anything you would forward, publish, or submit.** Before any AI output leaves your hands, it needs a real read. Not a skim. Especially for professional documents, client-facing content, or anything with your name on it. You are the author. The model is the first drafter.

**Specific facts, figures, and citations.** This is where AI errors are most common and most damaging. If Claude gives you a statistic, look it up. If it generates a citation, check that it exists. Lawyers have learned this the hard way: fabricated case citations have appeared in real filings, with real consequences. **Anything about your own organization.** Claude and ChatGPT know very little about your company specifically. Internal processes, org structure, recent decisions, your specific team's norms: none of this is in the model's training data. When you ask for something that requires organizational context and you have not provided it, the model will make reasonable inferences that may or may not match reality. You are the only one who can close that gap.

## The "does this sound like me?" check

One category of error is subtler than a wrong fact. It is a right fact delivered in a wrong way: wrong for your voice, wrong for your relationship with the reader, wrong for your organization's culture.

Brian, who participated in a Getting Started session on website building, named it well:

> "When I use these tools, some things just sound a little bit more like AI. So I'm conscious: Does this sound like AI?" — Brian Lightfoot, MVP Club community member

That question is worth internalizing. AI-generated text has patterns: it hedges consistently, it transitions smoothly between paragraphs, it uses certain sentence structures over and over. When you are the author, you notice when something does not sound like you. Readers notice too, even if they cannot name why.

A real editorial pass, one where you read aloud or at minimum read slowly, catches these moments. You are not looking for factual errors. You are asking whether this is something you would actually say to this person.

## How to build verification into your workflow (without slowing everything down)

The goal is not to spend as much time reviewing AI output as you would have spent writing from scratch. That defeats the purpose. The goal is a quick, deliberate check that catches the errors most likely to matter.

A few patterns that work well:

**Read for claims.** As you scan a draft, mentally flag every specific claim: names, numbers, dates, external references. Give each one five seconds of attention. Does it seem right? If you are not sure, verify. Most claims you will know on sight. The ones you pause on are worth a quick check.

**Paste facts back in and ask Claude to verify them.** This sounds circular, but it is surprisingly effective: take the specific claims from a draft and ask Claude in a separate message to confirm them and flag anything it is not confident about. You are essentially asking the model to do a fact-check pass on its own output. It will not catch everything, but it catches some things, and the process forces you to look at claims individually rather than embedded in prose.

**Add organizational context before you generate.** Many errors happen because the model fills context gaps with guesses. Giving Claude a paragraph of background about your team, your audience, or your organization's specific situation before asking for a draft reduces how much it needs to infer.

Human judgment on output quality is the critical layer. This came up repeatedly in our Getting Started series: AI speeds up the workflow, but humans still evaluate, refine, edit, and decide what to keep. That is not a limitation of the tools. It is how they are designed to work.

## What this means for your career

There is an interesting flip side to AI error that does not get discussed enough. The professionals who develop good verification habits are building something valuable: judgment that compounds.

When you review AI output carefully enough to catch what is wrong, you are also building pattern recognition for what good looks like. You start to notice how the model handles uncertainty (often: by sounding certain). You learn where it tends to slip in your domain. You develop a feel for when a draft needs a close read versus when it is close enough to pass through quickly.

That judgment is yours. The model does not learn between conversations. But you do.

A recent [HR Dive report](https://www.hrdive.com/news/workplace-ai-not-reliable-human-oversight/812949/) noted that only 17% of U.S. adults consider workplace AI reliable without human oversight. The professionals who matter to their organizations right now are not the ones who generate the most AI output. They are the ones who generate AI output their organization can trust.

## Working on this with other people speeds it up

One of the most useful things you can do to develop the verification instinct is watch how someone else catches errors in AI output, and have someone else watch you. The moments where a colleague says "wait, that number doesn't seem right" build the instinct faster than anything you will do solo.
