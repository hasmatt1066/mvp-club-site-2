## What "Building Internal Tools" Means for Non-Technical Professionals

Building a tool used to require a developer, a backlog, a few sprints, and a launch meeting. That's still true for complex enterprise software. But for the kind of task-specific tools professionals need every day (a market report formatter, a document intake processor, a quick-look dashboard), the calculus has shifted.

You can now describe what you want in plain English and Claude will write the code, deploy it locally, and let you test it in the same session. The barrier is no longer technical ability. It's knowing what problem you're trying to solve.

That second part is where most professionals already have the edge. You know your workflows. You know where the friction is. You know what takes too long every week. That knowledge, combined with tools like Claude Code or Claude.ai with Projects, is what makes building feel accessible in a way it genuinely wasn't 18 months ago.

### Carmella Thompson: Portfolio Site, No Terminal Required

Carmella has been building in Claude Code and recently put together a GitHub portfolio page (a public page that shows your projects to employers or collaborators) entirely within the desktop app. The part she found most surprising: she never had to open a folder, navigate a file tree, or touch the terminal.

The same skill that built the portfolio is the one that will save a chunk of her morning, every morning.

### Robert Martin: Game Asset Automation

Robert builds games as a side project. One of the most repetitive tasks in game development is resizing sprite images to match different screen resolutions. Every character, every asset, every frame. It's the kind of job that takes an afternoon if you do it manually.

Robert automated it. Using Claude, he built a workflow that batches sprite resizing across the whole asset library. What used to take hours now runs while he works on something else.

His situation illustrates the simplest rule I give people in coaching: "Anything you do more than once, write down how to do it, and then tell Claude: here's how I want you to do it. Just go."

### Angela Frances Peat: Institutional Knowledge as a Tool

Angela, a consultant, has been working through how to describe her clients' businesses as sets of discrete, clearly defined capabilities that an AI can actually act on. Rather than just maintaining a task backlog, she's building documentation that lets AI agents understand entire workflows, not just individual tasks.

"Capability models are increasingly important," she noted in a session. "Not just skills or context windows, but can you give them a full picture of how your business operates?"

The tool she's building is less about automation and more about institutional knowledge becoming actionable. That's a different kind of tool, but it's still something that required weeks of consulting work before you could describe it to an AI and have it do anything useful.

## The Most Common Starting Point: Something You Do Every Week

The best first tool to build is the one you're most annoyed by. Not the biggest problem. Not the most impressive use case. The one that makes you think "I wish I didn't have to do this manually" on a regular Tuesday.

From community sessions, a few categories come up again and again.

**Report reformatting.** You get data in one format, you need it in another. This is almost always automatable. Give Claude a sample input file, describe the output format, and ask it to write a script that handles the conversion.

**Template population.** You have a document template with twenty fields that you fill from a spreadsheet, a database, or an email. Describe the sources, describe the destination, let Claude wire them together.

**Research compilation.** You regularly read from multiple sources and synthesize into a summary. Claude can be set up to pull from designated sources and format the output the way you want it.

None of these require you to know how to code. They require you to know what problem you're solving.

## Why "Vibe Coding" Is the Right Frame for This

The phrase gets used in developer circles, but it applies here just as well. You're not writing code. You're managing a collaboration. You describe what you want, Claude builds a version of it, you evaluate what you got, and you iterate.

"This is the really important step," I tell people in sessions. "You gave it the requirements, it built it, and now you're managing the output through evaluation."

That's a managerial skill, not a technical one. You're the one who knows whether the output is right. Claude can generate a thousand correct lines of code that still don't solve your actual problem if you didn't describe the problem clearly. Your judgment is the critical layer.

This is also why community practice matters here. The people who get good at building tools fastest are the ones who build them alongside others, get feedback on what they made, and watch what other people are making in return.

## What Happens When You Build Your First Tool

There's a specific moment that happens to almost everyone who builds their first working tool. The novelty of "I made this" gives way to something more useful: an intuition for where else this applies.

Once you've built a report formatter, you start noticing every other place in your work where a similar conversion happens. Once you've automated an intake form, you see the three other intake processes on your team that have the same shape.

"Getting the app actually impacting the real world is the turning point that makes vibe coding feel meaningful beyond hobby projects," said T Gaines in one of our community sessions.

That shift is real, and it's contagious. The professionals who are building within MVP Club tend to come back each week with a thing they finished and a new thing they want to try.

## Starting Without Any Technical Background

A few clarifying points for people who want to start but aren't sure how.

You don't need VS Code or a terminal to start. Claude.ai with Projects lets you build workflows in a browser window without touching a command line. Angela Peat put it plainly: "The desktop is probably what I prefer, versus the terminal looks a little bit scary."

Claude Code in the desktop app is more capable, especially for longer sessions, but starting in the browser is a legitimate first step. Build something small there, see what's possible, and move to a more powerful setup when you're ready.

Plan mode is worth knowing about. When you're worried about Claude making changes you didn't intend, you can tell it to discuss with you rather than execute. I describe it this way in sessions: "It won't touch the code. It's only going to chat with you, so basically just turns it back into chat." That's a useful way to think through the design before anything gets built.

Start with something you know. The professionals who struggle are usually trying to build something impressive. The ones who succeed first build something boring that solves a real problem they have.

## What to Expect (and What Not to Expect)

The tool you build on your first session won't be polished. It will probably have rough edges, edge cases it doesn't handle, and at least one thing that doesn't quite work the way you pictured.

That's fine. That's the version you show your team member or use yourself for two weeks before you figure out what it actually needs to do. The iteration loop is where the real learning happens, and Claude is in the loop with you.
