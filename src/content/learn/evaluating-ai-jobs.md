A job posting just landed in your inbox. It says "AI Strategist," pays $145,000, and the responsibilities section is four bullet points long. One of them says "leverage AI tools to drive transformation." If you applied for that job and got it, you would spend your first three months explaining to your hiring manager what the job is.

That's the current state of the AI jobs market. Companies know they need to do something with AI. They're not always sure what. So they create roles with impressive titles, post them publicly to signal seriousness, and then figure out the actual scope once someone shows up.

Some of these roles are real. Some are placeholders. A few are genuinely career-defining opportunities. The skill worth developing right now is telling them apart.

## Why AI Job Titles Are Such a Mess

Every job title in tech goes through a naming cycle. There's a period of invention, where everyone calls the same role something different. Then a period of inflation, where the title gets applied to jobs that don't really fit. Then eventually some consolidation, where the market settles on standard definitions.

AI roles are firmly in the inflation phase. "Head of AI" grew more than 13x in job postings between 2023 and 2025, according to LinkedIn data [NEEDS CITATION: name LinkedIn report]. "Chief AI Officer" now appears at companies with six employees and companies with sixty thousand. The title tells you almost nothing about what the job actually is.

What this means for you: ignore the title. Read the responsibilities. Read the requirements. Look at what team this role sits in and who it reports to. That's where the real signal is.

## The Four Types of AI Roles You'll Actually Encounter

Not all AI job postings are the same. Most fall into one of four buckets, and once you can identify the bucket, you know whether the role is worth your time.

**The technical builder.** Data scientists, ML engineers, AI engineers, model trainers. These roles require Python, an understanding of model architecture, experience with APIs and deployment infrastructure. If you don't have a software background and the job description mentions TensorFlow, PyTorch, or LLM fine-tuning in the requirements section, this is not the role for you right now. That's not a limitation, it's just a different lane.

**The AI practitioner.** This is the growing middle tier: professionals who apply AI tools fluently to real business problems. Product managers who spec AI features. Marketers who build prompt libraries and automation workflows. Operations folks who build internal tools with Claude or ChatGPT. The requirements for these roles are changing fast. Six months ago they asked for "familiarity with AI tools." Now they're asking for demonstrated AI workflows and portfolio evidence.

**The AI strategist/leader.** Roles like "AI Program Manager," "Head of AI Adoption," "AI Champions Lead." These are often organizational change roles. The actual work is closer to internal consulting: identifying use cases, running pilots, coaching colleagues, measuring results. These roles go to people who have done the thing, not just read about it.

**The title laundering job.** This is the most common one. A role that existed before as a project coordinator, analyst, or specialist, now rebadged with "AI" in the title because the company wants to look current. The responsibilities are identical to the old version. Sometimes the compensation is better. Sometimes it's the same job with a new name.

The way to spot this last category: the job description mentions AI in the title and maybe one bullet point, but the rest of the requirements read like 2019. If "leverage AI tools to improve productivity" is the only AI ask, that's an add-on, not a transformation.

## How to Read a Job Description for AI Maturity

When you're looking at a role, here are four questions worth asking before you get excited or dismissive.

**What does AI actually do in this role?** Look for verbs. "Research AI tools" is different from "build AI-powered workflows." "Support AI initiatives" is different from "own the AI roadmap." Vague verbs mean vague scope. Specific verbs mean someone has actually thought about what this person will do on Monday morning.

**Where does this role sit in the org?** A "Head of AI" who reports to the CTO in a 200-person company has real authority. A "Head of AI" who reports to the VP of IT in a 5,000-person company is probably managing a software procurement budget. Same title, very different reality. Find this out in the first screening call if you can't tell from the posting.

**What does success look like at 90 days?** Any serious AI role should have a concrete answer to this. If the hiring manager says "we're still figuring that out," that's either an exciting greenfield situation or a sign that the role was created reactively and nobody owns it. Either interpretation is useful information before you accept an offer.

**What's the company's existing AI maturity?** A company posting an "AI Adoption Lead" role that has no internal AI tools yet is hiring their first practitioner. That's a very different job than the same title at a company already running AI pilots across three departments. Both can be interesting. Neither should be mistaken for the other.

## The Practitioner Gap Is the Real Opportunity

The technical AI jobs (engineers, data scientists) are growing fast but they're not the opening for most mid-career professionals. The real opportunity is in the practitioner tier.

According to LinkedIn's 2026 Economic Graph data, job postings calling for hands-on AI fluency grew more than 70% year over year [NEEDS CITATION: verify and ensure consistent with other articles]. But the demand isn't mostly for people who can train models. It's for people who can use AI tools as part of a real business workflow, and who can show that they've done it.

This is the gap most people are sleeping on. Companies have tools. They don't have enough people who know how to put them to use in a way that actually saves time, improves output, or reduces cost. A practitioner who can walk into a marketing team, identify which tasks are wasting hours, build three Claude workflows, and train the team to use them is more valuable than another person who completed an online AI certification.

The credential they're actually hiring for is evidence. Working things. Documented processes. Demonstrated results.

## Evaluating Compensation in AI Roles

The salary spread in AI jobs is wide enough that two roles with identical titles can pay $60,000 apart. A few things worth knowing when you're sizing up an offer.

Technical roles (engineers, ML practitioners) are compensating aggressively right now. The market is genuinely short on people who can build and deploy AI systems. Six-figure base plus equity at mid-sized tech companies is normal for experienced roles.

Strategy and practitioner roles are more variable. A first AI-adjacent role at a mid-market company might be $90,000. The same type of role at a well-funded startup or enterprise company might be $130,000. Your negotiating position comes from being able to demonstrate existing AI competency, not just willingness to learn.

Beware of the title bump without a comp bump. If a company is rebranding your current role as "AI [whatever]" but not adjusting compensation, the new title is marketing for them, not advancement for you. That's worth naming in the conversation.

## What "AI Skills" Actually Means to a Hiring Manager

I talk to a lot of people who are trying to get into AI work. The most common mistake is preparing to talk about AI in the abstract when the hiring manager wants to see AI applied to something concrete.

They don't want to hear that you've been following AI developments closely. They want to see a workflow you built. A process you automated. A document you created where AI handled the heavy lifting. The "I used Claude to do X and it saved me Y hours per week" story is twenty times more convincing than "I've been studying prompt engineering."

I have a $100/month Claude Pro subscription that I've had since early access. I use it every day. When someone asks me about AI in a professional context, I don't describe the theory. I pull up something I actually built and walk through what happened. That's the only kind of answer that lands.

The specificity is the credibility. "I built a reporting workflow that takes raw meeting transcripts and produces a formatted action item summary in about 90 seconds" is more believable than "I'm deeply invested in exploring the possibilities of generative AI." One of those is a person who did something. The other is a person who read about it.

## The Red Flags to Watch For

Some AI job postings are genuinely bad opportunities regardless of how interesting the title sounds.

Watch for roles where AI is the entire job description but the company has no existing AI infrastructure. "We're building our AI practice from the ground up" is only appealing if you have the experience and mandate to actually do that. Walking into a company as the sole AI person with no budget, no tools, and no executive buy-in is a setup for a frustrating year.

Watch for roles that promise AI transformation without specifying what gets transformed. "Lead AI innovation across the enterprise" is not a job description. It's a press release. The lack of specificity usually means the company is not ready to actually do the thing.

Watch for the "AI washing" pattern: roles at companies that mentioned layoffs in the same breath as AI adoption. A company in that posture is not a great place to build a career in AI right now.

## How to Position Yourself for Real AI Roles

Three things that actually move the needle.

**Start building a portfolio of AI work.** Not hypothetical projects. Actual workflows, automations, or tools you've used in your real job. Even small things count. A Claude workflow that saves you 20 minutes a day, documented and shareable, is evidence. See [Building a Portfolio of AI Projects for Career Advancement](/learn/ai-project-portfolio) for a framework on what to include.

**Put specific AI applications on your resume and LinkedIn, not general claims.** "Proficient in AI tools" means nothing. "Built and deployed a client intake workflow using Claude that reduced processing time from 2 hours to 20 minutes" means something. Be specific. Use numbers where you have them. The [AI skills that actually matter for career advancement](/learn/ai-skills-for-career) covers the specifics on how to translate your daily AI work into language that lands on a resume.

**Practice explaining what you do with AI.** In two minutes, out loud, to someone who doesn't know you. Not the theory. The actual thing. What tool you used, what problem it solved, what the result was. This is the answer to "what are you doing with AI?" that most people are not prepared to give clearly, and the ones who can give it clearly get callbacks.

## The Bigger Picture on AI and Your Career

The professionals who are doing well in this market are not the ones who have the most AI knowledge. They're the ones who have the most AI practice. There's a real gap between the two. Knowledge sits on a shelf. Practice produces artifacts, and artifacts are what employers are actually asking to see.
