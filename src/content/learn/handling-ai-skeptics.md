Your most skeptical team member isn't broken. They're probably the one who asks the hardest questions and has the clearest sense of what could go wrong. That's not a problem to fix. That's a resource to use.

The instinct when someone pushes back on AI is to explain it better. More context, more examples, a demo, a workshop. If they just understood what these tools can actually do, they'd get it. Right?

That's almost never how it works.

## Skepticism usually isn't about the technology

In a recent survey of 1,400 U.S.-based employees, [Harvard Business Review found](https://hbr.org/2025/11/leaders-assume-employees-are-excited-about-ai-theyre-wrong) that 76% of executives believed their employees were enthusiastic about AI adoption, but only 31% of individual contributors actually felt that way. That's not a knowledge gap. That's a trust gap, a safety gap, an identity gap.

The people I've coached through this at General Assembly, and now through MVP Club, consistently show the same pattern. The technical piece clicks relatively fast (sometimes embarrassingly fast, once people actually sit down with it). What takes longer is the emotional recalibration: Who am I if this tool can do a significant chunk of what I built my reputation on?

That question doesn't get answered by a better explanation of how Claude works.

[NEEDS CITATION: McKinsey 2025 survey on culture vs technology as AI barrier] Your skeptical team member is not an outlier. They're the norm. And the leaders who treat this as a psychological challenge, not a technical rollout, are the ones who actually move the needle.

## The three types of skeptics (and what they actually need)

Not all skepticism is the same. Knowing which type you're dealing with changes your approach.

### The Identity-Threatened Skeptic

This person has spent years building expertise. They're genuinely excellent at something. And they've heard enough about AI to believe it might make that excellence less valuable.

What they're thinking: "If AI can write the first draft, what's the point of the twenty years I spent learning to write the first draft?"

What they need: Proof that their judgment, context, and relationships are the actual product. The draft is cheap. Knowing what should be in it isn't. You're not losing ground here. Your expertise is what tells the AI what to produce and whether the output is any good.

### The Quality-Worried Skeptic

This person has seen AI get things wrong, maybe embarrassingly wrong. They're not being dramatic. AI tools do make mistakes. Their concern is legitimate.

What they're thinking: "I'm not going to stake my reputation on something I can't verify."

What they need: A workflow that keeps them in control. Show them how to use Claude or ChatGPT as a first-draft tool, not a final-answer machine. The person who reviews, edits, and approves AI output isn't abdicating responsibility. They're doing exactly what a good editor does.

### The "Not My Job" Skeptic

This person isn't opposed to AI. They're stretched thin and genuinely can't see where to fit one more thing into their week. Their resistance isn't philosophical, it's logistical. They're not saying no. They're saying "not right now, and maybe not ever if you keep making this sound like a project."

What they need is not a framework. It's one specific thing they can try in the next 30 minutes, with their actual work, today. Then you let them come to the conclusion themselves.

## What doesn't work (and why leaders keep doing it anyway)

The instinct to educate is strong. You've seen what these tools can do. You're genuinely excited. You want to share that. So you:

- Schedule a team workshop
- Forward articles and demos
- Suggest a shared AI challenge or experiment week
- Mandate adoption as part of a team initiative

These aren't wrong, exactly. But they tend to fail for the same reason: they treat AI adoption as an information problem, and it's not. Workshops and demos inform. They don't build the psychological safety that skeptics need to actually try something in public, imperfectly, and be okay with that.

The [4 Well-Intentioned AI Approaches That Miss the Mark](/blog/4-well-intentioned-ai-approaches-that-miss-the-mark) are worth reading here. Jill laid out in detail why the most sensible-seeming approaches (the all-hands, the tool evaluation committee, the company-wide mandate) so often fall flat. The short version: this is a behavior change problem. Behavior change requires practice and safety, not information.

## The approach that actually works: coaching, not convincing

The most effective thing you can do with a skeptic is ask more and tell less.

This is the coaching instinct. Instead of "Here's why you should try this," try "What would make this worth trying for you?" That question does several things at once. It treats them as someone with a legitimate perspective (because they are, even when they're wrong about the specifics). It surfaces the actual objection, which is often different from the stated one. And it gives you something real to work with.

A few questions worth keeping in your back pocket:

**"What's a task you regularly do that you find tedious?"**

This surfaces the low-stakes, high-repetition work that's ideal for AI-assist. Nobody's reputation lives or dies on the meeting recap or the agenda email. That's the right place to start.

**"What would you need to see to think this was worth your time?"**

This question gets specific. "I'd need to see it actually handle our terminology" is a real answer you can test. "I'd need to see it not be wrong" is too vague, but it opens a conversation about the edit-and-verify workflow.

**"What are you most worried about?"**

Say this with genuine curiosity, not as a setup to counter the answer. Skeptics often haven't been asked this directly. When someone feels heard, they're more willing to move.

## Show, don't tell (specifically)

If you're going to demonstrate AI, the demo matters a lot.

Generic demos backfire. "Let me show you what ChatGPT can do with a writing prompt" is not compelling to someone who's skeptical. It's abstract. It doesn't connect to anything they care about.

The demo that works uses their actual work. Ask if they'd be willing to pick something they're working on right now, open claude.ai, paste it in, and see what happens. You're not saying the output will be perfect. You're saying let's see what happens in five minutes.

That specificity changes the energy. When a skeptic sees Claude produce a reasonable draft of their quarterly review template, or a summary of a meeting they just sat through, the conversation changes. The tool is no longer theoretical.

## The identity threat is real, and it deserves a real response

[NEEDS REAL EXAMPLE: A specific coaching conversation where someone voiced an identity threat around AI replacing their expertise, and what shifted for them]

When someone says "AI is just going to take our jobs," the instinct is to reassure quickly. "Oh no, it's augmentation, not replacement. Your job is safe." That response is usually too fast and too easy. It can feel dismissive of a concern that's actually worth taking seriously.

Here's a more honest version: No one can predict exactly how these tools will reshape roles over the next five years (and anyone who tells you they do is selling something). What we do know is that professionals who are building experience with AI now tend to feel more secure, not less, because they're ahead of the shift rather than behind it. You don't have to pretend your field isn't shifting. You can acknowledge that it is, and also point out where the person's skills become more valuable, not less, as AI handles more of the commodity work.

The specialists, the relationship-holders, the people with deep organizational context: those are hard to replace. AI is better at the generic than the specific. Someone who knows your client history, your team's dynamics, your organization's actual priorities. That person's judgment is what makes the AI output worth anything.

This framing tends to land better than reassurance. It's honest. It's specific. And it actually addresses the question rather than talking past it.

## Practical things to try this week

If you're leading a team with skeptics, here are a few concrete starting points.

**Make it optional and visible.** Invite people to share what they're trying at the start of a team meeting. Even two minutes of "I used Claude for X this week, here's what happened" makes AI feel less like a mandate and more like a shared experiment. Once someone who was skeptical shares a small win, it tends to shift the group dynamic fast.

**Start with the people who are willing.** Spend your early energy on the team members who are curious, even cautiously so. Let them build the skill. Let them become the informal resource. You don't need everyone to adopt at once. You need a few people to go first visibly.

**Give skeptics a specific, low-stakes experiment.** Not "try AI this week." More like: "We have that vendor summary to draft on Thursday. Would you be willing to try pasting your notes into Claude first and see what it produces? We'll still edit it either way." Concrete, time-bound, low-stakes.

**Check what tools they already have.** A lot of the teams I've worked with don't realize they already have AI built into the tools they use every day. Microsoft 365 Copilot, Google Workspace, Notion AI. [AI tools your company already has](/learn/ai-tools-you-already-have) is worth sharing with your team before suggesting they sign up for anything new.

## Skeptics make adoption stronger

Here's something worth holding onto: the teams that adopt AI most thoughtfully usually have at least one or two serious skeptics in the room.

Skeptics ask the quality questions. They want to know what happens when the output is wrong. They want to know who's accountable. They want to know if the tool is actually saving time or creating new work. Those are not obstacles to good AI use. Those are the conditions for it.

The goal is to channel skepticism, not eliminate it. A skeptic who becomes a thoughtful practitioner is worth more to a team than an enthusiast who never thinks critically about what they're producing.

The [AI adoption research from Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2026/02/why-ai-adoption-stalls-according-to-industry-data) [NEEDS VERIFIED LINK] makes this point clearly: adoption stalls when organizations push for speed and consensus over depth. The teams that build sustainable capability are the ones that make room for legitimate questions.

If you're trying to figure out how to [explain AI to your boss, team, or skeptical colleague](/learn/explain-ai-to-your-team), the underlying principle is the same: meet people where they are, lead with curiosity, and let the tool's usefulness speak for itself.
