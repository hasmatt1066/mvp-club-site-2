### WARNINGS (should fix)

**Specificity: Real community example is undersourced in the proprietary context section**
- Location: "Carmella Thompson, a real estate agent in our community, built a version of this for her market reports..." (line 92)
- Problem: The article cites Carmella Thompson by name and describes a specific workflow (weekly market data → structured narrative, 1 hour → 15 minutes). This is positioned as a real example from community sessions, but there's no way for readers to verify it. If this example came from a community session, it should be cited more clearly. If it's from a transcript, attribution is good, but the specificity creates a trust vulnerability: readers may wonder if this is a real person or a composite.
- Fix: Add attribution: "(from our community sessions)" or "(from an MVP Club session)" to make the sourcing clear. Or add a brief quote from Carmella herself: "Carmella told us: 'I automated the process...'" This adds authority and voice.

**Tone: Brian Lightfoot quote lacks context and feels like a mid-sentence insertion**
- Location: "Brian Lightfoot, an MVP Club member who coaches executives, noticed this pattern in his own work with Claude: 'When I use these tools, some things just sound a little bit more like AI. So I'm conscious: does this sound like AI?'" (line 69)
- Problem: The quote is good and relevant, but the setup is generic ("noticed this pattern"). The sentence feels like it was inserted without full integration into the paragraph flow. The paragraph before discusses AI drafts sounding flat. This quote validates that, but the transition is abrupt.
- Fix: Strengthen the transition: "The need for this editing work shows up clearly in how people describe using these tools. Brian Lightfoot, an MVP Club member who coaches executives, puts it this way: 'When I use these tools, some things just sound a little bit more like AI. So I'm conscious: does this sound like AI?'" This connects Brian's observation to the paragraph's point more explicitly.

**Structural: Missing community voice from a founder (Matt's quote comes very late)**
- Location: Matt Hastings appears only in section "From the practice: what we see in coaching" (line 132)
- Problem: The article is unattributed brand content, but it includes a founder voice quote from Matt. This creates an odd dynamic: the article reads as brand content for the first 80% of the piece, then suddenly a founder appears to summarize patterns. It's not a HARD FAIL because the quote is valuable and well-placed, but it breaks the voice consistency. The article should either be consistently in a founder's voice from the start, or the founder quote should be better integrated earlier (e.g., in the mental model section).
- Fix: Either (a) reattribute the article to "Matt Hastings" and rewrite it in his voice throughout, or (b) move the Matt quote earlier to the mental model section (around line 26) and introduce it more naturally as "As Matt Hastings, one of our founders, puts it..." This makes the founder voice intentional, not accidental.

**Specificity: "which you already have" is vague in the Why section**
- Location: "The research is done. You know what you want to say. But assembling it into something readable, structured, and persuasive takes time you often don't have." (line 14)
- Problem: "You know what you want to say" is abstract. The brand guide demands specificity. This would be stronger with a concrete example: "You were in the meeting. You have the email chain. You know the situation. But assembling it into a readable proposal takes time you don't have."
- Fix: Make it concrete with a specific scenario.

**Audience: "white-collar work" in the intro may be too narrow**
- Location: "This guide covers the practical workflow for using AI on the professional documents that show up most often in white-collar work." (line 16)
- Problem: Not a big issue, but the brand guide emphasizes accessibility. "White-collar" is accurate but sounds formal and exclusive. The article itself mentions real estate (Carmella), which isn't strictly white-collar. The tone should be more inclusive.
- Fix: Change to: "This guide covers the practical workflow for using AI on professional documents that eat hours every week across most roles."

**Incomplete Citation: HBR study referenced but date may confuse readers**
- Location: "A December 2024 Harvard Business Review study..." (line 24)
- Problem: The article is dated 2026-04-02, so "December 2024" is over a year old. That's fine, but the phrasing "A December 2024 study" without context about why it's still relevant creates a subtle credibility issue. Why not a more recent study? A reader might assume you couldn't find newer data.
- Fix: Change the phrasing slightly: "A Harvard Business Review study (December 2024) found that professionals using AI writing tools completed writing tasks more than 50% faster..." This acknowledges the date without making it feel stale. Or add context: "Recent research from Harvard Business Review (December 2024) found..."

**Keyword Density: "Claude" appears 5 times, "AI writing" repeats frequently**
- Location: Scattered throughout (Claude in lines 28, 38, 69, 118, 119, 122, 132; "AI writing" in lines 17, 24, 117, 120)
- Problem: Not egregious keyword stuffing, but "Claude" appears more often than "ChatGPT" (which appears 3 times). Given that ChatGPT is equally valid, the imbalance might be perceived as bias or over-promotion. Also, the phrase "AI writing tools" repeats in step 1 and the tools section. The brand guide flags mechanical keyword repetition.
- Fix: Vary the language. Use "these tools," "AI assistants," or just "it" more often to break up the repetition. Add one more mention of ChatGPT or expand to "AI writing assistants like Claude and ChatGPT."

**CTA Positioning: Call-to-action is buried in "Where to go from here" rather than prominent**
- Location: The actual MVP Club CTA is in the final paragraph: "If you want to practice this alongside other professionals who are working through the same questions, that's what happens in MVP Club's weekly sessions. We'd be glad to have you. Come find us at [/community](/community)." (lines 141-142)
- Problem: This is a good invitation CTA, but it's framed as an afterthought ("Where to go from here") rather than a natural next step. It comes after external links (MIT Sloan) and feels like it's competing with those resources rather than being the primary next step. The brand wants the community CTA to feel inevitable, not optional.
- Fix: Reorder the final section. Put the community invitation BEFORE the external MIT Sloan link: "For your next step: pick one document you have to write this week... Once you've done it once... If you want to deepen this work alongside other professionals figuring this out, that's what MVP Club's weekly sessions are for. Come find us at [/community](/community). For further reading, the MIT Sloan Management Review has..."

**Tone: "Anything you do more than once, write down how to do it..." lacks setup**
- Location: Line 132
- Problem: Matt's quote is strong and would be excellent advice if it were clearer *why* this matters. The sentence before ("professional who get the best results treat it as scaffolding...") doesn't quite set up the prompt-as-template idea. Matt's quote feels dropped in rather than earned.
- Fix: Add one sentence before Matt's quote: "The biggest leverage comes from building a repeatable prompt. Matt Hastings puts it this way: 'Anything you do more than once...'"
