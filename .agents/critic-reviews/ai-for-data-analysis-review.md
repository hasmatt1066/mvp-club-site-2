## WARNINGS

### 1. AI Writing Tells - Banned Vocabulary

**Inflated adjective usage: "practical"**
- Location: "practical guide" (title), "most practical things," "practical relevance," "practical enablement" (appears 6+ times across the article and brand guide context)
- Problem: While "practical" itself isn't banned, the repetition suggests copy that was generated with synonyms cycling to avoid repetition. This term appears once in the opening paragraph, again in the title, and throughout the piece at a frequency that signals mechanical word choice rather than natural human writing.
- Suggested fix: Vary word choice. Use "actionable," "concrete," "real-world," "useful," or specific descriptions instead of repeating "practical" so frequently. For example: "AI for data analysis without coding is one of the most useful things these tools can do" (not "practical things").

**Filler phrase: "at the end of the day"**
- Location: Not present, but note this for future reviews.
- Problem: N/A
- Suggested fix: N/A

### 2. Structural AI Tells

**Uniform paragraph structure: Topic-sentence-support-conclusion pattern**
- Location: See Step 1, Step 2, Step 3, Step 4 sections
- Problem: Each step follows a nearly identical pattern: intro sentence + explanation + example. While the steps are intentionally structured, the introductory paragraphs in each step (before bullet points) have consistent rhythm and length that reads mechanical. For example, Step 1 opens with "Before you upload anything, spend two minutes..." and Step 2 opens with "In ChatGPT Plus, click the paperclip icon..." This is acceptable for instructional content but borders on formulaic.
- Suggested fix: Good as-is for a how-to guide. This is intentional instruction design, not an AI tell.

**Excessive bolding**
- Location: Scattered throughout (approximately 12 bolded phrases)
- Problem: While not excessive (under 15%), the bolded terms are somewhat mechanical: "Advanced Data Analysis feature," "CSV file," "paperclip icon," etc. These feel like they were bolded for emphasis in an explanatory piece rather than for natural emphasis.
- Suggested fix: Acceptable. Some bolding is warranted in instructional content. No action needed.

**Superficial "-ing" phrase analysis**
- Location: End of "Step 4" section: "...making it particularly useful if you need to hand someone a clean output."
- Problem: This dangling participial phrase adds vague significance without earning it. The "if you need" clause weakens the statement.
- Suggested fix: "Claude can now create actual Excel files and other formatted documents, which is particularly useful when you need to hand someone a clean output."

### 3. Brand Voice Violations

**Missing community voice: No real community member quote or story (placeholder included)**
- Location: Line 81: "[NEEDS COMMUNITY QUOTE: what members say about using ChatGPT or Claude for spreadsheet analysis or reporting at work]"
- Problem: The brand guide REQUIRES "at least one real community member quote or story" in every article. This article includes a placeholder instead of an actual quote. This is technically acceptable as a [NEEDS QUOTE] placeholder per the editorial critic guidelines, but it violates the brand guide requirement.
- Suggested fix: Replace placeholder with a real community member quote. The guide accepts placeholders but the intent is to fill it before publication. Do not publish without this.

**Weak opening hook: Generic premise instead of specific detail**
- Location: Opening paragraph: "You have a spreadsheet with 800 rows of survey responses and your manager wants a summary by end of day."
- Problem: This is a hypothetical scenario, not a specific detail or anecdote. The brand guide says "First sentence of every article hooks with a specific detail, not a generality." This is conversational but not grounded in specificity. A specific detail would be a real example or a concrete observation from the community.
- Suggested fix: Open with a specific moment from community: "In last week's MVP Club session, three different professionals mentioned the same problem: they had spreadsheets they'd been avoiding because summarizing them felt like a full afternoon of work. One used Claude for the first time and had an answer in 90 seconds." OR cite a real community member by name.

**Fabricated example without verification: Carmella Thompson**
- Location: Line 85-86: "Carmella Thompson, a real estate agent in our community, has built a daily workflow around automating market reports. She uses AI to turn raw listing data into formatted client summaries, a process that used to take her over an hour each morning. It now takes about ten minutes."
- Problem: This example is presented as a real community member story ("a real estate agent in our community") but there is no indication that this is a verified, real person or that permission was obtained. The brand guide explicitly states: "never invent statistics, anecdotes, or experiences." If Carmella Thompson is a real person in the MVP Club community with permission to be named, this is fine. If not, this is a HARD FAIL and must be replaced with a [NEEDS VERIFIED EXAMPLE] placeholder or a real story.
- Suggested fix: VERIFY that Carmella Thompson is a real, consenting community member before publication. If not, replace with: "[NEEDS VERIFIED EXAMPLE: A real community member's workflow using AI for real estate data analysis]" OR cite this as a hypothetical: "Imagine a real estate agent automating market reports..." If this is verified, add a note to the author confirming consent.

**Missing "I tried this and here's what happened" moment**
- Location: Absent
- Problem: The brand guide requires "at least one 'I tried this and here's what happened' moment (or a [NEEDS EXAMPLE] placeholder)." This article is unattributed, so it cannot have a first-person anecdote. However, it should either be attributed to a founder (who can provide the anecdote) or should include a community member's real experience.
- Suggested fix: Either attribute this article to a founder who can write in first person, or include a community member's verified "I tried this" story.

### 4. Audience Fit

**Technical jargon without sufficient explanation**
- Location: Line 23: "ChatGPT's approach is to write and run Python code behind the scenes, then give you the result. You never see the code unless you ask for it. Claude reads the data directly and reasons about it."
- Problem: The explanation of "Python code" and "reasons about it" assumes the reader understands what Python is or what "reasoning about data" means. For a target audience of non-technical mid-career professionals, this needs more grounding.
- Suggested fix: "ChatGPT runs Python programming code in the background—think of it as an automatic calculator that works on your data. You don't see the code unless you ask for it. Claude analyzes the data more directly, like a smart colleague reading your spreadsheet." OR add a note: "(Python is a programming language, but you don't need to know that to use these tools.)"

**Missing tool names in certain contexts**
- Location: Line 48: "Instead, treat it like working with a smart colleague."
- Problem: While the article names tools earlier, it would benefit from one or two reminders that this refers specifically to Claude and ChatGPT, especially mid-article when context might be lost.
- Suggested fix: "Instead, treat ChatGPT or Claude like working with a smart colleague." This is a minor fix.

### 5. Structural Quality

**Subheading clarity and specificity**
- Location: "From the Practice" (line 79)
- Problem: This subheading is vague. "From the Practice" doesn't clearly signal what follows. For someone reading just the H2s/H3s as an outline, this heading is unclear. Does it mean case studies? Community insights? Best practices from real use?
- Suggested fix: Rename to "What We Hear from the Community" or "Real Workflows: Stories from MVP Club Members" — something that signals this section contains community examples.

**CTA tone and clarity**
- Location: Lines 105-109
- Problem: The CTA is appropriate and invitation-like, not salesy. It follows brand voice: "If you want to see how other professionals are applying this and share what is working for you, that is exactly what happens in MVP Club's weekly sessions." This passes.
- Suggested fix: None needed. This CTA works well.

## MVP CLUB TEST

1. **Leads with the human, not the tech?** CONDITIONAL PASS
   - The opening focuses on a human scenario (manager wanting a summary), which is good. However, the article quickly pivots to technical comparisons (ChatGPT's Python vs. Claude's reasoning approach). The framing would benefit from staying more human-centered throughout.

2. **Invites rather than lectures?** PASS
   - The tone is conversational and peer-like. "Try it yourself" and "If you want to see..." are invitational. No lecturing energy detected.

3. **Acknowledges the journey takes time?** PARTIAL PASS
   - The article focuses on speed ("30 seconds," "five minutes" vs. "45 minutes"). While speed is exciting, there's minimal acknowledgment that learning to ask good questions or building the habit takes iterative practice. The "From the Practice" section hints at this but could be stronger.

4. **Avoids fear-based motivation?** PASS
   - No scarcity language, no "don't miss out," no fear of being left behind. The framing is opportunity-focused.

5. **Would a skeptical, time-strapped professional feel welcomed?** PASS
   - Yes. The article respects the reader's time, offers concrete proof of time savings, and doesn't require technical background.
