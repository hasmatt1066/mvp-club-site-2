## WARNINGS

### 1. AI Writing Tells — Structural Pattern

**Issue:** Rule of three abuse throughout.

**Location:**
- "Why AI output goes sideways" section lists exactly three reasons, then a fourth is added (but the intro says "one of four places"). 
- "The iteration loop" intro: "You wouldn't hand a new colleague a task with zero context and expect perfection on the first try. You'd explain, they'd draft, you'd give feedback, they'd revise." This is four parallel actions in sequence, which creates a slightly mechanical rhythm.

**Problem:** While not a hard fail, the reliance on groupings of three or four feels slightly templated. Human writing would vary more naturally. The phrase "four places" then "four steps" then "three common iteration mistakes" shows organizational pattern-thinking that LLMs favor.

**Suggested fix:** Vary the grouping. Could the "why output goes sideways" section have been two places instead of four? Or five? The artificial grouping draws slight attention to structure over content.

---

### 2. AI Writing Tells — Filler Phrase

**Issue:** "This is what Matt Hastings, one of MVP Club's founders, calls..."

**Location:** Line 59, quote attribution.

**Problem:** The phrase "This is what X calls" is not banned but appears in several places in the article and reads slightly mechanical (lines 59, and the pattern repeats). It's more "reportage" than conversational. More human: "Matt Hastings, one of MVP Club's founders, frames it as..." or just integrating the quote more naturally without the meta-introduction.

**Suggested fix:** Reduce the number of "X calls" or "X describes" setups. Let the quote stand closer to the hook.

---

### 3. Tone — Condescension Risk (Minor)

**Issue:** "You wouldn't hand a new colleague a task with zero context and expect perfection on the first try" (line 34).

**Location:** The "colleague analogy" section.

**Problem:** While well-intentioned, this reads slightly like talking to someone who might not grasp basic work dynamics. The target audience (mid-career professionals) already knows how to brief a colleague. This is over-explaining a premise they already live. Not a hard fail, but slightly patronizing.

**Suggested fix:** Remove the extended analogy or shorten it: "Think of this as a conversation, not a vending machine" is sufficient. The colleague detail is unnecessary scaffolding.

---

### 4. Brand Voice — "Wise Observer" Setup (Minor)

**Issue:** "That moment of frustration is where most people stop. And it's exactly where the skill begins." (lines 13-14).

**Location:** Opening section, before the "Why AI output goes sideways" subheading.

**Problem:** This is close to a "wise observer" setup. It's framing a universal experience ("that moment") then offering the insight ("where the skill begins"). The brand guide says to avoid "Something I keep coming back to:" but this is a lighter version of the same pattern. It works because it's not a wind-up, but it's borderline observation-posturing.

**Suggested fix:** Tighten to: "That moment of frustration is where learning begins" or cut it entirely and move directly to the diagnosis section. The reader gets the implication without the meta-commentary.

---

### 5. Audience Fit — Jargon Without Grounding (Minor)

**Issue:** "Vague goal, vague output" (line 22) and similar aphorisms throughout.

**Location:** The four-point diagnosis section (lines 22-28).

**Problem:** While accessible, the snappy maxims ("Vague goal, vague output") are presented without examples for each case initially. The examples come later in the Step sections, but a mid-career professional skimming section headers would benefit from one grounded example per diagnosis point. Currently, "The goal was unclear" is abstract; what does an actual unclear goal look like?

**Suggested fix:** Add one real-world example inline for each of the four diagnosis points. Not long—one sentence each would anchor the abstraction.

### 6. Structural Quality — Opening

**Issue:** First paragraph is strong, but the second paragraph ("Getting bad output from Claude...") reprises the same point.

**Location:** Lines 12-16.

**Problem:** Lines 12-14 hook with a specific moment. Lines 15-16 then restate the same insight (bad output = learning opportunity). This is a classic AI tell: restating the thesis to fill space. A human writer would cut one or the other.

**Suggested fix:** Delete lines 15-16 ("Getting bad output from Claude or ChatGPT isn't a failure signal. It's the first data point in an iterative process.") and jump directly to the third sentence: "The professionals who get real results from AI..." This eliminates the restatement.

### 7. Structural Quality — Subheading Specificity

**Issue:** "Why AI output goes sideways (and what you can actually fix)" is descriptive but slightly generic.

**Location:** Line 18 subheading.

**Problem:** "Goes sideways" is conversational but not specific. A reader skimming subheadings wouldn't know this section teaches diagnosis. Better: "Four reasons AI output misses (and how to fix each one)" — specific and actionable.

**Suggested fix:** Rename to: "Diagnose what went wrong: four reasons AI output misses the mark."

### 9. Required Elements — "I Tried This" Moment

**Issue:** The article includes one example ("Brian") but it's framed as a case study during a session, not as a personal teaching moment.

**Location:** Lines 85-86.

**Problem:** The brand guide requires "at least one 'I tried this and here's what happened' moment." The Brian example is good, but it's not authored voice—it's reported as "Brian, a consultant who went through a session." Where is the author's own iteration story? The article teaches iteration but doesn't model someone from the writing team doing it. This creates a "sage on the stage" feel rather than "peer expert."

**Suggested fix:** Add one author voice moment. Could be inserted in the "Three common iteration mistakes" section: "I used to regenerate 5 times hoping for different results. Then I realized I was changing nothing about the input. Once I started editing the prompt itself, everything shifted." This would ground the advice in lived experience.

### 10. Structural Quality — Paragraph Length

**Issue:** Several paragraphs exceed 4 sentences.

**Location:** 
- Line 35 (Step 1 explanation): 5 sentences
- Line 46-49 (Step 2 context block): 4 sentences (acceptable) but the quoted block is long
- Line 59-61 (quote and attribution): The block before and after the quote runs long

**Problem:** The brand guide specifies "Paragraphs max 4 sentences for web readability." While not all violations, some stretching suggests looser editing. Web readers skim; shorter paragraphs increase scanability.

"Before you send another message, spend 30 seconds being specific. 'The output was bad' doesn't help. 'The tone is too formal for a casual team update' helps.

The more precisely you can identify the gap, the more targeted your follow-up can be."

### 14. Structural Quality — Subheading as Outline Test

**Issue:** Subheading-only outline doesn't quite tell a coherent story.

**Location:** Reading just the H2s:
- Why AI output goes sideways
- The iteration loop: four steps that actually work
- When to start fresh vs. keep going
- Three common iteration mistakes
- The deeper shift: from one-shot to iterative
- A practical starting point for your next session
- Where people who iterate well end up

**Problem:** The outline is logical but the flow jumps around slightly. "The deeper shift" comes after "practical starting point," which is odd structurally—theory should come before application in instructional content. The current order is: diagnosis → steps → decisions → mistakes → theory → practice → outcome. A human writer might reorder.

**Suggested fix:** Reorder sections:
1. Why (diagnosis)
2. The deeper shift (theory/mental model)
3. The iteration loop (steps—now they follow the mindset)
4. Practical starting point
5. Common mistakes (anti-patterns)
6. When to start fresh (edge cases)
7. Outcome (where people end up)

### 15. Specificity — Tool Naming

**Issue:** The article names Claude and ChatGPT in the opening but uses generic "AI" afterward.

**Location:** 
- Line 12: "Claude spits something back"
- Line 16: "from Claude or ChatGPT"
- Line 22: "You asked for..."
- Line 34: "You wouldn't hand..."
- Line 46: "Tell Claude what..."
- Line 59: "your job shifts from requester to reviewer"

**Problem:** The brand guide says "Name specific tools (Claude, ChatGPT, Cline), never just 'AI'." After the opening, the article shifts to generic language. Lines 22-28 use "the AI" four times without naming Claude or ChatGPT. This dilutes specificity.

**Suggested fix:** In the "Why AI output goes sideways" section, specify: "You asked Claude for 'an email to my team,' but..." And in the Step sections: "Tell Claude that..." instead of just "Tell the AI..." The article should name the specific tool in most examples.

---

### 16. Voice Quality — Sentence Rhythm

**Issue:** Inconsistent sentence cadence in places.

**Location:** Lines 40-42: "The more precisely you can identify the gap, the more targeted your follow-up can be." This is a long, rhythmic sentence with parallel structure ("the more X... the more Y"). It's not wrong, but it's the kind of balanced symmetry that LLMs favor.

**Problem:** While not a hard fail, this kind of perfect parallelism, when it appears repeatedly, is an AI tell. Human writing is more chaotic.

**Suggested fix:** Vary more. "The more precisely you name the problem, the sharper your next attempt." (Different structure, less symmetrical.)

---

### 17. Content Accuracy — Citation Verification

**Issue:** Matt Hastings quote attribution (lines 61-62).

**Location:** Line 61: quote from "Matthew Hastings, during an MVP Club Getting Started session"

**Problem:** This is attributed but not sourced. Where is this quote from? Was it recorded? Is this paraphrased? For a piece about precision, sourcing matters. If it's real, the article should cite the date/session. If it's reconstructed memory, it should say so.

**Suggested fix:** If the quote is real and recent, add: "Matthew Hastings, during an MVP Club Getting Started session in March 2026." If it's paraphrased from a general theme, reword as: "As Matt Hastings describes it in our coaching practice:" and frame it as paraphrase, not direct quote. Precision in citation models the iteration skill.
