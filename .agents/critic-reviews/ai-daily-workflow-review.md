### 2. AI Writing Tells: Structural Issues

**Issue: Uniform paragraph structure approaching formulaic**
- Location: Throughout, but especially in "What 30 minutes actually looks like" (lines 32-48)
- Problem: Several paragraphs follow a strict topic-sentence-support structure. For example, the "Block 1" paragraph (lines 32-38) opens with the block name, explains what to do, then provides a sample prompt. Paragraphs "Block 2" and "Block 3" follow nearly identical scaffolding. This is not yet mechanical enough to flag as hard fail, but it's borderline.
- Suggested fix: Vary the structure. Some blocks could open with a benefit first, then mechanics. Or embed the sample prompt mid-paragraph rather than at the end. Add a one-sentence paragraph somewhere to break rhythm.

**Issue: Potential "rule of three" in lists**
- Location: Lines 66-74, the "Common mistakes" section has exactly four bullet points. This passes, but the three blocks in "What 30 minutes actually looks like" is exactly three. Not flagged as hard fail, but noticeable.
- Suggested fix: If unintentional, consider whether the three-block structure is the right choice or whether a two- or four-block structure would feel more organic.

### 3. Audience Fit: Specificity

**Issue: Missing specific example in "Block 2" section**
- Location: Lines 40-48, specifically the list of "Good candidates for this block"
- Problem: The article recommends "a follow-up email," "a document summary," "a rough outline," and "a few talking points." These are generic categories. The Carmella Thompson example at the start (market report) is specific and real. The rest of the article could benefit from one or two more named examples from actual community members.
- Suggested fix: Include a real community member example for at least one of the three blocks. For instance: "Like Marcus, a project manager in our community, who used Claude each morning to draft status updates for his team Slack—what used to take 20 minutes now takes five."

**Issue: "Research on habit formation" citation lacks depth**
- Location: Lines 23-24
- Problem: The article cites the European Journal of Social Psychology study on the "66 days" habit formation claim. This is real research, which is good. However, the finding is presented without context about sample size, the specific behavior studied, or confidence intervals. The number "66 days" is often misinterpreted as a universal truth (Lally et al. actually found it's a median with a wide range). The article's use is not fabricated, but it's slightly weak—it doesn't acknowledge nuance.
- Suggested fix: Either add parenthetical context ("the research averaged 66 days, though it ranged from 18 to 254 depending on behavior and person") or simplify to "Research on habit formation shows that behaviors become automatic through consistent repetition, averaging around two months."

### 4. Tone: Over-Explaining Basic Concepts

**Issue: Excessive explanation of habit stacking**
- Location: Lines 56-62, the "The one rule that makes this work" section
- Problem: The article explains habit stacking three times in three sentences: (1) "Build your AI practice on top of something you already do," (2) "This is called habit stacking," (3) "Morning coffee and email becomes morning coffee, Claude, then email." The target audience (mid-career professionals) likely knows about habit stacking or can infer it from context. The explanation is clear but repetitive.
- Suggested fix: Either lead with the principle and name it once, or drop the label entirely. Try: "The secret is attaching it to something you already do. Instead of carving out new time, you anchor AI to an existing routine. Your morning email check becomes email + Claude check. Your pre-meeting prep gets one new step."

### 6. CTA and Ending

**Issue: Ending feels slightly sales-y, not purely invitational**
- Location: Lines 102-103, the final CTA: "If you want to see how other professionals are building these routines and share what's actually working in your role, that's exactly what we do in MVP Club's weekly sessions. Come practice alongside people navigating the same question. [Join the community at /community](/community)."
- Problem: The phrase "that's exactly what we do" is slightly self-promotional. It's not a hard fail—it's not "Sign up now" or "Don't miss out"—but it edges toward pitch language rather than pure invitation. The brand guide says CTAs should feel like "Here's where people like you are figuring this out together" rather than highlighting what MVP Club does.
- Suggested fix: Shift the focus back to the reader and the community: "If you want to practice alongside people navigating the same question every week, that's where our community is doing it. [Join us](/community)." Or more stripped down: "Want to practice this alongside other professionals figuring it out? [Come with us](/community)."

### 7. Structural Quality: Opening

**Issue: Opening is strong, but first sentence doesn't start mid-thought**
- Location: Line 12-14, the opening: "Carmella Thompson, a real estate agent in our community, spent three months using Claude occasionally before anything changed. Then she built one repeatable workflow: every morning, she pastes the day's property data into Claude and gets a market report draft. That's it. Within two weeks, she had cut her report prep from 45 minutes to under 10."
- Problem: This is a good, specific, grounded opening with a real example. However, it's not quite "mid-thought"—it's more of a setup. The article is not violating the rule, but it could be tighter. The opening takes 4 sentences to deliver the hook. By the brand guide standard (hook within 3 sentences with a specific detail), this stretches slightly long.
- Suggested fix: Tighten to three sentences maximum: "Carmella Thompson, a real estate agent in our community, went from spending 45 minutes on property reports to under 10. All she changed was using Claude the same way, the same time, every day." Then follow with the broader insight in a new paragraph.

### 10. Potential Over-Simplification of Habit Research

**Issue: "66 days" claim needs nuance or removal**
- Location: Line 24
- Problem: While the article correctly cites the Lally et al. research, the takeaway "averaging 66 days" is one of the most misunderstood statistics in productivity writing. The actual research showed a mean of 66 days for a single habit to "automaticity," but with a range from 18-254 days. By presenting only the mean, the article implies a universal timeline, which contradicts the next sentence about individual variation. This is not fabrication—it's just imprecise.
- Suggested fix: Rewrite to: "Research on habit formation shows that consistent repetition builds automaticity, though the timeline varies widely by person and behavior. The key word is consistent, not intensive."
